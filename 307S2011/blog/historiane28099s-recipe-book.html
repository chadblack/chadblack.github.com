Historian’s Recipe Book

Posted: Tue, 08 Mar 2011

We have moved from the realm of historiography into that of historical methods, and while it would have been nice to receive a history cookbook from Dr. Black, I am glad we have moved into a more concrete realm of developing our skills. We ran with the metaphor of becoming a chef and this really helped my understanding the process of becoming a professional historian. From there we examined three key points regarding methodology. The first was that context matters above all else. When examining a historical document, we must consider several factors including time period, intended audience, position of the author, and structures present in which the source was produced. All of these have effects upon the evidence, whether they were intended or not. The second point is that language is not transparent. Through time, words change meaning and documents will thusly be read differently. As long as the historian recognizes these first two points (s)he is on the way to an objective piece. For me, this related back to the refutation of the “prison house” of language in the previous book. The authors dismissed the disjunction of reality and truth by clearly stating that these two aforementioned points  are present in most true historians’ methods. The third point seemed obvious but finding appropriate sources is the start of any historian’s work and must be done with diligence and a discerning eye.

After the discussion of methods we examined the history of ethnohistory and how its definition has evolved. This was interesting because initially  it concerned “primitive” peoples, mainly those not being in the white majority of these historians. The definition then transformed into a very specific statement and ended up being broader than the first one. As we were running out of time we brought up the piece that researched orphanages in Sevilla and applied its findings to children in colonial Latin America and the early modern Iberian Peninsula. This is clearly a piece of ethnohistory and the historian’s method of examining new documents allowed a richer, more objective history. She explained the social stratification within these orphanages and even the difference between orphans and foundlings. She concluded that people took advantage of these institutions in order to better provide for their children, a very interesting observation.
While learning the methodology is exciting, I envy historians’ minds who process this information automatically.
