Primary Sources

Posted: Tue, 29 Mar 2011

       So I thought I would write a bit about each of the chapters I read on primary sources for this past week: letters, newspapers, and court files.  First off, court files document by what standards people in the past were judged and how people interacted with these standards.  As an historian, when working with court files it is important to recognize that they contain a multiplicity of content (i.e. interrogations, testimonies, verdicts).  While this certainly gives the researcher a plethora of potential information, often the varying accounts can be riddled with conscious distortions of the truth.  Beyond this, the book lays out three key concepts to consider when using court files as primary sources.  First, one must remember the dialogical nature of court files — usually the documents are a compilation of multiple perspectives.  Moreover, it is critical to consider the files’ emplotment: under what circumstances led to the court files’ creation?  Lastly, the nature of court files presupposes an underlying cultural context, as one can catch a glimpse of what people in the past considered normal behavior.  Working with Constitutionalism as a topic I feel like I will be reading many, many court files as I move forward.  In fact, the article I read for the paper on methodology employed court files successfully in showing how certain cases increased the governmentâ€™s role within the American economy, albeit Constitutional limitations.
       The next primary source I read about was newspapers, as I unintelligibly discussed in class.  Newspapers are distinguishable sources largely because they filter through raw news and cover only the most contemporary issues.  Additionally, newspapers stand out as a source because they rely on paid advertisements to survive, providing advertisers with de facto licensing power.  Clearly, one has to consider this along with other elements of the paper’s institutional framework to determine if newspapers are source-worthy.  For instance, who really controls the paper: the editorial staff or the paying advertisers?  Another factor to reflect on is the visual context and overall editorial decisions that contributed to the newspapers’ production.  It makes quite a bit of difference in knowing whether an article was a front page piece or simply shoved somewhere along the inner pages of the newspaper.  Lastly, a potential researcher would also want to review the textual context of the piece.  At times the author’s tone, narrative voice, and sources used can help you determine if the newspaper is a credible source.  Clearly, newspapers are a tricky source to use effectively as they can be hard to analyze and have a great many prospective downfalls.
       Last but not least I also wanted to read the section covering letters as primary sources.  As the book discusses briefly, it is interesting to contemplate how the use of letters in writing great man history was a precursor to its extensive use in writing histories from below.  Letter writing is a relational process as one learns about both the author and the intended reader in accessing the personal life of the correspondents.  However, personal letters exist alongside more official letters, perhaps between a citizen and their governing body.  Obviously I feel like I would be working more with official letters while composing a political history versus a social one.  There are a few things that affect the way a letter writer articulates his/her thoughts, slightly limiting the reader’s window into the author’s views.  For one, conventions of letter writing constrain the author into pre-conceived formalities of letter composition.  Moreover, the authors words are molded by what they think the letter recipient expects.  However, in the end it seemed to me like the book offered letters as one of the better sources, at least in evaluating the mindset of an individual author.
