From Quito to England

Posted: Thu, 17 Feb 2011

This week’s class began with a library lesson that seemed invaluable to the production of our theses, but was also overwhelming. The sheer amount of information out there makes me sympathize with Lord Acton, but hopefully once my topic is narrowed down I won’t be so apprehensive about the research. The first reading of the week targeted this task of formulating a research question, and I can’t wait to have this completed. The reading on Quito was quite a bit more manageable than that of the moral economy in England, simply because of length. In the article on Quito, Gauderman’s subject was the refutation of the patriarchal label used to describe gender domination in Spanish colonies. Her article starts with a broad scope and narrows down to prove her point. She lays out women’s roles in the economy in this culture, then moves to the economy of Quito, and finally to textile production. Her method is very effective and her illustrative narration keeps the reader engaged. The object of research is the obraje owners and through the examination of notarial and criminal records she constructs a realistic view of them. This article accurately refuted the widely accepted label of patriarchy in this culture, and thusly her research problem was solved … a historian’s dream. The article regarding the moral economy in England during the 18th century was a little less engaging, but still proved and interesting point. The author refuted the spasmodic character of the food riots and did so through the lens of the worker. He successfully proved these occurrences were often supported by the community and certain officials, thusly legitimizing the workers’ actions. Both pieces were not only professional analyses of history, but were interesting as well. I hope my thesis can achieve this goal.
