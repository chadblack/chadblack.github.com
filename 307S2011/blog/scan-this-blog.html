Scan this blog!

Posted: Sat, 23 Apr 2011

While I was away at camp, the class read two very interesting articles regarding source digitization and archival preservation. The first article was published in the New York Times and explored a topic I was aware of but was ignorant of its depth and consequence. In Scan This Book!, Kevin Kelly discusses the promise and difficulties surrounding the placement of all the world’s work (especially books) on the web. He stresses the dynamic nature of this new database, and the relationships amongst works would be invaluable into learning and gaining new insights into what we already know. This would be invaluable in our field of history. If one could search the masses of primary sources, journal articles, and books surrounding a topic and employ the relationships outlined, there would be masses of new and innovative work produced. I can barely comprehend the effects this universal library would have upon the field of history. The problems regarding this digitization revolve around the hazy area of copyrights. With many copyrights being difficult to trace and copyright laws extending the length of these rights for ridiculous periods of time, there is a legal hurricane around this area. In the end, I side with Google, and see this potential library as the word’s greatest invention.

The second article, written by Louis Bickford, addressed the topic of the preservation of the documents produced by HRGNOs in the southern cone of South America. This region has experienced recent rights violations and the people are divided between remembering/addressing and forgetting/ignoring these atrocities. One can understand both points of view, but to forget can almost erase an event from history as shown by the genocide in the Belgian Congo. This clearly supports the stance of remembrance, and while it takes time for a nation to revisit the events, they must be aware of the preservation of documents, especially those from HRGNOs. Bickford stresses that this is absent in this southern cone and efforts must be increased to archive these materials. This is important to us because we understand the necessity of primary documents to revisit, reconstruct, and analyze these past events. While some want to forget, remembering will create a greater sense of appreciation for those who endured and will foster a society more vigilant of possible violations.
