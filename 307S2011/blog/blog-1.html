Blog 1

Posted: Mon, 24 Jan 2011

          Hey guys, I’m just here to briefly comment on last week’s readings and discussion.  So part one of The Modern Historiography Reader was a collection of four historian’s viewpoints on the scholarship of history.  Wow, that sounds extremely interesting……  The first three authors pose the essential question of what exactly history is and what it is professional historians should or should not do.  Lord Acton’s dilemma, in which he grappled with a moral obligation to point out the uniting and associated conditions of civilization, was intriguing when you consider what Cronon wrote in 2004.  In order to gain a real understanding of any past event, you have to analyze how each social/political aspect of the past relates to one another.  Like Cronon suggests, all historical knowledge is relational and the past is only a product of multicausal explanations.  My favorite article of part one was Carl Becker’s construction of Mr. Everyman, whom in some ways employs history and historical research in his everyday life.  Becker seemed to make the vocation more accessible by showing how most everyone will at some point make use of critical research methods that characterize the historical field.  Last of the four articles is Bonnie Smith’s “What is a Historian?”  Studying languages during youth, as the nineteenth century adolescences did, would have been the best way to prepare for a profession in history.  In today’s world, having a familiarity with multiple languages would obviously be very beneficial to an aspiring historian.  Smith also speaks of the broad gender relations that have characterized the professional world of history.  I had no idea that many historians were considered “so gendered in a way” because of the significance of their wives to their historical authorship.
          The fifth part of The Modern Historiography Reader was intriguing enough, mainly because I related to James Robinson’s outlook on ‘The New History’.  Don’t we all hope that in some way our historical writing could be relevant to current times?  I feel like I need to write a history that is compelling enough, through fact alone, to make a reader potentially change his/her perspective on any given political or social issue.
          Lastly, the Carnegie Essays on the Doctorate provided an interesting depiction of the requirements and work necessary to become part of the historian’s guild.  The way in which Cronon divvies up the shared values of the ‘historian’ helps one understand the intricacies of the professional doctorate.  Additionally, the information in the article helps shape my personal opinion of what would make a good senior dissertation topic.  For example, if one holds that history is inevitably written from a particular point of view and that this in turn shapes histories by correlating it with present politics, problems arise in potential topics.  In fact, it becomes a question or whether one could ever even delve into a historical topic of recent happening without entirely too much bias or subjectivity.
