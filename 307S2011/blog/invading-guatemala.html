Invading Guatemala

Posted: Mon, 04 Apr 2011

The account of Bernal Diaz Castillo in Invading Guatemala, (page 61-69) bordered on ridiculous. That being said, there were portions of the account which could be of use. First, some of the comical, like “Pedro Alvarado went to the province of Guatemala to found a town, and bring them peace.” For some reason that reminded of Ronald Reagan naming a ballistic nuclear missile, “The Peacemaker,” but I digress. Castillo also wrote about “cages that held Indians until they were fat enough to eat.” After the long climb to the top of the pass Castillo claims a force of six thousand attacked, while Alvarado (already known for inflating enemy strength) only reported three or four thousand. The end of the Castillo account said, “they were not fighters because they only lay in wait for us in the gorges, and shot at us with their arrows.” This combined with other statements about the ease by which they “made peace” (conquered the enemy) indicates a complete lack of respect for the enemy. I found this curious because it has the effect of diminishing the alleged victory because the opponent is unworthy.  In 19th Century North America we see the development of the noble savage, a “worthy” opponent, as a means to aggrandize ones on accomplishments in battle.

The limited value of the Castillo account can be found in the similarities with other versions; and as we discussed in class it can help to establish a timeline. He also mentioned the dead woman and dog reportedly a sacrifice, but Castillo did not indicate that it was a warning. That part of this story is a mysterious little detail, but it is clear by its very mention, that it had an effect on the Spaniards. When a source is so clearly biased it almost makes it easier to gain value from his/her writings because you do not need to determine the bias and you can set aside the information which is clearly prejudicial, like cannibalism.  Good book very useful for comparative analysis of an event with multiple sources.
