Archival Imperative

Posted: Tue, 19 Apr 2011

  The first article by Louis Bickford chronicled the archival imperative in Chile, Argentina, and Uruguay.  Bickford focused specifically within the realm of human rights’ documents, discussing the dire need to archive past human rights’ atrocities.  Far too often these documents are found sitting in dusty boxes, unorganized, and shoved into the most readily available space.  Moreover, Bickford identifies an obvious division between those whom stress remembering and those whom advocate forgetting their state’s traumatic past.  Another obstacle to appropriate human rights’ archiving is the matter of money, as the archives in Chile, Argentina, and Uruguay have very small, insufficiently funded budgets.  Bickford spends time identifying the various documents created by the HRNGOs on a country-by-country basis and then delves into her own well-developed archival construction plan.  She lays out a path that could result in appropriate archival preservation but doesn’t really deal with how these archives could get funding for the process.  State and private support are nearly non-existent and I just don’t see the archives being able to afford microform machines, film, or hardware on their own.
  The second article was entitled Scan This! by Kevin Kelly of Wired Magazine.  Currently I’m using Google’s Chrome browser to surf the web, I passed Spanish thanks to Google Translate, and now my phone is even running Google’s own operating system.  It seems as if there are no limitations to Google’s expanding reach, but… a universal library?  A library containing every facet of human knowledge, ranging from every text written to every picture taken? Impossible.  Yet, in today’s world of increasing archival preservation, where we have SUV-sized robot photocopiers, is it really that impossible?
  It is unfortunate that almost all intellectual property originating in today’s world is subject to such stringent copyright laws.  However, as Kevin Kelly points out, copyright laws may be the last saving grace of an already failing business model.  The world in 2011 is a fast-moving, technologically centric place where information can easily be transmitted with the click of a mouse.  Just consider the music industry and their attempts to regulate song distribution with tight copyright laws.  If I were so willing, it would be easy to circumvent these restrictions and obtain almost any song without cost and with minimal fear of repercussions. This is an obvious example of the legal clash between the physical copy and the searchable Web, a clash which Kelly promises will be a long one.  In the case of books, I feel as if there is a clear avenue for Google to pursue the 75% of dark-horse books with no clear copyright owners.  These books need to be returned to the ‘public sphere’ where they can help deepen our cultural and historical knowledge. As books become cross-linked with one another and begin to sport fancy user-generated tags, I don’t feel as if the historian’s job becomes easier per se.  One would think that the sheer amount of available information would ideally lead to new histories or perhaps at least the reimagining of past histories.  As is obvious, a universal library with infinite primary and secondary digitized sources would be the ultimate benefactor for any research.  However, as each researcher looked for the perfect amalgamation of all sources into the perfect interpretative framework, it seems like the researcher’s job difficulty could increase exponentially.
