Context Matters

Posted: Sun, 06 Mar 2011

       The article on ethnohistory by Russell Barber and Frances Berdan wasn’t the first time I had been introduced to the idea, but it was certainly helpful to analyze the authors’ attempts to conceptualize the study of ethnohistory.  One might think the task impossible after the failures of the Ethnohistory journal to establish a simple, yet fully encompassing mission statement.  However, the authors of the article spend less time worrying about formal definitions and more time focusing on how ethnohistory is defined by actions.  These actions include the research, scholarly output, and methodological approaches manipulated by the common ethnohistorian.  In fact, the author spends considerable time discussing the assumptions and approaches that combine to establish an interpretative framework by which the ethnohistorian constructs their work.  In many instances assumptions and approaches can be understood as dichotomies.  Take for example the differences in methodologies as represented between emic vs. etic / particularist vs. nomothetic / single-factor vs. multi-factor.  The decisions ethnohistorians make about which approaches to use can be largely extrapolated to most other scholarly discourses.  When assessing a primary document or generally conducting any kind of research, it’s crucial to employ a wide variety of appropriate methodological approaches. There is not a formula for applying these methodologies, nor any set of guidelines.  In reality, the methodological approaches one uses can and most likely should change as you move from topic to topic or source to source.  In part, recognizing an author’s methodology plays into the idea of ‘deconstruction’, the act of identifying an author’s general mindset and how it shapes a resultant document.  As we derived in class, context matters above all else.
       The other two articles were interesting in the ways they approached their objects of research: the capital case files held by Canada’s national archive in one and the records generated by the orphanages of Seville in the other.  For example, the authors of On the Case: Explorations in Social History recognized the complexity of power dynamics that was inherent in the context of bureaucratic case files.  In fact, the authors imbue that the reader must understand a case file’s original purpose, nature, and structure.  In other words, the contexts that the case files were established in matters beyond all else.
