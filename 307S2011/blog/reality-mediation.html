Reality Mediation

Posted: Sun, 06 Mar 2011

The readings every week seem to do a good job of putting words to concepts that once revealed seem obvious. This weeks readings were interesting, particularly the Emperors Mirror and the discussion on Reality Mediation.  The authors, Russell J. Barber and Frances F. Berdan discuss the difference between a historians description and reality.  We may intuitively believe that our description is the same as reality but if you think about it you realize that, like 100% objectivity, you can never completely capture the reality of a past event no matter how reliable your primary sources and observer of the event happen to be; but if we understand four ways to mediate that reality then we can write a more accurate description. The first is selection, what portions of a reality are left out. We need to be conscious of what does not make our description and why. This analysis might prevent us from overlooking a key aspect of our primary source. The next is emphasis; once something makes it into our description which sections receive more emphasis and why. The third is transformation; something was selected then modified.  Make sure you understand first that it was modified and then again ask why. The last notion is fabrication; beware of the source that is not telling the truth. (Barber and Berdan, 39.) The authors provided the example of Champlain’s description of an exotic bird sighting used to explain a detour in his journey.  It is suspected that he made up the bird to cover for being lost.  This piece of information might useful in a way other than rare North American Ornithological studies, it might for example refute the notion that Champlain never got lost.

By using what seem to be obvious concepts we can utilize this process of Reality Mediation to attempt to produce the most accurate description of events possible.
