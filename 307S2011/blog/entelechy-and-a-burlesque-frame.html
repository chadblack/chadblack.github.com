Entelechy and a Burlesque Frame

Posted: Wed, 06 Apr 2011

I was not sure if I was behind a blog or not so I thought I would take this opportunity to discuss a very complicated article (self assigned), I am reading for my research project in History 373. The article is cited as follows:

Hubbard, Bryan(1998) ‘Reassessing Truman, the bomb, and revisionism: The burlesque frame and entelechy in the decision to use atomic weapons against Japan’, Western Journal of Communication, 62: 3, 348 — 385.

 Is there anything in the title that seems a little complicated? “Burlesque frame” in reference to the use of atomic weapons; and in case you missed it, “entelechy.” When I read that I thought I had mistakenly click on something from the History 307 reading list. I looked up entelechy and got the best definition from Britannica, which said, “in philosophy, that which realizes or makes actual what is otherwise merely potential.” Now that I know what it means, I definitely know that as a historian I would want to avoid it.  I have been reading articles on Truman’s decision to use the bomb on Japan and was unable to put to words what some of the revisionists have been using as proof until I came to understand entelechy.  For example, Gar Alperovitz (big critic of the Truman decision) wrote about Japanese peace overtures to the USSR as if they were in fact peace overtures to all. I wanted to criticize that in my paper but lacked the vocabulary to make a clean attack.  Later, Hubbard titles a section, “Burlesque Frame: The Missing Context of Revisionist Re-readings,” and I thought “context is everything.” If you are still reading this post you deserve an explanation of the burlesque frame. Hubbard (credited to Burke) utilizes this particular “rhetorical frame” to provide a context that is the “mirror image” of the intuitive war frame of tragedy. The use of the Burlesque or comedic frame means that you avoid identifying with the victims of war so you do not lose your objectivity. This works because the actors in the Burlesque are absurd and do not garner pity. That frame helps us as historians, but Hubbard also writes that because the parties in war dehumanize their victims as means to accomplish their goals, that the Burlesque Frame is the most proper wartime context. Hubbard also used entelechy as the momentum behind the decision in his own analysis of Truman’s decision. This article was a nice piece of historiography which analyzed the debate and versions over what some believe to be the biggest historical event of the 20th century.
