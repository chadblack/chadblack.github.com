Object of Research or Subject of Argument?

Posted: Mon, 21 Feb 2011

       After reading and discussing the articles assigned for this past week. I’m thankful to be somewhat more clear on the differences between object of research and subject of argument.  The corollary between the two is simply that the objects of research generally act as the vehicle through which a historian arrives at his subject of argument.  A subject is wholly unsubstantiated without objects of research, and conversely, objects of research are merely pieces of evidence that necessitate a grander subjective interpretation.
       In the case of Kimberly Gauderman’s article, it is not terribly hard to recognize the objects of research: the women obraje owners and their respective stake in the economy of Quito.  Using the experiences of these women, Gauderman makes a claim to the opposition of patriarchal dominance that has traditionally been afforded to Spanish colonial territories.  It seemed considerably more difficult to identify the objects of research in the book I read for the historical problem paper.   Briefly, the book is about constitutionalism, the amending process, and the way in which amendments serve as a key indicator of changing American political thought.  Unlike either of the two assigned articles, the author chooses to partition his work into chronological divisions, divvying up constitutional amendments into eras of political change.  Therein which, the author narrates his claims by providing a plethora of political anecdotes (meetings, dialogues, conventions, court rulings) that intend to envelop the reader in the author’s main claim.  Are the objects of research these political anecdotes or more broadly and simply, just the entire chronological era of political thought that the author analyzes as divided by chapter?
       Another question that arose to me was whether or not the author’s historiographical approach to the work was considered in analyzing his/her narrative.  The two articles we read for last class used a cultural approach, largely determining how race, gender, or class played into their respective issues/claims.  Instead, the aforementioned book on the amending process recognizes the ‘Great Man’ historical approach, transitioning from one political agent or political event, straight to the next one.  Are these varying approaches relevant in narrative?
       The other reading The Craft of Research was a well-prepared, if somewhat formulaic, look at how to make a legitimate claim with full supporting reasons in the context of a lengthy research essay.  I feel like some of the most important things to take from the reading were the strategies employed by the authors in answering and responding to counter-claims by hypothetical readers.  Sometimes, I feel as if the flaw in my writing is an inability to conform my thoughts to meet the needs of my readers.  The tips offered by Booth are helpful in considering where readers will find problems with your writing, and how to construct appropriate responses to these arguments.
