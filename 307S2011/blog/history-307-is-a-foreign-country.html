History 307 is a Foreign Country

Posted: Tue, 01 Feb 2011

This past week’s discussion began with something rather comfortable to me, footnotes. After feeling rather intellectually inadequate due to the pre-class readings, this was a welcome topic. We settled on the notion that footnotes establish the author’s credibility as well as competence. We moved on to the affect the publishing industry has upon academic research and work. The ever present drive within (publication) companies for money seems to have changed the atmosphere of academic work in a negative aspect. The researcher not only desires to be published but usually needs to be in order to continue their profession. This need empowers the publisher and allows them to influence the content of the final product (i.e. footnotes, subject matter). Dr. Black then explained the levels of employment within the University in regards to professors and I honestly had no idea of the struggle for PhD’s to not only gain tenure, but a reasonable living! It explained a lot about the professors I have had in the past three years and I think I could guess which ones had more stable positions. This all related to the relationship between structure and agency in historiography. This publishing/university structure was deemed to inhibit the historian’s agency, and in order to have more of a free reign upon deciphering history, this must change.

This discussion then morphed into one that incorporated the readings, a time I was not looking forward to. While feeling like a lost puppy, we stumbled upon the topic of historical materialism vs. idealism. We defined materialism to mean the search for tangible explanations, in regards to historical causes, while idealism deals with the abstract. An example of materialism would be the Marxist approach to history and one of the readings addressed a specific group of scholars in Britain that followed this stringently. I feel I am oversimplifying or under-qualified, but I think I would take both approaches into consideration while writing history (Is this possible?). Our conversation then dealt with the derivation of meaning within history, and the cultural lens that must be present to do this. It seems that history is all about context, and Dr. Black then posed the question: Can a man accurately write the history of a woman? While the sexes may differ and this has caused inaccurate historical accounts in the past, an objective approach could definitely allow a history to be written. As long as the male not only considers the culture and structures present, but also the woman’s place in it, then it could be done.

I feel I have rambled/summarized enough and welcome enlightenment provided by my classmates and teacher.
