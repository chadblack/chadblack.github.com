Objectivity

Posted: Fri, 28 Jan 2011

Mark Bevir might be guilty of sophistry, but if not that, he is certainly guilty of first degree verbosity. To quote the great amateur historian Forrest Gump, “I am not a smart man,” but I will attempt to comment on/interpret his article. Let me start by saying that the Bevir piece had more -isms than my Philosophy class last semester (God I hated that class), back to Bevir and objectivity.  “No existing past exists in which to judge rival interpretations” that is why we lose all objectivity, relativism aside?  I think I understand what that means but am concerned about why it is a serious challenge to Bevir’s notion of objectivity. If I study a primary source historical document, interpret, and then write about it. My analysis will certainly be based on my current day experience, it is unavoidable. Does that mean I am not objective just because there are no interpretations from another time in which to compare my work? I agree with Bevir that like the scientists he mentions our theories should not be compared to prior work but to other work of our time. Comparisons to a prior time would help in determining the motives and value systems of the earlier historian; for example examining the influence of pseudo-science like Social Darwinism on an early 20th century historian’s interpretation of the Spanish American War.  What influences my work today and what might future historians say about it? I don’t care about future critics of my objectivity because the audience I am most concerned about lives in the 21st Century.  I also agree with Bevir that when we compare our work with others we can find common ground or overlapping theories on which to try and determine fact. For me it is as simple as this, strive to be 100% objective and 100% accurate but know it is a little bit like an irrational number at some point you need to round it off and except your answer as the most accurate possible.  While complicated the article does make for an interesting debate, I just wish he would have written it in language that Forrest would have understood.
