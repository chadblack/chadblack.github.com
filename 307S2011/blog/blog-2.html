Blog 2

Posted: Tue, 01 Feb 2011

        For this past week’s literature I decided to take notes as I was reading through it, primarily in order to help myself recognize some of the larger and interconnected ideas.  During discussion we didn’t really have time to delve into Mark Bevir’s ‘web of interpretation’ ideas, though I found them be pretty interesting.  Bevir holds that historians seek to establish a web of interpretation, formulated in terms of agreed facts, to help explain past events.  The interpretation should be analogous to a web, in that it should be extensive and all encompassing.  In fact, I think Bevir used the terms ‘fruitful’ and ‘open’ to describe any historical interpretation.  It is also crucial to recognize the fluidity of any possible interpretation of the past.  Inevitably, all interpretations and theories are subject to outside criticisms.  Yet, if one has established the fruitful and fluid ‘web’ of interpretation Bevir speaks of, it should be easy to incorporate opposing arguments into your own.  I wonder if Bevir would have said that historical knowledge (rooted in historical interpretation) only ever advances forward as the best, most successful interpretations continue to improve by incorporating or overcoming criticisms?
As to what we discussed in class, I’ve been thinking a bit about personal agency in history and its tensions with structure.  I know to exemplify ‘structure’ we provided such ideas as funding models and disciplinary standards, which definitely do limit one’s capacity to act.  However, when I hear ‘structure’ that limits one’s ability to act, I can’t help but think of the monolithic American government.  In a supposed free society, our government tightly watches those who have the greatest agency.  For example, consider Nixon’s paranoia with John Lennon and Lennon’s simple message of peace.  The American government tapped Lennon’s phone and had men follow him, primarily because they feared his capacity to act, his ability to impact society.  Marxist history or ‘history from below’ would have one believe that limited agency is a product of your social position, rooted in an economic framework.  In my life, I feel as if I have enjoyed a great number of freedoms that have allowed me to act and affect things external to myself.  Yet, if the government intends to strip our freedoms away and keep us tied down by a massive institute, which only has its best interests in mind, what hope is there for our personal agency within society?




I had to watch a documentary on Lennon for my Cinema Studies/American Politics class, so sorry about the above rant.  See you guys Wednesday.
