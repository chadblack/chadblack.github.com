Historiography I

Posted: Wed, 19 Jan 2011

Historiography- The study of how historians do history; that sounds about as exciting as watching paint dry, but I must admit that as an historian apprentice, I found parts of this week’s readings intriguing. I particularly liked the William Cronon essay and his discussion on objectivity. While absolute objectivity should be our goal, I think it is important to realize that it is unattainable. We will never be able to study our subject matter and leave our personal beliefs and culture behind. As Cronan points out realizing historians are biased helps us to critically analyze their work, and then seek the “other” point of view creating a broader view and hopefully a deeper understanding of the issue.
Understanding our bias should not prevent us from being as objective as possible. For example, I am a Coast Guard retiree and very proud of my service and the Coast Guard’s history and most of its missions. My senior thesis will probably deal with Coast Guard history. So if through the course of my research I discover something relevant to my subject matter but also unfavorable towards the Coast Guard would I publish the unfavorable information? Absolutely, for two reasons: One, because it is the right thing to do and secondly, because I would control the initial message. Another example from the other direction; I personally feel that the Coast Guard drug interdiction mission is a total failure and an enormous waste of tax payer dollars. Were I to write on the history of this mission I would include the proponents argument as a way to strengthen my own thesis. If I found that the evidence did not support my thesis and the mission is a successful one, then I would change my position and write it that way. Again because it is the right thing to do and also when I write something I want it to be as strong as possible, not necessarily bulletproof but praiseworthy. When I say the “right thing to do” it means the right thing as a reputable historian, not in the Lord Acton moral sense of right. I believe he crosses a line with his bias by instilling a pre-supposed moral code onto his subject matter. This could result in making the research less valid and more fantastical. For example, were Lord Acton to have discovered a primary source document which put his moral code and belief system in jeopardy would he have given it the proper credence? I think not.
Cronon’s comment concerning journalists writing history was interesting because the best history book I read this year was “Unbroken” written by journalist Laura Hillenbrand. Her research was outstanding and the narrative of Louis Zamperini a WWII POW was very compelling, proving that you don’t have to be a historian to do great history.

That’s all for this week.
