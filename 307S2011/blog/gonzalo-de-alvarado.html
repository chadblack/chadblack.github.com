Gonzalo de Alvarado

Posted: Tue, 05 Apr 2011

          Invading Guatemala by Matthew Restall and Florine Asselbergs provides us with a great example of how historical interpretation can rarely be justified through the use of a single primary document.  As evidence, consider the inaccurate scholarship prior to 2004 that juxtaposed Pedro de Alvarado as the primary conqueror of Guatemala.  If a historian were to construct his record of the events based solely on the accounts provided by Pedro, it would certainly be a limited work, positioning that he himself was responsible for the conquering and subsequent colonization.  However, when one reviews the letters provided by Pedro, Gonzalo, Jorge, and the Nahua/Maya accounts it becomes easy to see that many people were involved in the conquest, some even more so than Pedro himself.
          The testimony provided by Pedro’s cousin Gonzalo de Alvarado, whom joined his campaign in 1524, gives insight into an alternative dimension of the Guatemalan conquest.  As it were, Gonzalo’s writings originated as petitions to the King of Spain asking for retribution in light of his extensive work within Guatemala.  This institutional context has enormous consequences on the author’s mindset, tone, and overall flow of the piece.  For example, Pedro Alvarado’s letters like to emphasize the successes of the Guatemalan conquest in order to influence Pedro’s perceived image by Cortes, the Spanish King, and the larger Spanish populace.  On the other hand, Gonzalo Alvarado’s petition emphasizes the enormity of personal risk and subsequent losses by the Spanish conquistador.  Gonzalo’s rhetoric often makes the reader believe that only through amazing perseverance and luck did the conquering company manage to survive.  Additionally, unlike his cousin Jorge, Gonzalo seems to support the idea that Pedro Alvarado was the main actor in Guatemala’s conquest.  This is most likely due to the fact that Gonzalo wanted to exaggerate Pedro’s accomplishments, hoping the King would readily give pension/money to a close associate of the great conqueror.  
          Within a discursive analysis of Gonzalo’s letter, it proves exceptionally hard to follow any sensible chronological framework.  However, here is my attempt at formulating a timeline of Gonzalo’s travels:

Set-up: Gonzalo joins Pedro on his way to Guatemala and Cuzcatan.
Day 1: Gonzalo and company defend themselves against warriors at entrance to Capotitlan.
Day 2: Gonzalo and company arrive at Capotitlan and battle many fierce warriors.
Day 2 (night): Gonzalo and company take arrow bombardment from enemies.
Day 4: Company arrives in the town of Quezaltenango.
Day 7: Ten thousand Indians gather to wage war against conquerors.  Pedro attempts to offer peace, but messengers are rejected.
Day 8:  War in the town of Quezaltenango.
Day ?: (Some days after aforementioned battle): Company arrives in province of Utatlan.
Day ?: Company arrives in Guatemala and set out to pacify Cuzcatlan.
Day ?: (Many days after conquest of Cuzcatlan): Company returns to Guatemala and sets out in search of the “North Sea.”
Day ?: “Having walked for many days,” the company reaches an impasse and must look for an alternative route home.
Day ?: After many days, Gonzalo finds a road leading to Tecpan Puyumatlan.
Day ?: The company is attacked numerous times while travelling through Tecpan Puyumatlan province.
Day ?: After many days, the company must return to Guatemala due to lack of food and supplies.
Day ?: Lastly, Gonzalo delves into a generalized description of the six year uprisings and subsequent hardships he faced.

As you can see, it is almost impossible to create any sort of informative timeline based solely on Gonzalo’s testimony as he rarely provides approximate dates.
