Is the foreign so foreign?

Posted: Tue, 08 Feb 2011
“Structures then are “virtual” and are put into practice in the production and reproduction of social life.”

The Leslie Reagan book in an essence describes the idea of history being a foreign country. The vocabulary and their view on life were completely different. But, through the interpretation that Raegan gives, she is able to give understanding and significance to a time that is foreign to the modern reader. She is able to point out what is significant in that time and then come up with a thesis that is relevant in a modern perspective. She is able to take a study that is centered when abortion was a crime and then make conclusions to when it became legal. She is able to give a perspective on that change that is well thought out and shows the positive and negative changes that occurred because of the legalization. This is the responsibility of the historian to take the foreign country and interpret. To give their interpretation of the events in the most unbiased way possible. Inevitably it will come out, and that is what makes historiography interesting. It is what historians do with the research they have in presenting it in their final product.

In Sewall’s discussion of structure one of the first points he talks about is how structure can be limiting in thinking about the past. One of the best quotes he talks about this is, “A social Science trapped in an unexamined metaphor of structure tends to reduce actors to cleverly programmed automatons.” This is a huge boundary to get over as a historian. To give people of the past real human emotions. They were in some ways very much like modern humans. They had thoughts and feelings. These emotions effect their decisions, which in turn would have affected the process of events following these decisions.

Anthony Gidden’s idea of structure was the most useful to determining the importance of structure. Especially in the form of resources. Gidden’s best quote was when he stated; “resources are anything that can serve as a source of power in social interactions.” Another quote that helped to not just explain Gidden’s own ideas about structure but Sewell’s was “Structures are not the patterned social practices that make up social systems, but the principles that pattern these practices.” The metaphor of the working class in England could work in this situation. For the point of structure it is not the actual social structure of that society it was the decisions and then the eventual revolution that put them in that situation. Gidden puts it very simply as the process that is the structure. This fits into agency because agency then is the situation that the structure has put them in.
