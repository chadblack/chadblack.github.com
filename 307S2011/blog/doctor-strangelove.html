Doctor Strangelove

Posted: Tue, 19 Apr 2011

I am writing about the National Security Archive, which their website describes as, “An independent non-governmental research institute and library located at The George Washington University, the Archive collects and publishes declassified documents obtained through the Freedom of Information Act.” I had never heard of this website, but it appears to be source for CIA, Nuclear Policy and a whole host of information that make conspiracy theorists toes tingle. This post will focus on a film produced by the U.S. Air Force in the mid 1950s. From a historiographical point of view, I believe it gives some insight into the thought process of the senior nuclear policy makers in the Pentagon. This piece could definitely provide some context for a person studying the Cuban Missile Crisis for example. The film demonstrates a kind of automatic response to information that does not seem to be verified by multiple sources. The film had a very “Dr. Strangelove” feel to it, which was scary for a guy who used to go through the crawl under you elementary school desk drill. The write up for the film said, “By the early 1950s, senior military planners and defense officials had begun considering the possibility of pre-emptive attacks on the basis of strategic warning; that is, if the United States intelligence warning system collected reliable information on an impending Soviet attack, decision-makers could approve strikes against Soviet military forces to disrupt it.”[1] Let’s take them out before they take us out. It scares me to think how close we really were to completely destroying the Earth, and while the nuclear threat is still a very real and present danger, the world is no doubt safer now that the Cold War is over. Imagine if George W. Bush, as his team of Neo-Cons, were in power during the Cuban Missile Crisis in 1962. We might not be here today, or if we were the place might look like a Cormac McCarthy novel.

[1] William Burr, “‘Nobody Wins a Nuclear War’ But ‘Success’ is Possible
Mixed Message of 1950s Air Force Film on a U.S.-Soviet Conflict,” National Security Archive, National Security Archive Electronic Briefing Book No. 336, date accessed April 19, 2011, http://www.gwu.edu/~nsarchiv/nukevault/ebb336/index.htm
