Timeline vs. Metaphor

Posted: Tue, 05 Apr 2011

This past week we read Invading Guatemala: Spanish, Nahua, and Maya Accounts of the Conquest Wars written by Matthew Restall and Florine Asselbergs. The title is description enough, as the book presents and analyzes various first hand accounts of the conquest of Guatemala. We analyzed the third letter from Pedro Alvarado to Hernando Cortés and attempted to reconstruct a timeline and analyze the “literal” facts and also examined the use of metaphors within the letter.  After class I applied similar tactics to Bernal Díaz’s account of Pedro’s conquest of Guatemala and it was very interesting corroborating the details as well as analyzing the piece with some new historical skill. Díaz was also a conquistador in the region but arrived later than Alvarado and did not participate in all of his conquests. The tone of his account is similar to that of Pedro’s as he clearly views the natives as less civilized and their towns as “unsettled”. When searching the piece for metaphor, one realizes that Díaz characterizes the native societies according to the structures of his own, exactly as Pedro Alvarado does in his letter. The employment of metaphors such a town, city, fort, province, and region are all examples of this organization according to Spanish conceptions of society. While the metaphors employed paralleled Alvarado’s letter, so did the factual evidence. Events such as coming across a sacrificed dog and woman, mountain pass battles, the burning of town leaders, and the peacemaking efforts are present in both reports. The validation of such information is exciting, but one also must take into account Díaz’s sources, of which I am unaware. Even looking at the map and making a timeline, I found it difficult to directly tie the dates together even though they have similar chronicles. I found this exercise in class to be very stimulating and informative, and doing it out of class built my confidence and kindled my historical interest.
