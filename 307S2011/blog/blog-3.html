Blog 3

Posted: Mon, 07 Feb 2011

        William Sewell lays out a much more complex theory of structure than I had considered last week.  In his view, every structure that controls some aspect of social life can also be seen as structuring some other aspect.  Thus, structures can be seen as numerous and overlapping in many respects.  Sewell’s conceptualization of heterogeneous structures plays heavily into his determination to prove structural change is possible.  Indeed, Sewell puts together a formulaic argument based on his proposed five axioms, which posits a sustaining schemas/resources duality that control social action.  Structural change is possible because structures are a product of the social action that they control; yet that production is neither automatic nor singularly controlled.
        In my last blog, I talked a little bit about how a government could be seen as a limiting structure in its tension with personal agency.  Thus, it was refreshing to see Sewell discuss it, especially in terms of how structures harbor power, though may not be extremely well entrenched in social life.  Sewell describes the political ‘state’ as relatively near the surface of social life, which to me means that political structures are normally fragile despite their large power allocations.  Sewell uses the state analogy to show that the durability of a social structure is determined more by the structure’s depth than its power.
       Sewell’s interpretation of agency as a constituent to, rather than in opposition with, structure is confusing to me considering all we talked about in class.  Sewell says that the “capacity for agency is as much a given for humans as the capacity for respiration.”  Perhaps the capacity is there, but structural limitations work to oppose agency to such a degree, I don’t really see how one can say that structure and agency work in accordance.
       Finally, Leslie Reagan’s book When Abortion Was A Crime was an enjoyable read, as I felt that her narrative voice spoke for her rather than any pre-conceived bias.  I honestly don’t know if Leslie Reagan is pro-choice or pro-life, but most importantly, it doesn’t really matter.  It was nice to read the history of a controversial present-day issue, presented in terms of anecdotal historical evidence.
Reagan uses a mixture of deductive and inductive reasoning to reconstruct the societal happenings that occurred as a reaction to the criminalization of abortion.  The information Reagan could gleam from the context of a conversation was intriguing in and of itself.  If Reagan hadn’t provided all the stories/conversations that she did, I think her history of abortion as a crime would have been limited and incomplete, simply because of the amount she interpreted from those conversations.
