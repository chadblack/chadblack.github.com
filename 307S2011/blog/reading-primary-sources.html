Reading Primary Sources

Posted: Mon, 28 Mar 2011

This week’s assignment, “Reading Primary Sources” a multiple author compilation edited by Miriam Dobson (she is a girl) and Benjamin Ziemann (boy), was my favorite so far because out of all the prior readings this one seemed the most practical and useful of the lot.  I understand that is by design at this point in the semester, but it is nice to transition away from theory and passed (pun intended) historians.  I know we still had some dead Germans to talk about but part II was a great “how to guide” on a variety of sources, all of which I have decided can be called e-mail.  I wanted to discuss the two chapters I did not have time to discuss in class. Beyond diaries, I chose opinion polls and testimony. I have always heard that opinion polls can be manipulated to mean or say whatever you like, but I never thought about how they can be used to manipulate public opinion. When the media reports a poll “like it is science” the result can begin to sway the public in the direction previously reported in the poll.  See, we are a trendy species and at times can allow our minds to be changed on issues based on their perceived popularity.  Polls are not without value however, aside from the straight line results of the poll, they can tell us a lot about the mood of a past society based on the poll questions. For example, a future study on our society will be able to discern that abortion was a major topic for our time based on the amount of polls on the subject.

Speaking of abortion, Leslie Reagan’s “When Abortion was a Crime” taught us the value of testimony(see my 2/5/11 post). In “Reading Primary Sources,” the Testimony section was written by Devin Pendas who said, “testimony can answer some questions very well and others not at all.” That is because trial testimony by design tends to focus on a specific and narrow topic, like an alleged crime. That is what made Leslie Reagan’s research so impressive because she gained knowledge based on the unspoken in the testimony. Pendas demonstrates the difficulty in “who to believe” in opposing testimonies, with Nazi guard/Holocaust survivor testimonies. Very compelling testimony and the truth is not as apparent as one might think.  I highly recommend the chapter on testimony. This is a book I will keep and use in the future.  More next week, if I survive my mother-in-laws five day visit this week.
