The Truth

Posted: Tue, 01 Mar 2011

       Telling the Truth about History imbues upon its readers an understanding of the development of history as a discipline, investigating the various ways in which historians have traditionally surveyed the past.  It seems that the early history of history itself was restricted in scope by non-conformative absolutisms.  At first, most intellectual pursuits were performed in light of religion, as most correlated religion with the be-all, end-all truth.  During the 18th century and beginning within the framework of the Enlightenment, the authors recognize the advent of science as the pedestal for which all truths were founded.  Unfortunately, the enlightened enemies of religious absolutism created an absolutism of their own, one based around this scientific history and subsequent modernity.  In this methodological absolutism, scientists wielded a large bastion of power as they were thought of as those closest in contact with the objective truths of reality (grand schemas organizing all knowledge into a uniform body).
       After the devastation of World War II and the advent of the atomic bomb, science lost most of its glamour.  How was science supposed to be value-free, neutral, and a path leading to a greater understanding of humanity, when it had invariably led to such destruction?  From this starting point, opponents of modernity began their assault against it.  The heavy critique of modernity that has developed in the last half century has been the focus of ‘postmodernists’.  According to the author, postmodernism’s “primary goal has been to challenge convictions about objectivity of knowledge and the stability of language” (pg. 201).  As it were, postmodernists challenge the idea that an all-encompassing, objective truth can be accessible, at least not one which would create overlaying organizational schemas of knowledge.  I didn’t fully understand the postmodern convictions against language as well as its convictions against objectivity.  I think postmodernists hold that words don’t correspond to reality, only other words, insofar that reality is simply the creature of language?
       In the end, I feel like the authors make a convincing case for practical pragmatism and against all-out relativism.  In essence, it is essential for historians to recognize and move beyond these constricting methodologies of interpreting the past.  Additionally, the historian must recognize his/her own limitations in grasping only a part of the actual historical truth.  Yet, this relativistic view does not necessarily spell doom for the historian in his/her search for objectivity.  In fact, a grander and more objective reality can be ascertained primarily through the mingling of various interpretations originating from differing perspectives.  Within an ever-changing democratic society, it is crucial for all knowledge to flow interchangeably between society and individual - hopefully establishing the objective truths all historians seek.
