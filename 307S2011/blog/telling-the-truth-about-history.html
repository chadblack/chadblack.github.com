Telling the Truth About History

Posted: Tue, 01 Mar 2011

First off, this book was written by three women and not one man. Now that this tricky piece of information is out of the way, the piece can be discussed. The concept this book addresses seems to be the influence of certain periods upon historiography and how the ideals developed are eventually toppled and replaced. The first example of this progression in the dismissal of theological reasoning with the rise of the Enlightenment, and secularism pervaded the realm of academia. As national identities began to take root and influence history, they were then stripped down through the examination of various historical accounts of a nation. Science was then removed from its pedestal after the examination of social conditioning upon scientific research i.e. the influence of newly developed social theory upon Darwin’s theory of natural selection. The next chapter deals with the postmodernist’s attack upon the narrative of history and the individual in general. In all honesty, the subject of postmodernity piques my intellectual interests, especially regarding the prison of language, but its implications are not the most appealing. The authors come to the conclusion that the gap between reality and its narration does not render history and an individual’s narration of it unsound, rather they urge the formation of new historical methods for the future, which clearly applies to our position as students of history.
