Historiography 1

Posted: Sat, 22 Jan 2011
Historiography I

Leopold von Ranke stated, “the purpose of history was to clarify our notion of the origin and nature of the state.” What are the roles of the writers of history, historians? What makes them qualified to do this?

These questions were all answered by the reading this week and through our class time. Historians are not supposed to just explain history but interpret it. What this actually means has evolved since the professions birth in the nineteenth century. One evolution that has made an extreme impact on myself is what James Harvey Robinson describes. He gives a very good descriptive analysis of the shift in the history field from what we described in class as the Great Man History to wanting to learn about the everyman and how he lived his life. He also describes the shift from the interest in the sensational to the interest in the normal.

William Cronon, in his essay listed the qualifications that make up the profession and then presented his own thoughts on what should make up the training to be admitted to the historical guild. What makes Cronon’s thoughts interesting and challenging are not the list of qualifications but his own ideas. For myself they challenged my own ideas of training I will need. Cronon specifically talked about the Public History field, which is what I want to go into. He challenged the divergence that many public historians feel separates them from academia. He believes that public historians could have an academic training to understand the beginnings of the history in which they are presenting.

One topic that was discussed in a majority of the essays was the role of bias in history and accepting the natural bias that occurs in the writing of history. For me the bias of history is what makes the profession interesting and fresh. It is what keeps the profession going and limitless. Topics such as the Civil War and World War II are written about over and over again. But, what makes each work refreshing is the bias that occurs which makes it different from all works that came before it. Historians then analyze these works and write their own that will be analyzed in the future.

The key part of this is originality for the historians to keep asking new questions and interpreting in new ways. As William Cronon states, “Interpretation is as important as explanation.” Historians can all explain the events that they study. But, what sets them apart is how they analyze them and present it to the reader. Out of this interpretation questions are asked such as: “What is the larger picture?” or “Why is this important?” We also learn about the historian’s own time from their writings. For example we were able to learn about the nineteenth century and the moral codes of the time through Lord Acton’s writings.

As Carl Becker states, “History is the memory of things said and done.”
