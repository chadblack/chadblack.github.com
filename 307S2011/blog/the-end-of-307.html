The End of 307

Posted: Sun, 24 Apr 2011

          It’s really hard to believe that this is our last blog and more importantly our last week of 307.  Looking back at the schedule of readings, William Cronon’s “Getting Ready to Do History” from week 2 jumped out at me.  14 weeks separated, are we actually ready to DO history?  Will we be able to amalgamate occasionally problematic sources into an appropriate interpretative framework, using the appropriate methodology?  Is History writing even an approachable endeavor in the post-linguistic turn world?
           I can’t say I wasn’t anxious coming into 307 and I can’t honestly say I’m not anxious moving forward into 407.  Yet, that anxiety is now mixed with a comforting sense of confidence.  I know how to discover the sources, I know how to critically analyze them, and I know how to transform these objects of research into the thesis of an argument.  The only thing left to do is to do the history itself: enmesh myself in the documents of yesterday and hope that I can resurface on the other side with a provocative and objective work of historical scholarship.
          Reading the Yale Senior Essay Handbook definitely made me consider the prospects of devoting my entire senior year to the likes of a 50-page essay.  Surely there will be countless hours of time devoted to both research and writing over the coming year.  However, as the Handbook insinuated, I feel similarly that the senior paper will be a crowning culmination of my four years at the University of Tennessee.  There were a couple of things outlined by the Senior Essay Handbook that I liked in particular.  First of all, I like the idea of having a rigid timetable to work with.  The step-to-step process offered by Yale, wherein one moves from a research plan to a prospectus to a draft to a final product, helps me develop a methodology that works with my schedule, based on the knowledge of forthcoming due dates.  I was also attracted to the proposed idea of writing the research paper in small subsections.  Normally when I sit down to write out any sort of essay I first start by writing a single sentence I’d like to incorporate in the finished product.  The paper comes together from this starting point as I extrapolate outward and build the whole out of various pieces.  In many ways this approach is similar to dividing the paper into perhaps 10, 5-page papers, but simply on a smaller scale.  Sometimes I do feel like the strategy hurts the narrative flow of the paper, yet usually this can be counter-balanced by appropriate transitions.
