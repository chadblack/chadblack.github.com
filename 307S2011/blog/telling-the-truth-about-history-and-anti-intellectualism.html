Telling the Truth about History and Anti-Intellectualism

Posted: Sun, 27 Feb 2011

Telling the Truth about History was an interesting read. I found the discussion on relativism particularly interesting. The authors’ accurately portrayed how relativism can lead to form of nihilism with regard to history. The fear that history is polluted by relativism leads to a rejection of all histories, no matter how sound. It has become very prevalent lately for groups to deny the facts behind sound historical research because they do not agree with it or it makes them uncomfortable.  This form of anti-intellectualism is especially prevalent among the tea-party crowd who has based their movement on a historical event and a belief in the sanctity of the founding fathers. Tea-Party star Congresswoman Michele Bachmann, (R-MN), recently said, “But we also know that the very founders that wrote those documents worked tirelessly until slavery was no more in the United States, men like John Quincy Adams… would not rest until slavery was extinguished in the country.” I do not need to explain to history majors what is wrong with this statement, but this gross re-writing of history for political gain is very dangerous, particularly when historians who point out the errors are derided for being part of the liberal elite, influenced by relativism. You see that this is the showstopper, ending all debate. I think that rejection is a form of nihilism. Those ivory tower elites, Appleby, Hunt and Jacob eloquently wrote in History, “We are emphasizing the human need for self understanding through a coherent narrative of the past and the need for admittedly partial, objective explanations of how the past has worked.” And, “We are highlighting the need for the most objective possible explanations as the only way to move forward, perhaps not on a straight line of progress, but forward toward a more intellectually alive, democratic community, toward the society in which we would like to live.”[1] When they say to move forward they are referring to getting beyond the intellectual absolutist methods of heroic science, progressivism, and nationalism.  In the next to the last chapter they lay out how they believe we should proceed, with methods and models like practical realism, working with science (in a non-heroic model), new theory of objectivity, psychological dynamics of knowing, narratives and languages, and the textuality of texts. These models and methods are well developed theories based on the mistakes of our predecessors and give us a good chance to get it as right as we go forward.  It is more important than ever to produce the best product we can in the face of a growing wave of anti-intellectualism, to prevent any crack in our armor for the extremist elements (left or right) in our society to exploit. If not, before too long we might hear that George Washington defeated the British with an army of archangels and was able to shoot lightning bolts out of his ass (forgive the Braveheart reference.)

[1] Joyce Appleby, Lynn Hunt, and Margaret Jacob, Telling the Truth about History (New York: Norton, 1994), 229.
