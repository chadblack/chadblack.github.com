Week #6 with a blog

Posted on March 20th, 2010 by joh307

After reading the previous blogs on the course site and given our discussion in class, I’ll split this post up into two parts.  The first being the required topic, the dichotomies, and the second part being the student apathy here at Tennessee.  So this post will probably be very long. 

The first dichotomy is synchronic (single point in time) and diachronic (change over time).  While at first it seems it would be diachronic since it covers over 100 years, the article is really synchronic.  It examines the Seville orphanages during the long century and it really isn’t examing the changes from the start of the Long Century to its conclusion.  It is just identifying what life was like, how they were viewed, and other things at that point in time.  There is some examinations of changes during the Long Century like the new requirement for documents to prove high birth in 1721 but to view it as a comparison to the earlier part of the Long Century would be to miss the point of the article.  Next is emic (immersion) and etic (external).  This article seems to be emic because it really is a close look inside the culture of Seville.  When reading about the orphanages, we get accounts from former residents, administrators, and others offering their opinions on what life was like and how the orphanges operated.  Third comes particularist (one case) and nomothetic (several cases).  This article seems like the answer is both (since only Seville is studied) but going off of the author’s stated intentions, this article would have to be nomothetic.  It may only examine Seville’s orphanages but the author wants to use the data to illustrate the connections between children and their cultural settings in Spain as a whole.  Next is quanitative (measurable) and qualitative (not measurable).  This article is qualitative because numbers and statistics weren’t really used and the author states that a quanitative analysis wasn’t used either. The fifth dichotmy is personal influence and social forces.  It is primarily social forces as the article examines how orphanges were viewed and used in Seville and why they became such a vital thing for the citizens.  The next set includes ideationalist and materialist.  Considering it talks about the benefits of the orphanges such as an education and training to become a naval officer as well as the lengths people would go to get children into these orphanges, it would be a more materlist focus.  For conflict and consensus, I do not know which one would fit.  There was conflict between those of lower birth with the orphanage administrators because they wanted high birth children (as requiring documention illustrates) but there was also consensus over how useful and beneficial these orphanges were as many tried to get their children or relatives into them.  Finally the last dichotomy was determinist and stochastic-ist.  I’ll go with determinist because the social class and status of parents/relatives played the major role in determining selection into an orphanage. 

While I had started to type a big thing on student apathy here, I think I will have a much better idea of how bad it is after this week’s SGA elections and could give a better post then.  Anyway, there’s one party, starts with ‘F’, that is all incumbent and is fully intent on doing nothing for the students, continuing the SGA tradition.  The other party, starts with ‘T’, actually has people who want to make a difference and fight budget cuts and work to help the students.  I was told how during an SGA meeting last week with the Chancellor, only one bothered to ask about budget cuts (he is in ‘T’).  SGA has never been about serving the students and this year will be the election that could change it because it truly is an election between the incumbents and anti-incumbents.  SGA is needed to facilitate the change in student apathy.  If the SGA actually starts fighting for the students, that would certainly have a beneficial result for students as a whole.  I know as a freshman how disgusted I was at the SGA stabbing the student population in the back over the grading scale change.  Of course it looked much worse to everyone when the SGA executives who did the stabbing got rewarded with the Torchbearer Awards.  Reforming SGA is a necessary step into getting students here to care because it is the students’ voice.  If it is not speaking for the students, it is understandable why papathy would be so common.  I will do my part next year.  I joined to do something for the students because I was doubtful if anyone else would.  As it is now, who actually knows the name of their SGA senators?
