Week 10

Posted on March 21st, 2010 by Jed

As the authors of The Emperor’s Mirror state before dividing approaches into dichotomies, “a scholar often can choose an intermediate position between two extremes” (16). This is often the case when categorizing  “Not All the Orphans Really Are” by Valentina Tikoff using the categories in the “Conceptual Building Blocks” section of The Emperor’s Mirror.
The article defies easy placement in four approaches. The article investigates the long 18th century in Spain’s history. Many aspects of Spanish society must have changed in those 150 years. Such a sprawling mass of time seemingly points to categorization as ‘diachronic’.  Yet for the institutions handling children in Seville, it was fundamentally a single period dominated by a single structure which transformed at the beginning and end of the period. In this sense it is synchronic. It paints the picture of a ‘single’ time. Similarly, the article fits clearly into neither the particularist nor the nomothetic category. It concentrates on Seville. This is a defining quality of the particularist approach. However, it makes broad claims about Spain which should apply to other cases. This is a defining quality of the nomothetic. The article is further confused in its use of emic and etic terminology. It uses both emic (foundling, orphan, noble) and etic (fragmented, the long 18th century, marginals) terminology to redefine the approach to ‘orphans’ (she argues that we should divide the category as they did for better understanding) through etic frameworks. Finally, it emphasizes social forces, such as institutions and categories of thought, rather than personal influences. However, it investigates these through some particular cases of individuals and institutions.
In two other approaches it more clearly works within categories. It is largely qualitative rather than quantitative although she identifies patterns. It is also multi-factor rather than single-factor, taking into account multiple actors, institutions, and social forces.
In its assumptions it again defies the categories provided in The Emperor’s Mirror. It is to some degree both ideationalist and materialist. For example, the categories ‘foundling’ and ‘orphan’ are clearly evidence of ideas shaping reality while the actions of parents on behalf of their children’s welfare suggest a materialist bent. Similarly, both consensus and conflict operate within Seville’s long 18th century history of children. For example, the relative stability of the institutional system itself is evidence of consensus and cooperation. Yet, these institutions were not used in a way that their founders had intended, revealing conflict. Finally, she is neither a clear determinist nor a clear stochastic. She proposes trends in behavior and general understandings of categories but not unbreakable laws for either.
Thus, these categories provide a way to understand more deeply how the article functions and how it argues. They also show the complexity of Tikoff’s approach. However, the article reveals the categories’ limits. They are only approaches to analysis, not boxes to check.
