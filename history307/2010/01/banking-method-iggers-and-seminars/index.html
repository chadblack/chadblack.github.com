Banking Method, Iggers, and Seminars

Posted on January 23rd, 2010 by joh307

The readings for the past class I found to be very interesting and sort of changed my thinking in relation to my classes, maybe not so much in how I understand what is taught in them but how I see each class in general.  This might seem odd since the readings didn’t seem to have anything groundbreaking but in addition to be a History major, I am also an Accounting major so talking about the banking method of teaching for classes is more relevant to me.

Reading Freire’s defintion of the banking method that the teacher deposits information and “the students patiently receive, memorize, and repeat” is pretty much a great description of my business classes that I take.  When I am taught a way to solve a particular accounting problem, I memorize the steps and repeat them as necessary for all future problems without really thinking why that is the best way or even why it works.  Just knowing that it works is good enough for me.  It’s like that in other business subjects, if there is a problem X, then use solution Y or maybe solution Z.  Of course, the banking method does sort of work in the case of Accounting in that the stuff I learn now is supposed to help me down the road with the CPA exam, which is professional certification.  But that even is an example of just studying only the things on the test and doesn’t help me with things that won’t be on it.

The real problem with the banking concept is not necessarily how students learn but that it creates a check-mark system which can grow into areas other than just education.  And by check-mark system I mean, whether or not you meet some qualification or have some basic ability to do something.  I have experienced that a lot especially when interacting with Accounting firms.  Every firm that I have talked to has mentioned that I need to be a member of Accounting society because that shows them I am well rounded.  Every time I hear that I cringe because being a member doesn’t necessarily mean anything other than fulfilling the basic requirements of going to the Wednesday meeting and proctoring certain exams.  That example goes along well with the discussion in class about the expectations associated with college and how it is assumed you can do certain things while those who don’t go to college cannot.

These assumptions can tie into the Iggers discussion about Ranke.  He explained that Ranke valued objectivity extremely high and said it was essential.  But in addition to that, he showed how Ranke viewed events and people in vacuums.  In doing so he neglected to show an interest in the movements and thinking of the time period he was studying.  He missed possible answers of why someone acted the way they did or what underlying set of beliefs led them.  Like Ranke, the check-mark system can have a similar effect when looking at job applicants because by emphasizing a narrow list of things, you can easily miss out on important and significant experience or activities someone may have that would bolster their credentials and make them a far better fit for the job.

Finally with Kahn’s statements, it really is difficult in my experience to have a true barn-raising seminar.  Too often it starts out as a free-for-all where a select few care and strive to outdo each other while the others just don’t care.  That leads to what I think is the biggest problem with seminars and that is the reputation they have developed.  The reputation of being a free-for-all or for show-offs only is the problem inherent in them.  When a large chunk of the students don’t care or don’t want to be embarrassed by someone telling them they are stupid, it becomes difficult to have an enjoyable, enriching seminar.  When the students go in there looking to learn and develop an idea they have, it can be a real success.  In order to have a barn raised, everyone needs to lift or else the barn may never get built.
