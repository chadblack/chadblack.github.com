Bias and Reality: Week 2

Posted on January 31st, 2010 by kat307

The opening question in my notes reads as follows: “If you change language, do you change reality?” The simple answer, as we discussed in class, is no. You can call mercury go-go juice and die from drinking it. You can call something “non-consensual sex” and it still equates to rape, even as some terms may have very different connotations from their synonymous counterparts. However, our words and our language do have a lot more power than we would readily admit at times, which fits neatly in with the discussions of objectivity and known bias tackled later in the discussion.

My church bulletin contains a small box reading “Because words shape our thinking, we strive to use inclusive language in our worship.” In the case of my church, this often involves using non-gendered terms in hymns and sermons. But the power of language extends far beyond the Presbyterian Hymnal and well into the public sphere. When we discussed media and bias, we didn’t return to the conversation of language, but perhaps we should have. The words chosen by a particular writer or media personality (Nancy Grace, anyone?) are designed to elicit certain reactions from an audience. It’s all in the spin, and some of the spin is the phraseology. For example, referring to a prisoner as a “cop-killer in police custody” carries very different connotations than those of “person of interest held in connection with the crime.”

This can equally be applied to the writing of history. As we discussed bias, someone 9and unfortunately I can’t remember who) said that we should “use methods geared toward objectivity, even knowing it might not be achieved.” This was expanded in our list to regarding objectivity as a goal, with caveats of recognized biases in a format as accurate as possible.

Language can reveal our biases, and in the context of history, while it might not change reality, it can certainly change the perception of reality. We also addressed the quote “History is written by the winners.” This always reminds me of differing treatments of the Cold War in American and Soviet textbooks. Particularly when we study more distant events, we rely heavily on few first-hand accounts, limiting our perceptions and knowledge of the event and doubtless affecting our understanding of the period in question.

We also discussed whether it might be better to have a primary source with known biases, so as to better counter the biases in the interpretation, and this is another instance in which language and word choice can help us to better understand available information. As we make a “best faith effort,” language is an important factor to consider and to weigh in with the rest of our evidence.
