Week #3

Posted on February 5th, 2010 by joh307

Before analyzing Historians in Trouble through the “Statement on Standards of Professional Conduct,” I would like to talk about something that I found interesting that we didn’t spend too much time in class on and that is the case of Dino Cinel.  The case shows the danger of giving tenure to a person in order to convince them to take the job.  Tenure shouldn’t be something to be given out freely to entice someone to take a job.  Tenure is something that should be earned since it is so powerful.  While I personally do not like how tenure can keep someone in a job a long time after being discredited, as this case was an example of, I do like the practice in general of requiring a book to be published and other requirements as we discussed in class.  That at least can help weed out some of the more ethically challenged professors which certainly is a good thing. 

Moving on to the “Statement on Standards of Professional Conduct,” it really is quite amazing how the historians broke all these different rules.  It seems that once you get to a certain level, you are more likely to stop paying attention to the importance of professional conduct and start looking out for how to best help yourself.  At least most historians haven’t fallen into this problem.  But having already learned about what standards these historians have broken, I think it would be more interesting to look at Jon Wiener and his book through the “Statement on Standards of Professional Conduct.”  First, Wiener does a very good job at thanking people for their help in each of his chapters in the Acknowledgements section as the Statement suggests, “They [Historians] should always acknowledge assistance received from colleagues…and others, and give due credit to collaborators.”  He also does a good job at interpreting the past actions of historians and those actions meant by first asking questions about possible meanings and then choosing the likely one. 

Although he wasn’t perfect in his adherence to the Statement.  A glaring problem, that was mentioned several times during class, was his bias in choosing the stories to write about.  He chose stories that really put the right-wing in a bad light and made it seem like they always liked to turn minor and trivial errors into huge political firestorms while also blaming a vast left-wing conspiracies when they get busted for harrassment or unethical behavior.  While these things certainly are good examples of historians in trouble, it seemed too slanted because the left wing certianly has similar issues, maybe not as much but still do and a story or two would have made Wiener’s book far better.  As the Statement says, “Professional integrity in the practice of history requires awareness of one’s own biases…”  Reading the book, it was clear where Wiener was coming from but it still would have been nice to have him explicitly state  it. 

Also, during his chapter on Arming America, I found it a little problematic that he left out what Bellesiles said in his introduction, other than saying it was “a fierce attack on Charleton Heston and the NRA.”  He noted right after that the NRA was targeting him before the book came out but it had to have known about the introduction in addition to what was in the book.  Wiener should have mentioned what was specifically in the introduction instead of glossing over it and acting like it played a very little role in what followed.  It seems likely that an introduction attacking the NRA and Heston would have at least some effect on their actions and probably contributed to them trying to find any mistake to discredit him.  Even if the introduction played no role in the end, Wiener still should have mentioned that instead of leaving it up to the readers to come to that conclusion because it doesn’t seem so clearcut with how Wiener wrote the chapter.
