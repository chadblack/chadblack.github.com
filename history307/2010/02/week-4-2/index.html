Week #4

Posted on February 14th, 2010 by joh307

The two works we read and discussed in class, other than the Presnell book, this past week were on the topic of illegal abortion and the new left.  On the surface those don’t seem to be very similar in any way but after reading about them, they have several similarities in the method of research and investigation.  Both of the topics contain very large issues and the question that both historians had when starting their research must have been what would be the best way to go about it.  Should they take data and information from all over the country or should they focus on one area as sort of a case study?  Well both took an area as a case study, for Reagan it was Chicago and for Rossinow it was UT-Austin, and it was successful from what we read.  The reasoning for picking a small area to focus the research is that that particular area is representative of the nation as a whole and as Reagan points out, “though much of my analysis of the history of abortion draws on Chicago as a case study, sources verify that my findings hold true for other cities, large and small.”  Focusing on a small area to research and investigate enables the historian to get more detailed and specific and instead of skipping over significant local events to focus on national ones, events that could have gone unnoticed are giving a thorough investigation like the Chicago Times investigation or the CFLC at UT-Austin.  However, by focusing on a certain area, you can be at the mercy of the place in terms of what research you will find.  In Reagan’s case, only death records and personal stories were available to use to illustrate that use of abortion far more common than believed.  Fortunately for her, those sources did work and supported her case but they may not always.  As for Rossinow, it seemed that occasionally, he would start talking about the minutia of the CFLC which, while can help his explanation about the New Left at UT-Austin, makes the reader wonder just how well it could be expanded to a national level or if it really is relevant to even just UT-Austin.  Although it should be noted that some details that may seem trivial and unnecessary could become better explained later in the book, like the debates between Joe Matthews and John Silber on Waiting for Godot, for example.

Another similarity each shares is the usefulness of the introduction.  It is an essential part for any historical work because it is a skeleton of the work, explains the methods of investigation, contains the thesis, and many other things as well.  The skeleton tells the reader what to expect and what to look out for.  Meanwhile the explanation of the methods of research is needed to understand how the research was acquired and why that way was the best way.  Reagan’s reasoning for choosing Chicago, which was mentioned earlier, was located in the introduction.  And most importantly, the thesis is required in order to know what the historian is trying to show/prove.  Each introduction we read did a very good job at doing all of those things and even though we only read the first two chapters of the book, by reading the introductions, what we read was better put into context and we have at least a general idea of what followed later in the book.
