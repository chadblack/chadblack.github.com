Week #5

Posted on February 20th, 2010 by joh307

This past week we read two journal articles, one by Kimberly Gauderman about the role of women in 17th Century Quito and one by EP Thompson about food riots in 18th Century England.  Both were good examples of what a journal article should be, in how it’s structured, researched, and the topic.  However, as someone who has just started getting a good understanding of journal articles and historical works in general, I felt that Gauderman’s article was a better example of what is needed and how it should be done.  First, I think it is necessary to point out that since it is shorter, it does help someone who is new to what we’ve been doing in class the past few weeks because everything is more compact and it helps transitions and the thesis statement, for example, more visible and easier to pick out.  I think it’s important to understand how to work with the smaller articles before you move onto the bigger ones.

Nevertheless, Gauderman’s article was very useful in gaining an understanding about how journal articles work.  The first section was the introduction which explained the decentralization of colonial Spanish society and why that was important for her work.  In the second paragraph, she states the thesis and research methods and then proceeds to give the outline of her article which, as we discussed a week and a half ago, are the same things you would find in an introduction to a book.  Essentially a book or journal article have the same format, just differing lengths which is why understanding this article can be so useful in reading other historical works, you know where and what to look for.   Anyway, the book then starts its transition into the main part of the work, moving to a narrower topic each time.  It starts with women’s economic roles in spanish culture, then the economy of Quito, and then her research into Quito’s textile industry.  The first two topics provide background and give context on why women could have an economic impact and why studying Quito would be better than perhaps other locations.  After those topics, we know spanish women could succeed because of the colonies’ decentralization and that Quito was a stable, prosperous textile town and not an unstable mineral town.  Now we can move into the crux of the argument by reading the stories of various women in the textile industry, showing that women did have an economic impact in Quito through the textile industry while also detailing other information like the horrible conditions for the indigenous workers.  She then wraps everything up with the conclusion at the end.  Gauderman’s article did a great job at showing the necessary features of any historical work and her article in particular is a good starting point for anyone learning about research and how to properly read a work of history.  It certainly will help reading longer articles and books.
