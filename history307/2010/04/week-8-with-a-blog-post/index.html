Week 8 with a blog post

Posted on April 3rd, 2010 by joh307

The failure and limitations of language that we discussed during last class is something that in normal, everyday life occurs more than most realize.  Saying the opposite of what you actually mean to someone you just met, like the Aztecs did with Cortes, seems absurd now but it isn’t really different from saying things either jokingly or sarcastically to someone whom you just met too.  They likely will see you as serious unless you tell them otherwise after the fact.  You act normally until you realize that doing so creates problems.  I know when I came here, jokes and comments I made were those I made back home but here people didn’t understand what I meant so I dropped doing them altogether rather than explaining the joke, thus ruining it.  You can’t expect everyone to know what you mean and that fact  is sometimes slow to be realized.  Being on the other end of those kinds of failed jokes can be even worse if the person blames you for not getting it or understanding it was a joke in the first place.  Someone that I barely know is someone whom I will take at face value, unless it is clear that the person isn’t being serious.  But if something is said with a straight face, you can’t assume that the person is joking or being ironic.  The limitations of language is also something that creeps into conversations far more than is realized.  Just thinking about stories I have told, there have been numerous examples of explaining something in terms that helps the person listening better understand.  I may say “sort of like the lecture hall underneath the McClung Museum” whenever I talk about being in an underground auditorium/lecture hall somewhere other than UTK.  Describing what the place actually looked like probably isn’t going to be nearly as useful to the listener than by just saying it’s like a place the listener knows.  I know listening to someone’s story, if I can’t visualize it or have some reference point, it’s going to be harder to understand just exactly what was going on or what it was like.  Just as in good writing, when speaking, you need to know your audience.  Otherwise, people can get the wrong impression and conclude things you didn’t actually mean.  That isn’t helpful to anyone.
