Poor players in the historiosphere

Posted on February 7th, 2010 by iloope

An idea that struck a chord as I read through the America Historical Association’s Statement on Standards of Professional Conduct and reflected on several other readings that we’ve encountered thus far in the semester: history as community. Shared ideas and principles that dictate the professionalism or truth of the subject. This idea implies that history is a discipline that is subject to the rule of law within that community and as we read this week members of that community, even distinguished members, consciously and unconsciously manipulate the values that all peoples of historical academia should comply.

During this weeks class discussion, one in which I think a lot of us really enjoyed, Jon Wiener’s Historians in Trouble focused on unprofessional malfeasance inside the realm of history. Parts of the book read like a poor attempt by some bad apples in the profession to exercise their value deficiencies as a power display. These members of the neo-con thought police unscrupulously used their expertise and connections to not only avoid due punishment, but were given prestige by power political forces in the face of obvious wrong doing. While other parts of the book seemed as if the individuals involved were less concerned about the truth of the historical record and more concerned with falsifying facts to support their bias beliefs.

One area that we touched on in class that the book whispered about was the lack of administration oversight in dealing with the problems of wrong doing. While not specifically dealt with in Wiener’s book the idea that administrations will harbor individuals not only accused of academic fraud, but the lack of concern for personal and professional protection some administrations displayed towards their student body. In an academic world fueled by money from special interest groups there’s little wonder of why administrations seemly turn a blind-eye towards the people involved in these scandals. Money vs. truth. Sadly truth pays the tab. “Integrity of the historical record” words to live by not words to manipulate. The historians in Weiner’s book lost focus of this pivotal reality. Every wrong actions can call into question the “integrity of the historical” record by no longer placing the focus on productive scholarship and healthy academic debate, but a reliance on the prestige of an individuals or an institution to clear-up wrong doing and cases that we saw simply swept the dirt under the rug without giving a second thought. What’s nice about real history is that these wrong doings are on record so that in future instances of academic fraud or unprofessional behavior, the community can and should take swift actions as to avoid these instances that call integrity into question.

Week #3

Posted on February 6th, 2010 by Jed

A central theme of Historians in Trouble is the implicit hypocrisy of people involved in writing history. Many of these historians behave unprofessionally insofar as the AHA guidelines articulate what historical professionalism entails. Chief among these unprofessional professionals is Allen Weinstein for the variety of his offenses over such a long span of time. Throughout the process of debate over his work, and even before the debate began, he flouted various key principles, as Jon Wiener demonstrates. During the process of research he violated the principle of “free, open, equal, and nondiscriminatory access” (AHA Statement on Standards of Professional Conduct) in gaining access to Soviet archives. After he came under attack, he never gave others access to his sources, dishonoring the historical record by intentionally obscuring a potentially “clear trail for subsequent historians to follow” (AHA). Yet his long record of errors is not merely a collection of mistakes. They are evidence of a general attitude. He is hostile to open debate. He does not accept that “historians often disagree and argue with each other…[that] interpretive disagreements are vital to the creative ferment of our profession, and can in fact contribute to come of our most original and valuable insights…that the resulting disagreements can deepen and enrich historical understanding by generating new questions, new arguments, and new lines of investigation” (AHA). He wanted to win, and end, the debate. Perhaps more than politics, this is the important similarity between Weinstein and the Bush administration: a closed approach to information. Of course, this is why his appointment as archivist was so startling.
However, if part of what makes this hostility so reprehensible is his supposed adherence to a professional code of conduct, making him a hypocrite, then the American Historical Association itself is worthy of even more reproach than Jon Wiener gives. In their Statement on Standards, they write of two lines of defense against plagiarism. First, scholars must cultivate tidy work habits. The second line, though, “is organized and punitive. Every institution that includes or represents a body of scholars has an obligation to establish procedures designed to clarify and uphold their ethical standards” (AHA). Although they had a department for handling scholarly abuses, they often used it to brush Wiener’s complaints to a corner. If “the real penalty for plagiarism is the abhorrence of the community of scholars,” they need to bring these cases of dishonesty into the light as soon as possible. Furthermore, their abandonment of attempts to police scholars, mentioned in the conclusion of Historians in Trouble, takes both ethical and procedural clarity from the attempt to uphold historians’ standards. This was what most surprised me in Weiner’s book: not right-wing organizations’ persistence, but the degree of apathy in the AHA and among so many historians toward clear profession hostility to the principles of open debate and truth-seeking.

The American Historical Association issued a statement on standards of professional conduct in order to address some issues facing the historical community and provide a framework to provide “honor in their work.”  The standards state the “trust and respect of one’s peers and of the public at large are among the greatest and most-hard won achievements that any historian can attain.”  This trust is easily broken when one’s work is found to be full of plagiarism and can harm, if not destroy the person’s career.

Stephen Ambrose’s career and reputation have taken a large hit due to plagiarism complaints against him.  His book “The Wild Blue” contained nearly exact statements to Thomas Childers’s book “The Wings of Morning.”  This lead to further investigation and it was found that this was not his first, nor his last plagiarized work.  The AHA characterizes plagiarism as “the expropriation of another author’s work, and the presentation of it as one’s own…”  The best solution to this problem is to be as thorough as possible and cite everything.  Something I failed to consider about sources and footnotes was the fact that it aids future historians and researchers to continue on and further develop your theories or ideas.

A case that stood out to me from the reading was the Allen Weinstein case.  His first transgression was he paid former KGB agents for “exclusive” rights and access to the archives of the KGB.  This action was a clear violation of the “integrity of the profession” as the Standards call it.  Then to further tarnish his image Weinstein went to withhold the documents from allowing other historians to have access, as he promised.  The standards set by the AHA state that historians favor “free, open, equal, and nondiscriminatory access,” to archives.  Weinstein was further confronted with Navasky who tracked down his sources and sent them copies of the portions of their input that were used in the book.  All of the sources agreed that they were misrepresented or misquoted in some way (pg.40).

Jon Wiener’s, “Historians in Trouble” provided great insight into the mistakes that historians fall into and the damage that can be caused to one’s career.  The AHA’s standards set in place are strong on the integrity of one’s work. Historians face constant scrutiny anytime they uncover something is potentially controversial, it is vital the we have all the bases covered as historians in order to avoid embarrassing shame for failing to follow these standards.

week 3: Historians in Trouble

Posted on February 5th, 2010 by Andrew

This week we read over Jon Wiener’s Historians in Trouble. As the title implies, the book deals with professional historians who get into controversy and scandal. In class we discussed such controversies, mistakes, and scandals surrounding such historians. The worst included cases of outright plagiarism and sexual/administrative abuse. Others were undeserving of the treatment they received.

In all the book by Wiener shows that the academic profession of history is not an impregnable ivory tower separate from the rest of the world’s problems, biases, and shortcomings. It is indulged in the same competition, marketplace, power struggles, and sinful shortfalls as the rest of humanity. Many of the examples were obvious ones and the unjust persecution of some historians showed how if you piss off the wrong people (who have power) you can find yourself without a job, academically shunned, and even under death threats.

The most practical lesson to learn from it (besides the above “don’t piss off the wrong people”) is that you should make sure you footnote everything, give credit when its due (quotation marks, etc.), and make sure your interpretation or translation of a document is correct. As Wiener quotes a source, being a historian working in an archive all day can make you rush and scribble down inaccurate notes which can alter your findings. Even if the error is small, as shown in Wiener’s book, you might lose your job because you pissed off someone with your findings/interpretation and they in turn hound you, the academic world, and your university over those few errors as if you were guilty of murder or blasphemy of some Biblical scale.

Second Week

Posted on February 3rd, 2010 by afieldsutk

So I totally forgot about writing a blog post last week (I don’t have internet at home so I have to plan time at the library). So this post is for last week’s readings/discussion, and my third week post will follow soon.

I want to comment, as some other folks did, on the question of language making reality. I have a second major in philosophy (I like history better!) and have spent a good deal of time on post-structuralist (or postmodernist) thought, and this idea has always been one of my favorite things about the post-structuralists, especially Foucault and Derrida. Essentially, I agree with the thesis that ‘reality’ is constructed, or at least constrained, by language. I consider the only meaningful uses of the words ‘truth’ and ‘reality’ to be in reference to sentient (not necessarily human) experience–which is not to say that nothing would exist without that experience, only that we can’t meaningfully talk or think about what it means for something to exist outside of experience. Foucault, who despite his criticisms of the possibility of doing history is in many ways an historian himself, deals with this in a really interesting way in writing the history of ideas (for example in his book The Order of Things) where, instead of treating intellectual history as a neutral battlefield for rational arguments and events which evolve over time, he writes history as consisting of a series of epochs in which the “conditions of the possibility of knowledge” (essentially, the language and, hence, the intellectual frameworks that are available to humans) differ in ways that constrain all areas of human thought in particular ways. Our own time is no exception, although Foucault’s histories all conveniently end before his own lifetime.

It’s interesting, and maybe a little bit scary, to think about how this applies to history. The Iggers book describes several epochs of thought in historical scholarship, each with its own ideas about the proper methods, subjects, and styles of writing appropriate to history. Like Foucault, Iggers is much more careful about making sweeping claims about our own time, but if all other periods of historical writing (and human intellectual history generally) can be describes in terms of constraining frameworks, why not ours? It’s the conceit of every generation to believe at least implicitly that all previous generations had fatal flaws but we, at last, have a roughly complete and accurate view of things. Post-structuralism has gone a long way toward forcing us to give up this conceit, but it certainly hasn’t purged the tendency for all of us as individuals to look at things this way.

The upshot is that our writings are not more ‘objective’ or ‘true’ than any of the older works that we’re eager to find fatal flaws in. The question becomes: if not to discover objective truth, what is the purpose of doing history?

Week #2

Posted on February 3rd, 2010 by Jed

The thinkers loosely grouped as the postmodernists challenged all pursuit of knowledge, from literature to physics, as they saw that cultural, social, political, and linguistic forces shaped the process of creating truth. While they critiqued each branch of knowledge, not everyone seemed equally worried. For instance, in the world of literary theory, postmodern and post-structuralist thought shook the discipline at its very foundations. Those who interpreted literature and produced works of critique had to grapple with these concerns before they could continue interpreting and producing. On the other hand, while I’m sure postmodern ideas worried many philosophers of science, the actual pursuit of scientific knowledge seemed to continue without disruption, if the march of the computer is any evidence.
Although one factor that divides these two responses is the distance between theory and practice, another factor is the aim of each. Directly linked to purpose of a discipline is the means of evaluating success. What was the aim of literary criticism? No one knew. Or, everyone knew, but for them. There was no consensus across the discipline. As a result, there was no clear means of evaluating the relative success of a given piece of criticism. Conversely, physics has long had a clear aim: understanding the workings of the matter and its motion. How do they know if they’ve understood? The knowledge is useful. Although there is no unified theory of physics, there have been clear leaps in people’s ability to control matter that testify that they are ‘close enough’ to call it ‘true’ knowledge. Truth seems to be that which is useful to believe is true. Thus, a crucial factor that separates literary studies and physics is utility.
No wonder, then, that history reacted to the postmodern strain of thought in a way that lay in between the two. As we discussed, history contains elements that border on both the literary and the scientific. Its aim is a representation of the past much as the aim of science is a representation of certain phenomena. Yet its means contain large elements of the literary: metaphor, narrative, rhetoric. These were the elements that people can most easily see as evidence of biases. These came under attack. History as literature was shaken.
However, I take heart from Iggers’s note that only Britain and America took the critique seriously. For although the postmodern critique deserved serious thought, it need not have slowed the pace of historical research. It resulted, not in the cessation of historical writing, but in the development of new methods, new ways of understanding language and culture. This was appropriate because history has utility. It might be that it has utility most obviously in sociology or economics, but these are disciplines which could not stand without the study of history. These disciplines know they are finding some degree of ‘truth’ because their conclusions have utility. Thus, insofar as history helps further social sciences with direct use, history has a clear use.
So while the social sciences each have a particular aim, history has no such immediate goal but instead works a step back from the desk of an urban planner or CEO. The result, though, is not a lesser degree of ‘truth’, which would curtail the relevance of historical thought. Instead, the result is a liberation. Historians have a greater freedom in language than the social sciences by virtue of spanning fields, taking on so many questions, and keeping from the fray of memos. Economics needs a particular language because it deals with such particular issues, ones that float at a distance from common use. Historian can use that language as well, but when they tackle issues that most people can understand without technical language, they are free to use a literary voice. This is the liberty historians have that I most appreciate. It allows history to be accessible, eloquent, and no less worthwhile. It is a lesson from Iggers I hope to remember in writing a thesis.

Bias and Reality: Week 2

Posted on January 31st, 2010 by kat307

The opening question in my notes reads as follows: “If you change language, do you change reality?” The simple answer, as we discussed in class, is no. You can call mercury go-go juice and die from drinking it. You can call something “non-consensual sex” and it still equates to rape, even as some terms may have very different connotations from their synonymous counterparts. However, our words and our language do have a lot more power than we would readily admit at times, which fits neatly in with the discussions of objectivity and known bias tackled later in the discussion.

My church bulletin contains a small box reading “Because words shape our thinking, we strive to use inclusive language in our worship.” In the case of my church, this often involves using non-gendered terms in hymns and sermons. But the power of language extends far beyond the Presbyterian Hymnal and well into the public sphere. When we discussed media and bias, we didn’t return to the conversation of language, but perhaps we should have. The words chosen by a particular writer or media personality (Nancy Grace, anyone?) are designed to elicit certain reactions from an audience. It’s all in the spin, and some of the spin is the phraseology. For example, referring to a prisoner as a “cop-killer in police custody” carries very different connotations than those of “person of interest held in connection with the crime.”

This can equally be applied to the writing of history. As we discussed bias, someone 9and unfortunately I can’t remember who) said that we should “use methods geared toward objectivity, even knowing it might not be achieved.” This was expanded in our list to regarding objectivity as a goal, with caveats of recognized biases in a format as accurate as possible.

Language can reveal our biases, and in the context of history, while it might not change reality, it can certainly change the perception of reality. We also addressed the quote “History is written by the winners.” This always reminds me of differing treatments of the Cold War in American and Soviet textbooks. Particularly when we study more distant events, we rely heavily on few first-hand accounts, limiting our perceptions and knowledge of the event and doubtless affecting our understanding of the period in question.

We also discussed whether it might be better to have a primary source with known biases, so as to better counter the biases in the interpretation, and this is another instance in which language and word choice can help us to better understand available information. As we make a “best faith effort,” language is an important factor to consider and to weigh in with the rest of our evidence.

Week 2 (31 Jan 2010)

Posted on January 31st, 2010 by zussmanvolunteer

The discussion from Wednesday, brought up several interesting points that I feel have been often over looked. While the process of professionalization, begun under the guidance of von Ranke, helps to legitimize the historical field and the historian, it also raises the question as to what role does commercialization play in the pursuit of history? It would be a lovely world in which commercial interests did not play a pivotal role in the minds of historians, but, being the pesky creatures that we are, needing food and shelter, necessitate this evil. With this being the case, how do historians stay true and honest to their craft while simultaneously, being profitable? Additionally on this point, how does the historian reconcile an aspect of entertainment and interest in their work, while adhering to the code of conduct that has been established?
In recent years, a number of historical tracts have come into popular culture and have made a name for their authors. 1776 by David McCullough, Band of Brothers by Stephen Ambrose, or the very popular works of Robert Remini, all fall into this category. While these men, and there have been many others, have come into the limelight of prosperity because of their works, do these works compromise their professional ethos to achieve success? Works that have passed into mainstream literary culture do possess many of the establishments that von Ranke created, such as linearity of time and a grand narrative emplotment, I personally find a major difference from these works and those that would be “academic” works.
While I cannot speak for everyone, I believe that there is a profound difference between the works that have become popular and successful and those which have become successful in the academic circles. An example of this would be Guns, Germs, and Steel by Jared Diamond. While this book has been wildly successful in bookstores, it has taken a great deal of criticism in the academic community. Iggers Historiography, while interesting, will never reach the bestsellers list. Is there a difference in professionalism between Stephen Ambrose and Robert Remini and Marc Bloch or Lucien Febvre? Has the essence of the historian changed in popular culture but remained the same in academic circles?
These are questions that I simply do not know the answer too, although I will try. I am a simple person, and I believe that the essence of the historian is not necessarily what Mr. Iggers or Mr. Cronan put forth. While these are unique aspects of what a historian can adhere to, for me, the essence of the professional historian is the ability to successfully pass on the past to the present and preserve it for the future. While Iggers addresses this in his final chapter, the face of the historian is changing. We owe much to von Ranke, but we owe equally as much to Herodotus and Thucydides and Homer. They may not have followed the ethos of a professional historian, but they achieved the overarching goal of every professional historian.

1945, 1968, 1989 and Getting Past Post-modernism

Posted on January 31st, 2010 by Andrew

Last week we discussed the basics of professionalized history and the questioning of each principle as a result of historical events in the 20th century. For instance the destruction and death caused by World War II and the Holocaust brought into question of modernity and technology’s progress. The Revolutionary 1960s and the 1980s were also destructive. This week we also finished up Igger’s Historiography which goes into great detail of historians’ great questioning of their own practice.

I remember that when I studied abroad in Northern Ireland, I had a teacher who was a renowned as a historian and political scientist. The day he focused on the Marxist interpretation of “the Troubles” in class, he noted that it was only an interesting academic and not a practical way of looking at things since it was discredited in the 1980s. It was amazing to hear him say this and chuckle at the though of it since he based his histories of the Troubles and writings that favored the Union with Britain through a “Neo-Marxist” interpretation.

Some would obviously ask what good are the above professor’s writings then? This is the response one would give if you believed in Francis Fukuyama‘s The End of History (that is Western capitalism is the end all, be all) or the extreme belief in no universal truths or metanarratives called Post-Modernism. While historians have acknowledged that objectivity can not be an achievable goal and have also rejected the tyranny of the metanarrative, the professional pursuit for history has survived such ordeals.

Instead objectivity is a never-ending pursuit for a goal to find a truth as close to what really happened as humanly possible. Metanarratives have given way to a balanced of narratives, structures, and the agency of people. So “discredited” interpretations and historical writings thought to have been “discredited” by the 20th century’s upheavals, should instead be thought as merely focusing on certain parts of history – i.e. just one piece in a larger jig-saw puzzle.

Week #2 post

Posted on January 30th, 2010 by joh307

During this past class, we discussed several things such as the old and new historian’s list, technology, and agency vs. structure.  But of those things that I found most interesting, it would have been the new historian guild list.  Between now and von Ranke’s time, there were many seminal events to take place and I was intrigued by how those events shaped history as a profession.  After reading Iggers and discussing it in class, I understand why von Ranke listed the things he did.  He wanted objectivity and stressed the science part of the study.  But that was a far different time and I was interested on what the end result would be for the profession now.

The three big events that challanged von Ranke’s list were WWII/Holocaust, the 1960s, and 1989.  Basically, they disputed the notion of the grand narrative, the continuing move toward progress, and discredited one of the major schools of historiography, Marxism, among many other things as well.  The end result of all those things were a new guideline for the profession as outlined by William Cronon.  The important thing is that while it is a new list, it doesn’t set out to dispute and reject everything that von Ranke said but where he had some good points, refine them to make them better.  Doing so builds upon the work of previous historians rather than breaking the links and trying to start new.

Statements that were kept or just refined were the emphasis on primary sources (that will never change), striving for objectivity with the knowledge that it could never be fully reached, and the use of some narrative form.  But of course there were statements that were rejected like actions demonstrate intentions and the emphasis on scientific ethos.  The new list from Cronon mentions how historians are not looking for generalizable laws since each event in the past was unique and influenced by different things and how history is non-scientific based but evidentiary based.  Cronon’s list as a whole also illustrates that now, the limitations of the profession are acknowledged and not ignored.  History cannot be truly objective.  It can come close in certain situations but can never be the real thing.  Furthermore, the intentions of actors can not be definitive every single time.  Looking at an action may give an idea of what an actor was thinking but depending on the other information, nothing concrete can be concluded, just an inference but nothing more.  By acknowledging history’s limitations, it can make the profession better in that people know what to do and what not to do as well as what to expect.
