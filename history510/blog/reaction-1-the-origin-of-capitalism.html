Reaction

Posted: Fri, 08 Oct 2010

First, let me say that, while I hate to complain about the legnth of such a quick and short book, it could have been much shorter; It seems as though Ms. Wood felt the need or the desire–depending upon her arrogance, I suppose– to make a point and then spend five to ten  pages repeating it.

Second, aftern  noticing on the back that the cover design to my verson is attributed to  ”random”, I noticed that it really is.  At least Webber’s book has a picture of the proto-protestant on its cover.

Third, I really like the Idea of capitalism being essentially a social event, such as Brenner and Thompson present.  Wealth seems to have gone from serving a practical and social purpose (first, stability then the acquisition of luxury items) to becoming the purpose of effort; which may or may not be the defintion of capitalism.

This brings me to my point: I take issue with what appears to be her definition of Capitalism and what I believe to be the general definition of capitalism. The Capitalist ethic, most people might agree, is that a person uses an advantage in a product–whether it be a surplus or even simple availability– to sell for more then it cost to acquire or produce. Her alienation of worker efficiency from “extra-economic” advantages– or the separation of “market” and  ”Markets”, which seems to imply that competitive advantage is eliminated once the technology of transportation is eliminated and causes people to look for another advantage–  allows her to make a social point about the all-inclusive nature of capitalism  but removes perhaps the “spirit” of common capitalist critique. Morally speaking (and I am not saying she is making a moral point) ,  Capitalism then becomes not just the evil of greed but instead is just the evil of exploitation; I am not sure the difference is so important.
