Ranke's historical truisms

Posted: Sun, 19 Sep 2010

Either my parents read Ranke to me as a child or my teachers in grade school preached him, but I’ve always felt that the proper way to present history was through the facts and how things really were.  In reading Ranke’s many introductions and snippets, one can learn of his method wherein he felt that history was a science requiring inordinate amounts of source knowledge.  When I read the part about the “reader is animated by feelings of pleasure and hope, on examining the catalogues,” (61) I thought to myself that he was like a kid in a candy store walking into the archives of the cities.  I also deduced that he was either rich himself, had rich benefactors, or a great expense account to be able to travel to these places and live there while researching in the archives.
Ranke makes several important observations and statements; like saying don’t pity people who like to read old papers because these are the remnants of past ages(70), or that knowledge is inconceivable without knowing the events of past ages(113), or that historical works of great reputation and usefulness become obsolete. (258)  Even though he wanted his work to be his legacy, he apparently knew that eventually it would be eclipsed by better work.  Doesn’t that sound a bit like Kant, whom he disliked?
