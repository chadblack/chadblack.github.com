Empathy

Posted: Fri, 29 Oct 2010

Many people confuse empathy and sympathy. Empathy doesn’t mean sharing someone’s feelings, or having pity on them; it means recognizing that they have feelings (and motivations, assumptions, fears, etc) and that they are, in fact, humans and not objects. As such, empathy is fundamental to any human interaction, unless it’s some inhuman, brutal force of power where one treats another as an object. Maybe one aspect of severe autism is that those children don’t have empathy, and so can’t understand and interact with other people.

In any case, if historians are studying people, real human beings, they need to have empathy for their subject. Historians need to—as much as possible—understand who those people were inside, what motivated the behaviors under examination. Figuring out the reasons for events requires empathy, and provides coherence and significance to the description of those events. If a historian somehow managed to study an era without empathy for it, the write up would be a list of random, disconnected impressions that create confusion instead of clarity. That’s not history. Statistics, maybe, but not history.
