German Idealism…

Posted: Sun, 19 Sep 2010

Immanuel Kant proposes that history is not simply the recorded events of men as they struggle against each other and against nature for survival.  Rather, human history has a meaning, a divine purpose, as it outlines the development of mankind along a path ordained by God.  Kant suggests that God—Nature—has fore-ordained man to develop a harmonious global civilization wherein the individual is freed of barbarousness and vanity.  In Kant’s universal future history, rationality and morality have triumphed over self-interested individualism. 

This development is structured along man’s dual nature of building and destroying.  Man craves cohabitation and community so he develops societies wherein he may live with others, yet his innate individualism causes him to break with society the instant it is established.  According to Kant, this contradiction is divine, and it is the driving mechanism behind history—history is driven by mankind’s inner conflict.  Only over time does man learn to be rational and moral, and only then to societies thrive and become civilized.  Once established, nations develop similarly, devolving into warfare and strife until they can learn to peacefully coexist under a larger, international governing body.  The end of history will be found in the globalization of freedom and liberty, for only then can man truly understand God and assume his true (and possibly existential) consciousness. 

But what does this say about history as a profession.  For the purposes of the modern historian, Kant suggests that history is merely the story of human conflict and the development of a rational morality rooted in the results of conflict.  To be sure, there is a larger meta-history of divine guidance and progress, but Kant simply cannot separate philosophy from history.  As such, his system is innately flawed as he looks for only what he seeks to find.  To a certain extent, all historians to this; but Kant’s philosophically driven history is very narrow in its perspective and may marginalize or obscure the actual causes and meanings of events. 

After reading Kant’s theory of a universal cosmopolitan history, I am curious where we may place a history written according to such parameters within the three constructs of history established by Hegel?  To be sure, Kant’s construct does not fall into the category of original history.  Within Kant’s construct one could arguably find elements of universal and pragmatic reflective history, but there may also be trends towards the larger philosophical history.  Where does Kant’s interpretation of history fit within Hegel’s constructs—into which category does Kant fall?  Do Kant’s and Hegel’s individual theories mesh with each other on a larger scale?
