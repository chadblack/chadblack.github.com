11/28 Logics of History

Posted: Mon, 29 Nov 2010

In his book, Sewell argues for more dialogue between history and the social sciences, asserting that each has something to teach the other. He notes that historians often appropriate social scientific theory, but that sociologists, perhaps from a desire to appear as scientific as possible, generally do not adopt the temporal theories inherent to historical study. His autobiographical discussion of the turn from social to cultural history in the 1980s was very well done, and the personal lens gave the chapter an interesting angle. His discussion of structure was a little hard to follow, but given the very clear style of the rest of the book, I suspect that my difficulty with that chapter was due more to my lack of background in the field, rather than to Sewell’s explanations of the concept.

The chapters on the taking of the Bastille and the Marseilles dockworkers were the ones I found most interesting and helpful. I appreciated the application of theory to a concrete historical event (another term Sewell describes well); it allowed me to better understand how the theories discussed could be applied, rather than just leaving them in the abstract. I also appreciated his argument for using quantitative data, when applicable, as an additional means of mining sources for information. As a medievalist, my source material is somewhat scarce, and being able to extract every pertinent thing from it is crucial. I rather wish we had read this book earlier in the semester, as it clearly defined some terms that I had struggled with in other readings and for which I had been unable to find clear explanations.

I have a couple of friends doing graduate work in sociology, and reading Sewell’s book makes me want to initiate a dialogue with them to learn more about how they undertake studies in their discipline. They both study modern ecological sociology, so I’m not sure how much theory/methodology we could exchange, given our different foci and time periods, but I think it would be interesting to compare notes.
