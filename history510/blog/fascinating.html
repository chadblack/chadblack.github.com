Fascinating

Posted: Sun, 28 Nov 2010
I think that is how Mr. Spock would describe The Logics of History by William Sewell.  At least, I doubt he would use his other famous line, “illogical” to describe it.  I myself will have to read it again…and again before some of the ideas will sink in, maybe.  He caught me right out of the gate with the notion of how historians think about time and social interactions, or social temporality as he termed it. It’s fateful, contingent upon the sequence of events that are complex and historically contextual; they are a mix of continuity and change that happens chronologically.  I believe that’s the gist of it.   He then talks about a shift from social history to cultural history, which sounds like a good base for ethnohistory.  I will agree that culture is learned by the individual through the agency of the society, community, or group they are in, and through repeated exposure to a distinct set of symbols – uniforms in the military or wooden shoes in Holland.  Could we not say that academia has a culture all its own as well by employing this definition?
In chapter 8, he used the French Revolution and the storming of the Bastille as his base for defining historical events.  To me, it read as what makes an event historical.  Think back to 9/11 as an historical event.  Political and social structures, at least between Al Qaeda and the U.S. were dislocated.  There was a cultural transformation with symbolic interpretation; lots of songs, movies, and books. It was shaped by particular conditions.  There were definitely heightened emotions, and acts of collective action by emergency personnel and people on the street.  It was marked by the displaying of U.S. flags everywhere, and even the current military actions being taken are its legacy.  It changed the way we think of travel and how airports, amusement parks, and stadiums like Neyland, treat security.  The attacks may have been contained to a few places, but the action left a mark on the nation and spurred action just like the attack on Pearl Harbor, but I’m pretty sure a nuclear bomb is not in the Middle East’s future.  However, this section of the book offers us a good way to look at historical events.
Like I said, I’m going to have to re-read most of this, or as the old folks used to say; let it percolate for a while, before I can articulate better the ideas therein contained.  For now, this is will have to suffice.
