Thoughts on Archives and Digital History

Posted: Tue, 23 Nov 2010
  • Dan Cohen and Roy Rosenzweig, Digital History: A Guide to Gathering, Preserving, and Presenting the Past on the Web.
  • Antoinette Burton, Archive Stories. Durham: Duke Univ. Press, 2005.

This week’s readings were of eternal practicality to my future.  Digital history was an interesting introduction into online historical writing. Though it presented itself as a how to manual it also openly states the limitations of digital history.  The cost and time that goes into creating and maintaining a website can often shadow the actual boundaries of a department or historian.  Also the manipulability and sometimes inaccessibility, side affects of the internet, are two major factors of skepticism which I retain for digital history.  One of the positive aspects to digital history, which I admittedly use everyday, are the online databases, run often by libraries from various campuses, which facilitate the ability to do research.

Concerning archive stories, this quote really spoke to me,

“We would not like to end on yet another trumphalist note, because we recognize that the telling of stories, like the production of history itself, has no intrinsically redemptive power, whether revolutionary or conformist.  Stories—in whatever narrative form—embed as many secretes and distortions as archives themselves; their telling encodes selective disclosures, half-truths and partial pasts no more or less than do histories ‘proper.’” Pg 20

Archive Stories caused me to rethink the infallibility of an Archive.  I, of course, understood that approaching the materials within an archive a historian should never take said documents at face value, but I failed to realize that the archives themselves are subject as well. The information they collect reflect what ever status quo their geopolitical locations deem.

This work also brought to my attention  the importance, uses and limitations of oral history, such as consistency and reliability, which I then realized are the same issues historians face with written history.  Now I do not mean to understate the fact that oral history has a large grain of salt to swallow… however it is one of those final frontier, as far I can tell, when Textual evidence leaves you in a bind.
