10/17 Novick

Posted: Sun, 17 Oct 2010

In Chapter 11, Novick notes that the post-WWII swing back to an historical consensus was like a pendulum swing back from the relativism of the interwar period. I had this idea throughout the book as the author presented his main narrative, apart from the side story and details of increasing numbers of professors and history doctorates awarded and the decreasing salaries. In reading the book, I felt that the pendulum was often driven by the societal contexts in which the historians were working: endowed positions that sought historians who fulfilled the views of donors, the interwar lack of optimism, for lack of a better word, and the general consensus found, at least superficially, after WWII.

Novick’s book was interesting, but sometimes difficult to wade through. I feel as though I’ve been presented with so many names and opposing ideas that I can’t keep them straight. Becker and Beard, who came up quite frequently, are certainly two who are memorable, with their relativist leanings.

Bevir’s article is an interesting take on objectivity and seems to be an attempt to reach a middle ground. Absolute objectivity cannot exist, contrary to the views of American historians c.1900, but complete relativism is also unwarranted. As a budding historian considering how objective it is possible to be, and how much my experiences/circumstances/life will color or should color my historical interpretations and writings, I can see merit in his idea that a type of objectivity can be found through comparison of theories and arguments with an eye toward finding where they agree and how they diverge. He draws an important distinction between responding to criticism through simple defense of a theory vs. altering or advancing that theory in response to critics.

Though I agree with that thrust of his argument, the article itself is a little too philosophical for me, and I take issue with some of the assertions he makes and the way he supports them. I often found that he used an extreme case as an example and then generalized the result to more moderate instances. The best example is his argument that because humans can perceive the difference between a dog and a wolf (and therefore continue to survive), human perceptions are generally true. There’s a biological, survival instinct that encourages us to know the difference between something we can trust and something that could eat us. The inability to perceive facts and correct theories in history may deny us a degree and subsequent employment in the historical field, but it, on its own, will not kill us…
