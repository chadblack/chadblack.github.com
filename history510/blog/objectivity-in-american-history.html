Objectivity in American History

Posted: Sun, 17 Oct 2010

Peter Novick, That Noble Dream | Mark Bevir, “Objectivity in History”

I was struck this week by Peter Novick’s sweeping review of objectivity; in many respects, Novick’s work was less a history of objectivity and more of a history of American professional history.  Objectivity is cleverly used as a vehicle to examine the growth and development of the profession. 

In its infancy, professional historians found a sense of certitude in history as the story of man’s progress.  Because they were rooted in scientific processes, methodological, cultural, political, and ideological judgments were unassailable and protected them from criticism.  The Great War changed all of that; the pre-war certitude which characterized the profession’s faith in progress eroded, creating a declining confidence in professionalism that helped call the associated doctrine of objectivity into question during the 1920s and 1930s.  Relativism would reign supreme during this period.  

Similarly, World War II would shift the foundations of the industry yet again, as relativism, pragmatism, legal realism, and the social sciences came under increasing attack.  During the post-war boom, historians constructed a new, somewhat chastened, partially hybridized objectivist synthesis which trivialized the relativist critique.  Novick ultimately asserts that American historians were compelled to take sides in a larger ideological struggle; because it was a war of absolutes, the Cold War could not allow for indecisiveness and relativism. 

The logical extension of Novick’s argument is that American historians have never really been concerned with objectivity at all, that it was merely a buzzword of the profession used to defend professionalism.  From this perspective, Novick’s assessment corresponds to Bevir’s argument against empirical objectivity.  Bevir suggests that empirical objectivity is impossible because man’s experiences or observations are innately personal and embody the theoretical categories of the individual having the insight.  Thusly, man cannot determine conclusively whether an individual theory is true or false because such judgment will be based on theoretical assumptions embodied in his observations. 

Bevir then argues for a comparative objectivity.  For him, objectivity is created by “comparing and criticizing rival webs of interpretations in terms of facts” upon which most historians tend to agree within their specific discipline (334).  Reduced to an analytical exercise, objectivity is rooted in ideas of professionalism and comity which require: criticism to be given and taken seriously; frowns upon dismissing contrary evidence as a mere exceptions or deviations from the truth; a presumption against purely face-saving responses to critiques of theories.  In short, Bevir’s comparative objectivity is founded on little more than intellectual honesty. 

Of the two, Novick’s was certainly the most thought provoking read.  Objectivity has certainly ebbed and flowed in American professional history, and Novick suggests that while there have been concerted dissents for and against relativism, objectivity, and professionalism, the flux of objectivity is largely the result of events outside the control of historians.  Objectivity is, therefore, an ideal which is—depending on which side of the aisle you are on—either a goal to be sought after, or an unattainable pipe dream which clouds the true motives of history.
