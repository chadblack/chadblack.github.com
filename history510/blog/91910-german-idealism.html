9/19/10 German Idealism

Posted: Sun, 19 Sep 2010 I must confess that Kant’s take on history seems, to me, to be rather teleological, with its idea to it is possible “to have a history with a definite natural plan for creatures who have no plan of their own” (Introduction) and that man has the ability to continually progress to something better, despite his many attempts to no do so. Of course, this teleological feel has strong elements of the Enlightenment in it. I do find it interesting that Kant doesn’t see that his historical Idea contradicts the efforts of empirical historians; as I understand his theses, it wouldn’t necessarily do so, but it would alter the perception of history and the interpretation of events, such that one would end up producing the kind of history that states that, for example, the Merovingians, the Carolingians, medieval France, etc. were all purposeful steps in the creation of modern France.

The beginning of Hegel’s work is certainly much more comprehensible than the latter parts, in which he delves into the more philosophical ideas of Freedom, Spirit, and Reason—all capitalized. Hegel divides the study of history into three broad categories: original history, reflective history, and philosophical history. While reading the first part of his introduction, I was rather struck by the modernity of his views that sometimes came through—certainly not everything he wrote, but part of it. The critical reflective history, which Hegel seems to disparage, appears to be the type of history that Ranke would have favored with its “criticism of historical narratives and an investigation of their truth and credibility” (7). I found the rest of Hegel rather difficult to wade through, and I am an interested to hear everyone else’s opinion on his work. I did find Hegel’s definition of history interesting, that it is the “development of “Spirit in Time,” (72), again a statement that has a definite Enlightenment feel to it.

I found the excerpts from Ranke to be the most interesting of the three. I’m very glad that we read Grafton’s history of the footnote before reading some of Ranke’s specific text, as it gave me an idea of some key points to look for and, more importantly, made me aware of his place in historiography (otherwise, I would have wondered why we were reading something from this person whom I had never encountered). Most striking was Ranke’s definite privileging of eyewitness accounts, which came through in a variety of ways, whether explicitly stated in his works or not. His view on this certainly seems to have filtered down to today, as James Humphreys mentioned in his blog post.

As a medievalist, many of the secondary works that I read contain numerous statements that qualify the works of primary sources—something along the lines of “It must be remembered that X lived and wrote 70 years after King Y died.” Or its corollary “Z’s writings are especially valuable, as he was a contemporary of Pope W.” Thus, I too have been trained to feel that eyewitness, or at least contemporary, accounts are of the utmost importance. This concentration on first-hand narratives does put an interesting spotlight on last week’s reading, in which we found the denigration of Spanish conquistador accounts of the New World, even though they were the first Europeans to actually visit the American continents and have contact with the Amerindian civilizations. One wonders what Ranke would have thought of their accounts.
