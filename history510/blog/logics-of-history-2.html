Logics of History

Posted: Sun, 28 Nov 2010

Our reading this week was Logics of History: Social Theory and Social Transformation, by William H. Sewell Jr.  In it, Sewell relates history to a vocabulary of social theory, making historical study more relevant to social sciences and cultural studies, and vice versa.  He places Historical events into a theory of social structures in an attempt to answer the question, what can history and social science teach one another?  That is a wonderful point of view.  Historians should not shy away from utilizing other disciplines to create an accurate picture of whatever their topic may be.  Sewell believes that Historians can learn a lot about theories of social structures from social scientists.  On the other hand, he believes social scientists can learn something of ‘the temporalities of social life’ from historians.

Sewell’s idea seems like common sense to me, and it was refreshing to have it set down so eloquently with examples that show the effectiveness of combining the benefits of the disciplines of history, sociology, political science, economics and anthropology.  This is a fitting way to close out the semester, as we began the year talking about trends of social sciences mixing with historical practice in search the best ways of ‘doing history.’  In short, this is the way I would like to approach history in my own studies.
