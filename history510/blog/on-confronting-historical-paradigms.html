On Confronting Historical Paradigms

Posted: Fri, 01 Oct 2010

In Confronting Historical Paradigms by Steve J. Stern et. al., the authors provide detailed critiques of the attitudes and language employed by world systems theorists and make suggestions for the improvement of historical analysis.  Specifically, the authors advocate taking an approach toward Latin and African materialist models and their affects on the individual peasantries, as opposed to earlier attempts to define a single peasantry construct.  However, they also point out that even world systems theory doesn’t always apply to the European model, which was the metropole to developing peripheries.

The major issue with world systems theory, and its offshoots, as these authors point out, is the overgeneralized, even false classification and characterization of peasants, as if they were the Marxist model of a European proletariat, or as if they existed as voiceless, unchanging shadows in a capitalist system.  The grouping of the differing seigneurial systems in Mexico and Oaxaca into one model is a prime example; as is the differing situation of peasantries in South Africa and the Western Sahara.  However, the authors are clear to point out that it is not wrong to place a particular group within an economic system, as long as proper care is taken to avoid the pitfalls of viewing the world system as the most important for historical analysis, as opposed to peoples, regions or states.

My overall impression of this collection is that the authors did an good job of using specific example to, not completely condemn, but explaining and break down some of the historical misconceptions that were developed within cultural and social mindsets from the 1950s to the 1980s, while offering up positive trends toward productive alternatives for future historical analyses.
