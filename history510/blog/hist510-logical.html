hist510-- Logical?

Posted: Sun, 28 Nov 2010
When I first completed Logics of History by William Sewell, I was not sure exactly how I felt about the book as a whole. Although I finally came down on the favorable side of the book, this was not one of my favorite assignments for this course; I believe the word that most accurately how Logics of History struck me is “unexpected.” From the title, I suppose I was anticipating a book directed more toward developing historical methodology instead of one that aimed to promote interdisciplinary theoretical constructions. This preconceive mindset was a surprising inconvenience proved to be a surprising inconvenience while reading the book, for I constantly had to readjust my expectations and remember to judge the book based on its own merits. I must admit that I enjoyed the setup of Logics of History quite a lot; having each chapter as a self-contained argument helped divide the book into manageable segments and allowed Sewell to develop the individual points most important to his overall theme.

On a basic level, I agree with Sewell that social theory needs some reworking in order to be useful to contemporary academics. Beyond this fundamental observation, I most strongly agree with his critique on the problems of social theory and how he proposes to address these issues. One of the biggest problems he identifies is the static nature of social theory in the social sciences, which precludes the possibility of historical change. I believe that incorporating historical concepts of temporality would benefit social theory precisely because, as Sewell says, it would make such structures flexible enough to accommodate the inevitable changes society and culture go through over time. I thought Sewell did an admirable job of arguing for greater incorporation of basic historical assumptions into a working social theory, although my favoritism toward the discipline of history predisposed me to accept claims on the value of historical methodology.

I also appreciated Sewell’s ideas on “social network of scholarship” that exists amongst the intellectual community of scholars. By this, Sewell simply means that no scholarly work is created entirely independent of “outside” works, outside in this case referring to extra-disciplinary. This idea ties directly into one of Sewell’s basic premises: the need for greater interdisciplinary cooperation between history and the social sciences, namely sociology, anthropology and even economics/political science.  It is his belief in the interconnectivity of these disciplines that drives Sewell’s search for a more universally comprehensive social theory. Although I agree with Sewell’s conception of an “intellectual community” that transcends disciplinary bounds, I hesitate to extend this argument to the length Sewell does. I accept the assertion that each discipline experiences an overlap of goals and methodology, but these basic similarities should not override more pronounced differences. Historians, sociologists and anthropologists are all trained to look for different things and each pursues its goal in the manner most appropriate.

Because of my conviction that each discipline is inherently unique, I also found myself wary of Sewell’s attempt to create a social theory applicable to each discipline. I am certainly not saying that such a thing could not be done, or would not be useful if accomplished. I am simply not sure that a universal social theory is necessarily desirable in contemporary scholarship. To make such a theory acceptable to all disciplines, the emergent structure would necessarily be characterized, if not entirely composed, of compromises. I cannot help but wonder if each discipline is better off adhering to the form of social theory most suitable to it.
