Objective comparisons

Posted: Sun, 17 Oct 2010
Mark Bevir wants us to look at objectivity in history based on comparison.  It is his idea that objectivity can be found through debate between rival theories where the two sides can find some common ground on a subject.  The two parties then debate based on rules, which govern their intellectual honesty.  All of this sounds good until he goes into the part of the anthropologist and the tribe getting to an understanding where they can communicate about their world views, and compare the merits of each.  I have a question on that as it pertains to history.  Unless the tribe has a written history before contact, I would think that anything learned from the anthropologist will be incorporated into the tribe’s understanding of their own history.  Thus, that history is altered by the contact.  I think that a historian would rather record the tribe’s history untainted instead of discussing world views with them.  Would it not be more objective to learn their history rather than impose upon them yours?
