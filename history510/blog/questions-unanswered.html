Questions Unanswered

Posted: Sun, 12 Sep 2010

Jorge Canizares-Esguerra, How to Write the History of the New World

In his voluminously footnoted tome, Canizares-Esguerra sets out demonstrate that a critical, scientific, source-based practice of history developed in Spain and her American colonies prior to the emergence of Leopold von Ranke as the father of modern history. 

Very briefly, the author’s argument suggests that in Renaissance and Enlightenment Europe a “conjectural” or philosophical methodology developed which later morphed, via epistemological debates, into a system which placed validity on contextually understood primary sources and the inclusion of extra-literary sources.  Rather than discount Amerindian sources, early Spanish chroniclers went to great length to include them in their works and the suspicion of Amerindian sources was the result of an Enlightenment era backlash against Renaissance humanism.  The Spanish literati were actively engaged in a debate about the validity and use of sources pertaining to the New World, thus creating a prototype of Ranke’s modern methodology of history.  Ultimately, this method found its way to the New World and was used by patriotic bourgeois writers to critique European histories of the region as uninformed.

After finishing the book, one wonders about the author’s intent because the narrative ends rather abruptly without a satisfactory conclusion.  If Canizares-Esguerra intended to demonstrate, through his narrative, that colonial Spain developed a critical, scientific method of historical investigation then he succeeded—documentary evidence may suggest a protomorphic theory of critical history in colonial Spain.  If this was his goal, however, he could simply have written a lengthy article or two and accomplished just as much as he did with his book.  The book simply does not leave the reader with a sense of accomplishment or closure. 

After reading the book, I found myself wondering: if this was a concerted trans-Atlantic movement among the literati of imperial Spain, why did it fail to have any impact on the larger Western world?  The author suggests, in chapter 3, that this protomorphic scientific history gained great prominence in Spain, but that factionalism at court and within the academy over methodology ultimately derailed most attempts to publish prominent histories and relegated many manuscripts dusty archives.  Annoyingly, he only briefly addresses actual circumstances which likely prohibited the success of this movement in his discussion of Spanish unity. 

All of this leaves me wondering—if the movement was hampered by factionalism both within the academy and at court, and if it failed to produce any major literary works acknowledged by the larger Western world, was the movement really a coherent movement at all?  Furthermore, does it even matter if these developments were occurring in Spain if they failed to influence scholars in the rest of Europe?  Often times it is not the discoverer who gets credit but the man who popularizes the idea.  Lastly, one has to wonder, was the movement fully understood in the New World, or was its methodology simply co-opted in service to patriotic goals in the wake of imperial Spain’s collapse?
