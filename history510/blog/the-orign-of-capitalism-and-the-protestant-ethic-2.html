The Orign of Capitalism and The Protestant Ethic

Posted: Sun, 10 Oct 2010

Our Readings this week were Max Weber’s The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism and the Origin of Capitalism: A Longer View, By Ellen Meiksins Wood.  I found their writing styles to be lighter fare than our last few weeks’ readings, having digested Marx, Engels, Stern et al with difficulty.  However, these two theses were no less compelling studies, and built upon the others’ theories while bringing some excellent observations to bear.

Aside from Weber’s sweeping generalizations about peoples of varying religions behaving in one way or another, and his Darwinesque depiction of Protestantism winning the battle over capital, I thought he made a very similar argument to that of Wood.  That is, they both believe that true capitalism started in England, though I think they would disagree about the timing.  On the other hand, the major difference between the two is that Weber seems to fall into the trap Wood warns us against.  He assumes that capitalism has everywhere in various forms at least since the Middle Ages, and that it is a natural economic evolution.  For her part, Wood believes that capitalism was in no way a foregone conclusion, and that it formed late in the pre-modern era amid much jockeying for position among different types of economic systems in France, Germany and the Italian city states. 

Furthermore, while the two would agree that capitalism won the struggle, and is firmly entrenched in every corner of the globe today, they would likely disagree that this is the result of a natural order of things, or that religion played the key role.  Wood argues that protestant rhetoric likely formed as a result of capitalism and not the other way around.
