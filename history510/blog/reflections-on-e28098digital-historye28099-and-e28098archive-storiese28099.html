Reflections on 'Digital History' and 'Archive Stories'

Posted: Sun, 21 Nov 2010

Rather than wax philosophical, I’ll just say up front that I enjoyed these two books a great deal.

Daniel J. Cohen and Roy Rosenzweig’s “Digital History: A Guide to Gathering, Preserving, and Presenting the Past on the Web” is intended as a “practical handbook rather than a theoretical manifesto” (p. 13). To the extent that they focus on the general ins and out, the pros and cons, of the Web as a history tool, they succeed quite well.

The authors’ evenhanded tone does seem a bit odd as they discuss the phenomenon of “grassroots historians’” online handiwork. They seem rather indecisive regarding the level of trust that should be given Web sources. But their views on copyright law approach outright stridence.

Nevertheless, I rate this as a useful, and still timely, guide, as per the authors’ goals.

“Archive Stories,” meanwhile, is a treasure trove of diverse, but still widely relatable, tales pertaining to historians’ archiving efforts.

Standouts include:
• Ramirez’s “A Living Archive of Desire,” which highlighted the poignancy of a life that, at least at face value, is far removed from my own.
• Sentilles’ “Toiling in the Archives of Cyberspace,” which in several ways dovetails nicely with the Cohen and Rosenzweig book.
• Fritzsche’s “The Archive and the German Nation,” a fascinating look at one nation’s “absence of a common past and the enormous violence entailed in the attempts to produce it” (p. 204).
• Pohlandt-McCormick’s “Soweto in the Archives of South Africa,” a terrific recounting of the writer’s attempt to undo some of the damage done by a regime whose officials “erased the histories of their victims and contrived to create histories that denied their culpability” (p. 301).

“Documents are never innocent,” Pohlandt-McCormick writes (p. 316). And the same is no doubt true of the aforementioned accounts. But irrespective of their authors’ agendas, those works have great value, representing as they do once-lost threads now woven into history’s rich tapestry.
