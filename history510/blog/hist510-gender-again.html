hist510 Gender Again

Posted: Sun, 14 Nov 2010
This week, I was not pleasantly surprised upon completion of The Sexual Contract. Fortunately, I was not disappointed either and the book managed to satisfy my somewhat higher expectations following the discussion last Monday. Although I enjoyed many of the ideas put forth in this book as well as Pateman’s use of gender theory, I found some parts rather difficult to get through.  Pateman’s deep familiarity with contract theory, a knowledge I do not particularly share, also hindered my attempts to fully understand her arguments.

From the start, The Sexual Contract grasped my attention.  Although I have had some exposure to the original contract, I must admit that prior to reading this book I was guilty of many of the misconceptions Pateman critiqued.  According to Pateman, the story of the original contract has not been fully explained because a critical component of this contract, the sexual part, has been ignored. The original contract’s social role has been well examined, but its role as a modern form of patriarchy remains something of a mystery.  I greatly appreciated Pateman’s constriction of her patriarchal examination to a specific form of patriarchy as it emerged in the modern world. In addition, Pateman acknowledged that women’s positions have varied greatly across time and geographical distances. Our discussion of Bennett’s book last week left me leery of attempts to generalize gendered structures too broadly. I also enjoyed Pateman’s argument that political right originates as patriarchal right. I have never considered the original contract as the means by which pre-modern patriarchal right was transmuted to political right and new form of patriarchy, but this argument was quite persuasive for me. I was, however, slightly hesitant of Pateman’s suggestion that the original contract was established only as a way to subjugate the female sex. I would not deny that this motivation factored into the decision to contract, but I disagree with the extent to which she pushes this argument.

For me, the most interesting part of The Sexual Contract was the discussion of patriarchy. It was interesting to see Pateman’s argument for the incorporation of patriarchy into historical study following Bennett’s last week.  Pateman disagrees with the idea that patriarchy declines as a viable form of political power in the last couple of centuries. Instead, patriarchy is still a dominant influence that affects not only the household but politics and the economy as well. I must admit I was not completely enthralled by her long discussion of the theoretical confusions surrounding patriarchy, but I admit the usefulness of such a survey.  Perhaps my favorite part of the patriarchy argument is her assertion that patriarchy is a social invention. Pateman delves briefly into biology and theories on man in the state of nature, particularly Hobbes, to establish a father’s disassociation from his child. According to Pateman men have no natural right over women or children; in fact, maternal right ruled in the state of nature because it was the mother who bore the child. After inception, the father no longer played a role and had no direct link to the child. Barring a natural foundation then, the idea of patriarchy must be a human invention. The patriarchal powers of fatherhood have an entirely different social meaning than those of motherhood. Although Pateman refers frequently to the invention of patriarchy, I was disappointed not to see a discussion of how such a power construction emerged. Of course, such a task is likely too large for this book but I would enjoy a study explaining this phenomenon. 

Overall, the sexual contract was one of my favorite books this semester. I thoroughly enjoyed reading a more concrete application of theoretical ideas rather than a pure study of theory. I have also been surprised at the level of appreciation for women’s studies the last two week’s readings have instilled in me. Women’s studies have never figured largely into my historical perspective, but that has already started to change as these reading have diverted my attention to the pervasiveness of such factors. Although gender studies are valuable in their own right, I find even more useful the application of such theories as a means of shedding additional light on other areas of history traditionally devoid of such considerations.
