Confronting Historical Paradigms 10/03/10

Posted: Mon, 04 Oct 2010

I must admit that this book was a little difficult to get through; I’m not a fan of economic history or Marxist theory. Even though the work seemed to deal largely with peasant movements, it still had that feel of economics about it, which I find sometimes difficult to understand and often hard to become engaged with.

That being said, I did like the focus on peasants as individuals and the idea that we must study peoples as singular units instead of lumping them together. Though I do not have a background in economic historical theory, it seems to me that the world-systems theory does subsume many people and cultures into the desire to create a label and a paradigm, which likely is not as universal as it wants to be. Stern’s discussion of the Potosi silver mine was an insightful dismantling of Wallerstein’s thesis, I thought.

I particularly enjoyed Isaacman and Roseberry’s essays, perhaps because they did not concentrate on the historiography and the theory quite as much (or perhaps their essays were just longer, so the theory took up proportionately less space), areas with which I’m not familiar. They seemed more engaged with the actual history and with presenting concrete examples, along the lines of Stern’s Potosi mine, to illustrate/discredit the theories they were discussing. I would have liked for some of Isaacman’s information to have been more developed; it felt at times as though he was slinging snippets of data at the reader to prove his point by the sheer number of examples he could use. Roseberry’s section was very good at pointing out that Marxism often doesn’t consider the agrarian peasant—it focuses on the wage-worker.

As Amanda has said, when I began reading the book, I was expecting the authors to be blatantly anti-Marx. Instead, they seem to accept some of his basic ideas, but with some modifications, specifically the need to address groups individually instead of universally.
