Reflections on 'The Sexual Contract' and 'Gender Trouble'

Posted: Sun, 14 Nov 2010

Were male-female relations still in a comparatively primitive state 22 years ago? Was Carole Pateman then entirely too inclined to see everywhere the legacy of an unenlightened past? Or am I now too naïve to see pervasiveness thereof?

In “The Sexual Contract” (1988), Pateman does an excellent job critiquing contract theory – in particular many of its inherent inequities and contradictions. Particularly fascinating are her citations of the tortured logic that was used to justify slavery and women’s exclusion from society’s functionings.

In short, from a social standpoint, her position is eminently sensible. It’s a well-reasoned explanation for what I understand or have seen firsthand of gender relations among those of pre-baby boom generations.

And yet …

At the risk sounding like an apologist and/or a simpleton who feels we’ve attained the best of all possible worlds (no need to elaborate on the myriad well-known ways we still fall short), I – a 40-year-old male – don’t believe present-day situation is quite as bleak as the one Pateman outlines.

The dynamics between men and women at large probably are in some ways apt to bear vestiges of the old, exclusively male-authored contract. But among married couples my own age, for example, I see very little that resembles the unilateral subordination that Pateman depicts; nor do I see even an expectation thereof. (I should add, I’m not qualified to comment on any evolution of attitudes toward prostitution.) So there’s that, for whatever it’s worth.

As for Judith Butler’s “Gender Trouble” (1989), I found particularly intriguing her assertion that “only the feminine gender is marked … the universal person and the masculine gender are conflated, thereby defining women in terms of their sex and extolling men as the bearers of a body-transcendent universal personhood.” (pp. 13-14)

Such a sentiment is cause to ponder the English language’s annoying (but supposedly innocuous) lack of gender-neutral pronouns – an absence that traditionally has rendered male pronouns the default setting.

And then, specific to our university, there’s the practice of referring to our women’s athletic teams as the Lady Vols (as distinct from the male just-plain Vols). How might that be explained, and is it a sign of progress that such nomenclature is now rather quaint?
