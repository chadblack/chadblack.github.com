Gender and Sexuality II

Posted: Sun, 14 Nov 2010

Our readings this week were Gender Trouble: Feminism and Subversion of identity, by Judith Butler, and The Sexual Contract, by Carole Pateman.  I found these two works to be interesting; even if I didn’t entirely agree with their arguments.  Gender Trouble discusses what amounts, in relation to my own interests, to be a fringe topic of how different sexualities are not given equal weight in gender discussions.  Instead, they are often dismissed as abnormal, and therfore excluded from ‘normative’ constructs of gender studies and sexuality.  The Sexual Contract, on the other hand, takes a universalist look at gender relations in which male dominated society has created a civil construct that has enslaved women through a sexual contract gives new form and funtion in our modern society to the old pre-enlightenment patriarchical system.

Pateman’s work is a call for true gender neutrality in both the public and private sphere.  “The appropriate feminist response then seems to be to work for the elimination of all reference to the difference between men and women in political life,” (pg 16), and “if contract is extended into the private sphere, inequalitites of status between men and women in marriage must disappear,” (pg 167).  Given the bleak picture of the relationship between the sexes that Pateman paints throughout the book, this hardly seems achievable.  To me, the male seems demonized in The Sexual Contract, and women are portrayed as martyrs to the system.  Even though, in a Marxist sense, women own the means of production, and males want to own this process, so one might think women would have more influence on the system.  I’m being facetious, but this raises an issue we discussed last week of the level of female complicity in the system.  Pateman portrays women as subjugated and exploited vigorously by the male.  I don’t know; it all seems very general to me.  Pateman fails to interpret the nuances of familial cooperation that exist between man and wife in the ‘sexual contract.’  Even as I fully realize the legal constructs of our society have been male dominated hsitorically, there are obviously redeeming qualities to this relationship that allowed it to survive as long as it has.  So, although I didn’t completely disagree with her arguments, I must say some of them definitely raised an eyebrow.

In Gender Trouble, Judith Butler explains that “I opposed what I took to be heterosexualism at the core of sexual difference fundamentalism,” (1999 preface, pg viii).  Butler argues that a focus on normative sexuality as opposed to lesbianism and homosexuality leads to skewed analyses of gender.  I agree that the male female dichotomy alone in a heterosexual milieu narrows the focus of gender studies, I’m not sure the inclusion of all sexual preferences would add clarity to the major arguments of gender studies such as the issues with which Pateman wrestles.  However, I do think Butler is wise to point to the need to exercise caution in gender related analyses by understanding that the general context in which one is conducting one’s studies may not be entirely representative.
