Empathy and my Understanding

Posted: Mon, 01 Nov 2010

“Does a historian need empathy to understand the past?”

In short, yes.  Empathy as I understand it, is understanding the emotions of people from the past and withholding our judgement of them.  It is rather presumptuous to think that one can truly understand what a person felt in, for example, the Reconquista on either side of the conflict but plenty of clues and decent guess work can land a historian in the right direction and like most things in the historical field, trying is a good portion of the battle! I must admit though… passing judgement always seems to sell books, Crusaders as evil doers or heroes seem to stock the  minuscule medieval history shelf at  any Barnes & Noble or Borders book store.

“Should the historian have the obligation to approach that past culture with the anthropologist’s cultural relativism?”

No. I feel this is limiting the historian to a specific view and I am far to pluralist to allow this to happen.  I enjoy reading different schools of thought on the same period as they keep each other in check.

“Finally, is “culture”, and however *you* define that term, an indispensable object of historical inquiry? Is it the most significant? How do you define it?”

First, to define “culture,” I have no problem with the anthropological definition of the way of living done by a group of people at a certain point in time.  And culture gives context to historical inquiry, I would be weary of separating them.  Concerning it significance, it depends on the question asked, after a context is established the historical inquiry could deal with an infinite amount of topics, and culture itself wont be the main discussion.
