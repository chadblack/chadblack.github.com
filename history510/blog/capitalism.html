Capitalism

Posted: Fri, 08 Oct 2010

Ellen M. Wood, The Origins of Capitlaism: A Longer View

I was struck this week by Wood’s rather slim volume on the origins and development of capitalism.  Yes, Max Weber is important, but Wood is able to dismiss him with a barely a single page of commentary.  According to Ellen Wood, the rise of capitalism was not inevitable—if anything it was an historical accident spurred on by a precise set of circumstances in a confined location.  Wood begins her discussion by dismissing the current historiography of capitalism’s origins—including Marx—arguing that the dominant works either ignore the question of capitalism’s origins, focus their investigations towards questions of why it did not develop elsewhere, or that they are invalid because they are anachronistic.  At their core, they all take on basic assumptions that profit is the single motive of mankind, and that capitalism arises naturally out of that motive.  Thus, the rise of capitalism is discussed in terms of overcoming obstacles to its ascension. 

The prevailing understanding fails to acknowledge the particular imperatives of capitalism, the mechanizations of market operations, or the laws of competition, profit maximization, and capital accumulation.  In Wood’s opinion, capitalism is a very recent response to a specific set of circumstances arising in the agricultural economy of early modern England.  According to her evaluation, a basic market system existed in farming leases in which substantial landowners (most aristocrats) abandoned systems of extracting substantial rents out of tenet farmers in favor of coercing them to be more productive, thus increasing their rents.

Supported by Locke’s ruminations on property ownership and “improvement” of the land by using it to its maximum benefit, a system of agrarian capitalism developed.  The agrarian model promoted efficient farming, thereby enabling the most successful and efficient farmers (who produced greater income for their landlord through their efficient success) to work more land with less labor.  Thus, a largely landless laboring class developed which provided the requisite labor for Britain’s nascent industrial revolution.  Britain’s industrial growth was seen as a threat by other European nations who then forced capitalism on their societies in order to keep the pace in a zero-sum political system.  Imperialistic by nature, a fully capitalism caused Britain to embark on an age of empire-building, thus setting the stage for the eventual globalization of capitalism.

Overall, her theory is coherent, but I think there logical fallacy in it.  Wood’s argument suggests two things: (1) that capitalism is not natural, that it is just a happy accident arising out of a specific set of circumstances, and (2) that as such, there are viable alternatives to capitalism which we may impose upon society.  Wood suggests socialism as a viable alternative.  The fact that she does not provide any framework for doing so can be overlooked.  The key is that she suggests it is possible because capitalism is not natural and was a choice.  Yet, in her own argument she posits that capitalism is, by its very nature, expansionistic—it will constantly spread by exploiting technology, labor, natural resources, and any other means at its disposal.  As an accidental development, it is entirely possible that capitalism might never have come into being.  However, given her stance on capitalism’s innate expansionist drive, is it possible that having been established that capitalism can ever be toppled?  I don’t think she has made her case for the reversibility of capitalism.
