Reflections on 'How to Write the History of the New World'

Posted: Sun, 12 Sep 2010

Somebody (maybe or maybe not Winston Churchill) once said, “History is written by the victors.”


For some four centuries following Columbus’ 1492 voyage, Spain unquestionably was among the biggest winners in the realm of New World colonization.

And yet, as Jorge Canizares-Esguerra’s book seems to illustrate, even among Spaniards, there was in those years no clear consensus regarding the histories of the conquered regions, or the conduct of their “countrymen” as they did their conquering.

I say “seems” for two reasons.

First of all, this is a very dense work. Canizares-Esguerra not only elaborates at length on his main points, he also has a habit of repeating them, making his writing that much thicker.

But this book also has challenged me in a second, and more significant, way. It underscores the ways we often cast our own inherent biases as the “gold standard” by which other outlooks are to be judged. Unquestioningly attuned to our accepted views of history, we have a knee-jerk tendency to dismiss alternative views, even when, objectively viewed, they are no more and/or no less “fantastic” than our own.

So as I read Canizares-Esguerra’s words, I became conscious of the fact that my initial reactions thereto were to some extent shaped and colored by that which I’ve long thought I know to be true. Conversely, I get the sense that those words are in some ways a manifestation of what the author thinks or wishes to be true.

Does that make sense? No? Well, I’m not sure it does to me, either. This is heady stuff.
