It's A Small World, After All...

Posted: Sun, 03 Oct 2010

OK, I’m going to attempt to do less summarizing and more opinion oriented blog posts on the books from now on.

I actually really enjoyed this book. The essays really tear into modern Marxist conceptions of the world-system. I enjoy seeing universalist concepts being overthrown, because there are no absolutes in history. The region by region analysis of the capitalist world-system, pointing out a hundred exceptions to it, very effectively tears a hole in the entire concept. The world-system is revealed to be a very, very small world, consisting mostly of Europe, North America, and prosperous Asia, with Africa and Latin America being shown to not fit in with the neat conceptions. The African examples were, to me, even more powerful than the Latin American ones, which used a number of modes of production and had their own economic platform, but were still practicing capitalist forms of economic practice. In Africa, on the other hand, “peasants” among others actively and successfully resisted incorporation into the capitalist world-system. The capitalist model is neither universal nor inevitable.

The chapter on peasants in Africa was my favorite. I like resistance stories anyway, and a description of the systems by which the “peasants” resisted integration through everything from flight to banditry, and refusing to partake in cash crop economy by returning to subsistence farming. The Latin American segment afterwards reinforces, to me, the fact that Marxism doesn’t take into account the agrarian world nearly enough, a fact Mao and Lenin tried to incorporate in their own ways, though their theories are failed ones in my opinion as well. Agrarian societies are so far out of the scope of the industrialized world that Westerners don’t know what to do with themâ€”or at least the Marxist world-system doesn’t.

So if the world-system breaks down when you look outside of the industrialized world, what does that leave us? Is Marxism invalid? I think economic superstructure is still a valid jumping off point, but the progression of universal capitalism into socialism seems to be pretty much not true.
