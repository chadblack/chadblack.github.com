Discipline and Punish-- The Birth of the Prison

Posted: Sun, 31 Oct 2010

Our reading for this week was a philosophical extavaganza, Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison, by Michel Foucault.  Just in time for Halloween, part one offers a horrific scene of punishment through several forms of torture designed to mimick a trip to hell, ending with the offender, a regicide, being quartered and burned to ashes.  Nice!  Seriously, this is a very thoughtful and thought-provoking look at how our society deals with punishing criminals, and how their treatment reflects an evolution of our intentions in disciplining and punishing.  Spoiler alert, we (the public) have systematically removed ourselves from the equation.

The book takes us, with a mixture of chronology and topic, through the development of punishment as public spectacle, which prevailed until the late 17th century, through a reformist period typified by the Philadelphia prison, to the formation of our modern day prison system.  Foucault’s study is more than just a historical survey however, it is a study of the cutltural change that lead to how we envision discipline and punishment.  Foucault draws interesting parallels here to the idea of the fetishism of capitalism we’ve studies in previous readings.  “If economic exploitation seperates the force and the product of labour, let us ay that disciplinary coercion [imprisonment] establishes in the body the constricting link between an increased aptitude and an increased domination,” (pg 138).  In short, there is an underlying sociological theme to discipline and punishment that involves why we do it, and how we do it.  Our reasons and methods have changed over time to reflect our own evolving ideas of what the role of the prisoner in our society. 

Not all things have changed drastically, and not all things reflect the supposed more humain society we live in today.  This is the paradox Foucault presents us with.  The individual cell is a prime example.  A monachistic throwback that has gone through many uses in the history of the prison system, practically every inmate has an individual cell in the modern version.  On a final note, I found Foucault’s discussion of the prison system as business philosophy from the 18th century onward to be intriguing in light of current issues with prison systems in Arizona (a la their new immigration laws), and the ongoing search for a new Guantanamo.
