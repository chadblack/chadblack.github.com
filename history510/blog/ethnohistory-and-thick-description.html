Ethnohistory and "Thick Description"

Posted: Sun, 24 Oct 2010

Our readings for this week were Toward an Interpretive Theory of Culture, by Clifford Geertz, Other Customs: The Anthropology of History, by Marshall Sahlins, and The Emperor’s Mirror, by Russel Barber and Frances Berdan.  These readings give an interesting view of the contemporary thought on what it means to do anthropology, and some time-honored practices to aid in the practice of anthropology and ethnohistory.  The Emperor’s Mirror, published in 1988, offers is set up like an instructionl manual for performing ethnohistory, and many of the tips and sundry case studies could also easily cross over to the study of history.  The book offers many avenues of approach for dealing with primary sources with the purpose of performing a micro to macro style historical research which is where it derives its ethnographic influence. 

The Anthropology of History, published in 1983, also stresses this micro to macro method of doing history.  “The idea is that history is culturally constructed from the bottom up: as the precipitate, in social institutions and outcomes, of the prevailing inclinations of the people in general,” (pg 517).  With this in mind this essay also stresses that the historian always be mindful that earch culture has its own mode of historical activity and its own historical consciousness that may look very different from our own.  The idea of Heroic History in many cultures is used as an example, as it varies in form from the European idea that the “theory of history is that social outcomes are the cumulative expressions of individual actions,” (pg525).

Thick Description, first published in 1973, contends that many old anthropological notions, such as Skinnerian behaviorism, objectivism and operationalism are no longer viable in ethnography.  ‘Thick description’ is instead an attempt at proper interpretation of the symbols that every culture uses in order to discover the context behing a subject’s actions.  Conversely, the objective detailing of a person or cultures actions with no regard to their context if ‘thin description.’  Ultimately, Geertz asserts, ethnography is, at its heart, an interpretive activity, and not an observational one, because things “we call our data are really our own constructions of other people’s constructions of what they and their compatriots are up to.”  “Most of what we need to comprehend a particular event, ritual, custom, idea, or whatever is insinuated as background information before the thing itself is directly examined,” (pg 9).  So, beyond the good practices of observation and dilligence in collecting information required by the historian as well as the ethnographer, it is also crucial that the researcher becomes very skilled in the art of cultural interpretation as well.
