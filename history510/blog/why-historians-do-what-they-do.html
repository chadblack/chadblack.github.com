Why historians do what they do

Posted: Thu, 26 Aug 2010

While reading Grafton’s “The Footnote,” I came across this, “…historians perform two complementary tasks. They must examine all the sources relevant to the solution of a problem and construct a new narrative or argument from them.” (4, 5) He also went on to say that use of footnotes “identifies the work of history in question as the creation of a professional.” This spoke to me as it related to what we discussed in class last Monday. How are we as emerging historians to show that we truly belong to this guild? Mathematicians use proofs of mathematical formulas, or postulates. Doctors site case studies of diseases and cures to show they are correct in what they are saying. We have the written records and documents. Why a person did this or that can be ascertained by what was said to witnesses and what the person himself may have written. All the while we are never forgetting that those telling the story are infusing their own view as well, or are aware that posterity will use their words for its own interpretation. Still, Grafton returns to the idea that “footnotes serve to prove the author’s membership in a guild rather than to illuminate or support a particular point.” (108) It simply shows that we have done the legwork to find information on the topic upon which we are expounding.
