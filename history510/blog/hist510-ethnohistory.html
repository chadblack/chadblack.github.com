hist510 Ethnohistory

Posted: Sun, 24 Oct 2010
Unlike several of the books that have been assigned this semester, I found that I could offer no real complaints about The Emperor’s Mirror. I felt this was an extremely well-done book that provided a great deal of useful information in a stylistically enjoyable manner. Although much of what the book contained was not necessarily new information, it was nonetheless useful to have some basic methodological approaches clarified and expanded upon. Many of the points Barber and Berdan brought up were, for me at least, familiar considerations that had been introduced since I began my historical training in undergraduate college. Regardless of any former acquaintance with these foundational factors, I still found it enlightening to have issues such as the influence of background, complications with evidence, and basic research methods directly addressed. 

Reading this book was particularly interesting following Peter Novick’s That Noble Dream. Ethnohistory as put forth in The Emperor’s Mirror seemed to assume that objectivity is an impossible goal, although Barber and Berdan would not consider objectivity to be a desirable end in the first place. I have always been a little uncertain as to the exact definition of ethnohistory, and this book did a great deal toward clarifying some of the questions I had regarding this new approach. Personally, I felt that the differences between history and ethnohistory were quite subtle; as a historian, I am still not sure I entirely agree with ethnohisory’s identity as a completely separate phenomenon from historical study. It seemed that ethnohistory’s methodological approach would work well as a sub-area of the historical profession as well, although I am sure the same argument could be made for incorporating it into anthropology just as there is a valid argument for its independent existence. 

I found myself agreeing with much of what Barber and Berdan defined as ethnohistory, particularly in relation to the goal of ethnohistory. Relativism has a much-respected place within this methodological approach, as I believe appropriate when attempting to study the interaction of different societies in the distant past. I especially enjoyed the distinction that ethnohistorians were attempting to provide a medium through which the past could be viewed, to create an understandable discourse with the past that allows modern readers access to a completely different world. As the authors stressed, ethnohistory embraces a multiplicity of interpretations rather than seeking a universal, or even generally consensual, explanation. The Emperor’s Mirror does a good job at providing a convincing argument for the existence of and acceptance of this diversity. As almost all historians acknowledge, the background and experience of individual authors necessarily affect their perception of historical events. Although evidentiary differentiations are just as important when explaining the past, this factor does not often receive the same degree of attention. I felt that the extensive treatment Barber and Berdan devoted to highlighting the many possible interpretations the same piece of evidence could have, for both contemporaries and modern historians, to be one of the most useful and interesting parts of the books. I believe most historians are aware of evidentiary complications, but personally I will be much more conscious of such difficulties than I was prior to reading this book.

Geertz’s excerpt likewise dealt with the emergence of ethnohistory as a methodological approach. Geertz’s work was a more theoretical piece than The Emperor’s Mirror and focused on the idea of providing “thick description,” where thick description provides for the subtle meanings societies attribute to actions according to cultural norms. Ethnohistory as described by Geertz seemed to resemble anthropology more strongly than the historical tinge it had in Barber and Berdan’s book, which doubtless contributed to his emphasis on the interpretation of culture. According to Geertz, culture is a public phenomenon that provides the framework against which meaning is attributed. I found Geertz’s example of the wink versus twitch to be particularly enlightening. Another interesting point was his assertion that ethnohistorians should attempt to capture the individuality of cultures while still exposing “normalness,” or general patterns. Drawing this distinction reminded me of an earlier week’s debate on the validity of grand theories as opposed to localized studies, which we likewise concluded was best solved by a compromise between the two. Although the reading for this week did not strictly deal with the historical profession, I still found them to be some of the most interesting and helpful works so far.
