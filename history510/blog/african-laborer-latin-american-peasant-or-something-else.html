African laborer, Latin American peasant, or something else...

Posted: Sun, 03 Oct 2010
It seems to be the same pattern with each of the texts I read.  In the course of learning what the author is attempting to elucidate, I get a secondary education in words and authors.  This go-round, I learned words like reification and obfuscation, which are nearly opposite.  I got to learn about Wallerstein’s core, structural-functionalism, African agency, modernization theory, underdevelopment theory, and modes of production analysis. The five authors cover these topics very deeply with examples and case studies, and enough notes and references to make another book.  Seriously, Chapter 4 alone was 60 pages long, and then had 54 pages of notes/references to back up what Isaacman wrote about peasants in Africa. 
I won’t rehash a synopsis of the book, but a couple things stood out to me.  First, all the authors seem as if they are covering time-frames in African and Latin American history from a capitalist-Marxist viewpoint. They are following what is happening in these areas as the people move with fits, sputters, trials, and tribulations toward capitalism.  Isaacman refers to all these attempts at labeling and studying such changes as paradigms. (209)  The peasants, slaves, workers, and various other non-elite characters are viewed as they go about resisting capital’s control, thwarting capital’s advance into their life – forcing changes they do not want.  Second, they are critiquing and expounding ideas of others in light of new research of themselves and others and recent ‘paradigm’ shifts.  Mostly they advocate listening to the peasant voices, both from oral histories and those hidden within written records of the controlling political powers.
My problem with all this is that these authors are trying to place all of this within the context of a capitalist-Marxist world view.  I feel that both Africa and Latin America, and even North America and Asia for that matter, are areas that developed for millennia outside the European sphere.  Asia in the form of China and Japan achieved a written language long before the others, including Europe, and even the Mesoamerican empires of the Mayan, Inca, and Aztecs had their own form of written communication.  Asia is touched upon at the end of the book as a reference, and the author did start on hegemony, (388-9) but they did not go that much deeper.  The main idea that leaps out at me is that the European nations exercised the idea of ‘might makes right’ over established nations who could not match them militaristically, or better as the right of conquest.  China was able to withstand their onslaught until the opium wars I think, when the British used their military to broker a business deal the Chinese were unwilling to make.  Africa and Latin America did not have such a large unifying government like the Chinese.  The Aztecs were close militarily until disease reduced their numbers and their vassals decided to deal with the Spanish – that really turned out well for them.

What these authors seem to be writing about is the continued resistance of those conquered people.  If these dissimilar groups could agree that ‘the enemy of my enemy is my friend’ then they may be able to assert their collective will to achieve a better life for their descendants, or as seemed the case time after time, to be left alone in their world without greed and ambition.  Who knows what kind of world system would have emerged if these people had been left to develop at their own pace and in their own way.
