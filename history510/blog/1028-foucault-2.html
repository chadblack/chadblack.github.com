10/28 Foucault

Posted: Fri, 29 Oct 2010

I’m going out of town this weekend and I’m not certain when I’ll be back, so writing this early…

After reading the Foucault book, I can certainly tell that he has philosophical leanings. Not being much of a philosopher myself, or being someone who cares for the subject, I’m not completely certain what to make of this book. I really enjoyed the first half of the book in which he discusses the public nature of penance and the ways in which it was imposed on the body (largely because the idea of the “public” and corporeal history are two fields that greatly interest me), but after that, around halfway through, I felt as though Foucault really lost his grounding in history and the philosophy took over. At times, it felt more like he was simply creating points for his argument rather than basing them in actual fact.

There’s a strong thread in the book, especially in the second half in which he discusses private penance in the penitentiaries, in which Foucault really seems to almost prefer the public, body-based penance. In discussing his antipathy toward newer forms of societal discipline, I think he goes too far in equating the soul-based, regimented penance of prisons with, for instance, reforms in the public schools (educational issues are a topic for a different post). Nevertheless, his assertion that private punishment was primarily a deterrent toward others considering committing the same crime rather than an actual chastisement for the offender is interesting; however, I think the same could apply to public body-based punishment as well. Similarly, toward the end of the book Foucault discusses ways in which the new types of penance are centered in the hands of the state. However, discipline had always been a part of the state, in the sense that it was in the purview of the king, as Foucault notes. Additionally, in the pre-modern era, church and state were not separated in the way that they are today; thus, ecclesiastical punishments carried out by the Inquisition can be considered a type of state-based discipline.

Thus, there were parts of this week’s reading that I enjoyed and parts with which I disagreed. I’m interested to know what everyone else thought, and I’m looking forward to the discussion on Monday.
