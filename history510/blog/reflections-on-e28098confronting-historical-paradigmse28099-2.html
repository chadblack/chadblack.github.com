Reflections on 'Confronting Historical Paradigms'

Posted: Sun, 03 Oct 2010

It is by now axiomatic to say that the 1990s were a decade that changed the world. Of course, when Americans express such thoughts, they typically are referring to political developments in their world – i.e., the First World.

But in this work, which was released in 1993 – and which is subtitled “Peasants, Labor, and the Capitalist World System in Africa and Latin America” – the authors consider other realms, and the roots of the economic dynamics found therein. In doing so, those writers address developments that often get short shrift, to the extent that they are remembered at all, in the U.S.

In considerations of the “underdeveloped” state of much of Africa and Latin America, the principles of dependency theory and world-system theory traditionally have been evoked. So it is interesting to see this book’s authors characterize those beliefs as overly simplistic and even “circular” in their logic.

Likewise intriguing are the writers’ examination of, and sometimes outright challenges to, notions regarding the nature and origins of peasantry and the African slave trade.

Finally, because this work, as tends to be the case with any such effort, comprises a snapshot of views as they then existed, as shaped by that which had come before, it is interesting to take the authors’ interpretations and compare them with what since has happened (e.g., the spread of poverty in Latin America, the official end of Apartheid in South Africa, the furtherance of Robert Mugabe’s controversial land reform program in Zimbabwe).

That’s the sort of thing we now, like they then, can do with the benefit of hindsight.
