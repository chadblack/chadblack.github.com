Marx & Engels Reread

Posted: Sat, 25 Sep 2010

You know, there was a time when I would have dreaded rereading Marx. That was before Hegel.
In all seriousness, I read Marx only once before, my freshman year of college in a 200 level philosophy class about the founders of Western Thought. We read Plato, Marx, Dewey, and Rousseau. And I’ll admit, I wasn’t enamored with Marx at the time, because I was a freshman, and all I really wanted to do was party and act like a complete idiot. It’s amazing how when you look back at when you were younger, sometimes you just want to smack yourself in the face.
Needless to say, I got very little out of that class, because I was barely there even when I was physically in the seat. So my vision of Marx and Marxist thought was a little basic, and after reading Hegel for last week, I was genuinely excited to reread him.

Here’s what I get out of Marx:

1. History has a system of governments, of which the final will be socialism. Capitalism, just like feudalism or previous socioeconomic forms, will fail, and the new society will be communism in its pure, non-Soviet sense, a classless, stateless, egalitarian society.

2. History is the story of class struggle.

3. Hegel had a great idea, just horrifically backwards; Feuerbach had a great way of using Hegel, though his conclusions were not necessarily correct. Flipping Hegel’s ideas upside down was the way Marx and Engels described as being the best way to use him. The physical world is what is real, and all of our conceptions are products of it, rather than the cause of it.

4. Historical materialism is basically the concept that all society, all institutions, have an economic framework, the real foundation that everything else is built on. Economics is the key to society and history.

5. Marx was brilliant, and his ideas, while not in vogue anymore, are an interesting way to look at the universe; but Engels is a darn better writer, and makes things much clearer than Marx does.

I enjoyed reading Marx much more this time; the passage of almost 5 years and a lot of growing up helps, as does having just read something infinitely more dense. The other reading, not from the primary source, was significantly less help. I did not find, this time at least, Marx to be all that difficult, or Marx’s ideas to be obfuscated in any way. The Postone reading actually made Marx less comprehensible in my mind.

It was good to reread Marx. I’m excited for Monday’s discussion.
