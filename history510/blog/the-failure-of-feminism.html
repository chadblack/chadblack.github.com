The Failure of Feminism

Posted: Sun, 07 Nov 2010

Judith Bennett, History Matters: Patriarch and the Challenge of Feminism | Joan Scott, “Gender: A Useful Category of Analysis

After reading Bennett’s History Matters, I am utterly lost as to what she perceived the impact of her book to be.  She rants against the male-dominated hierarchy of professional history, demands a greater focus on pre-modern women’s history, and an increased attention to the oppression of women in all periods of historical investigation.  The entirety of her book came to little more than a lament for the separation of professional history and feminist activism.  Taking such an approach, her argument seems confusing at best and incomprehensible at worst. 

Because she is a professional historian, perhaps the greatest insult to her and the tripe she calls a book is to say she suffers from nostalgia.  While Bennett asserts her lack of nostalgia (153), she frequently reminds the reader that she wants to restore the feminism that has been lost.  She writes: “This book seeks to recover … [the] ideal of a seamless union of history and feminism” (1); “Feminist activists and scholars, once so steeped in history have lost interest in the past” (29); “it is time for historians of women and gender to regain our feminist indignation” (154). 

The entirety of Bennett’s argument is that “women’s history seems to be losing its feminist edge; deferring unnecessarily to male authority, dwelling overmuch on biographies of unusual women, taking a sharp cultural turn away from social and material considerations, attending more to diversity than to power differentials, and allying with a history of masculinity that is developing in surprisingly problematic ways” (22-3).

Despite her constant rant about what is wrong with women’s history today, she never comes to any sufficient conclusion as to why the loss of militant feminism is such a terrible thing for women and gender historians.  Because the historiography of women and gender has become so widespread and culturally inclusive, Bennett suggests that it has lost its feminist edge.  Perhaps she adequately describes the expansion of women’s history and its pluralistic development over the last four decades, but she fails to explain why the evolution of an inclusive, globalized point of view is such a terrible thing for the women’s movement or for the genre or women’s history.  It seems that history only matters for Bennett when it is explicitly political and specifically affirms her feminist point of view.

Joan Scott, on the other hand, presents a more logical and understandable perspective of the women and gender field of history.  She suggests that the term gender fits nicely into the social construct of expectations pertaining to men and women.  Like Bennett, Scott suggests that there is a trend within professional history to be more inclusive in historical investigations which naturally brings differences of class, race, and gender to the fore (1054).  Scott goes on, however, to examine the strengths and weaknesses of the theories and viewpoints which dominate the current historiography of women and gender. 

Scott succeeds because she is able to form a coherent argument for the inclusion of gender-based examinations.  She argues that because gender is a “constitutive element of social relationships based on perceived differences between the sexes” as “a primary way of signifying relationships of power” (1067), that it may open new perspectives in examining historical actors.  Owing to its creation as a social construct, gender-based analyses are applicable to nearly all realms of historical inquiry, from class, to race, to politics.  Scott argues that “historically specific, normative definitions of gender” are “reproduced and embedded” in the culture of people, time, or place (1073).  Because “gender is one of the recurrent references by which political power has been conceived legitimated and criticized” (1073) it may provide a better understanding of the motivation and ideals of the period and people under investigation.
