On Gender and Sexuality

Posted: Sun, 07 Nov 2010

This weeks assigned readings were Joan Scott’s 1986 article “Gender: A Useful Category of Analysis,”  and Judith Bennett’s, History Matters.  I found these two works to be quite instructional on a subject about which I have very little knowledge.  I was unaware there was such a heated debate among feminist historians about how to best define and do feminist history.  Judith Bennett actually quotes Joan Scott directly in her conclusion as someone whose philosophy of “simply comparing data about women didn’t get us very far,” places her firmly in the category of the ‘third wave’ of feminism she seems to feel is more a Sex in the City style feminism in comparison to her hard-core ‘second wave’ of feminism in the 1970s. 

Although I agree with Bennett’s basic points, I disagree in some ways with how she might put them into practice.  For example, I agree that feminism should be confrontational to inequalities against women today, and, where possible, history should be used to clarify/bolster this position.  At the same time, I don’t think the idea of creating a category identifying ‘lesbian-like’ behavior in order to create more historical precedents, sources, or foundations for research is necessarily a good idea.  Another conclusion Bennett makes is that the patriarchal system has come to be ignored due to its unpopularity among postfeminists and general crticism of talk of women’s oppression being amateurish and overly emotional.  However, as Bennett clearly points out, the gender movement has branched out into many finer forms of argument, and universal history has gone by the wayside.  Perhaps patriarchy is the universal system of feminism, and maybe the system is being refined, and, over time, will be redefined. 

It seems Bennett feels jaded by the transition in focus from feminism to gender study, but she does admit the new outlook shows an overall maturation of the field.  In that respect, you can’t have your cake and eat it too.  On the other hand Joan Scott’s article is a look at the use of “gender as a way of referring to the social organization of the relationship between the sexes,” (pg 1053).  In my opinion, this more inclusive approach seems more enlightened than Bennett’s, and may actually serve better to solve her problems with fodder for medieval research and beyond.  In Scott’s view, no understanding of the feminine can be achieved without attempts at understanding the male in the same ways.  While this makes perfect sense to me, this is likely to be just the theory that Bennett considers a further homogenization of her beloved field.
