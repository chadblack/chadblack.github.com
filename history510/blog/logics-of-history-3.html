Logics of History

Posted: Sun, 28 Nov 2010

William H. Sewell Jr. argues in Logics of History, that historians, sociologists, and anthropologists all have unique insights into the study of the human condition and unique limitations, based on the assumptions and practices of their respective disciplines. Sewell therefore argues that the most complete and incisive study of social history would be done with due consideration shown to each of the disciplines. He argues that this is in scholars best interest, not only to improve the work done and the quality of the understanding the work would engender, but also because each discipline shares interest in many of the same things, and thus have much to benefit from such academic exchanges. He explained his motives in encouraging such interdisciplinary study due to his personal experience working with scholars of other fields.

Of particular interest to me was Sewell’s examination of Geertz. Since we have read some of Geertz’s work on “thick description” in this class a few weeks ago, I was intrigued to read Sewell’s take on it within this study. Sewell notes first that Geertz is the preeminent ambassador of anthropology to other scholastic traditions, though he is often criticized within his own field. Sewell agrees with the materialist critique of William Roseberry that Geertz lacks sufficient attention to temporality and power relations, looking at cultural practices simply as given and detached from their historical tradition(177). Sewell had previously given this to be the major failing of anthropological study, and the area in which historians could contribute the most to the other two disciplines. Still, Geertz remains very important to historians, which Sewell attributes to the similar interest historians and anthropologists have in studying different cultures. He says, “History, like anthropology, specializes in the discovery and display of human variety, but in time rather than in space,”(179-80) or, as we have been apt to say all semester, “the past is a foreign country.” Answers pertaining to understanding people who operate from a different set of assumptions is naturally intriguing to historians, whether the subjects of the study are past or present. We are both most comfortable evaluating cultures from some type of distance, looking in from the outside. Finally, I found his case studies at the end of the book to be interesting, though personally I would have been more interested to see one at the beginning of the book and one at the end.
