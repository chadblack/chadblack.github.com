hist510 Gender

Posted: Sun, 07 Nov 2010
I was initially leery of the readings for this week simply because I tend to shy away in general from feminism and women’s history. To my mind, feminist history conjures images of resentful women creating female-only histories that do the exact same thing as their male-centered counterparts do, only switching the subjects’ sex. With this mindset, therefore, I was quite relieved to discover that both the article and the book shunned this approach in favor of a more mutually balanced history of women that focused primarily on integration. I thought both texts provided well-reasoned arguments for the broader incorporation of women’s history into more mainstream history and why such a development would prove useful to the overall profession.

Joan Scott’s article focused mainly on the term “gender” and the position this idea currently holds in the historical profession. According to Scott, gender has traditionally entailed two different aspects: descriptive power and theoretical power. For much of its existence, gender has primarily been employed in its descriptive form rather than its analytical one. Scott, however, argues that gender would serve a more useful purpose as an analytical category used to better understand human relations. Gender as a theoretical tool rejects any biological implications of the term and is instead regarded as a means of examining culturally constructed social identities. What I found most interesting about this interpretation of gender is the universality it implies for both historical applications and sex in a biological sense. Gender is not only appropriate for the study of women doing traditionally female activities, but it can now be expanded to include studies of male politics. Scott argues, convincingly I believe, that the subtle effects of gender extend into all areas of human relationships. Things such as the cult of manliness and traditional ideas of male protection all fall within the category of “gender”, thus justifying the incorporation of gender studies into traditionally male-dominated spheres. I also found the somewhat ambiguous nature of Scott’s conception of gender to be intriguing; her argument that gender identity, although operating under an illusion of coherence, is actually a constantly shifting phenomenon. 

As much as I enjoyed Scott’s article, I was surprised to find that I liked History Matters by Judith Bennett even more. The overall style of the book was well done, from the clear organization to the relaxed yet professional prose. In addition, as a medievalist I appreciated her use of medieval examples, which I believe made the book even more interesting. When I first read the book’s title, I expected another generalized argument for the overall usefulness of history, not an argument for the greater incorporation of history into women’s studies. Needless to say, I appreciate her chosen course much more. Although I am not a strong proponent of women’s history, this book forced me to ponder if perhaps that might not be because of the forward-minded approach taken by many feminists that Bennett argues against. According to Bennett, the study of women’s history is in great need of historical help to strengthen the discipline in terms of accuracy and potential efficacy. I strongly agree with her assertion that feminists need to look back to the past to truly understand and work to address women’s subservient position in society. One of my favorite parts of this book was Bennett’s argument for a theory of continuity in women’s history rather than periods of foundational change that currently dominates. Personally, I imagined from Bennett’s argument a dual existence of women’s history where the foundational layer, the system of patriarchy, remained constant while the upper layer reflected societal change. Even though shifts may occur on this upper level, such as the status rise of a previously female-dominated trade, counter changes will arise to insure the foundation of patriarchy remains. 

For me, the most interesting part of Bennett’s book was her analysis of patriarchy. As she points out, many historians shy away from such charged terms as “patriarchy”, particularly historians of women seeking to maintain academic legitimacy. Bennett, however, argues that this dismissal of patriarchy is not only inaccurate but ultimately detrimental to women’s history. I found myself agreeing with her use of patriarchy as a concept around which historians of women can focus their study and not, thankfully, as a meta-narrative. Patriarchy is such a useful starting point for study because of its constant pervasiveness in society; although it has existed in different forms over the centuries, patriarchy has remained constantly present to insure the inferior status of women. The overall effect of the patriarchal system has been to keep women socially, economically and politically disadvantaged, but this does not mean that patriarchy was universally bad. In fact, Bennett suggests that patriarchy has survived so long because women have found ways to exist in and take advantage of the system, ultimately contributing to its endurance. This aspect of Bennett’s argument was particularly interesting to me and her fair-mindedness greatly improved the substantial respect I already held for her as a scholar. Bennett’s brief study of brewsters in England to illustrate her points was particularly enjoyable, and I fully recommend her complete study Ale Beer and Brewsters. 

The readings for this week once again pleasantly surprised me, and I found them to be particularly useful because they countered so many unfounded misgivings I held toward women’s history. Both pieces presented convincing arguments for the applicability of gender studies to the broader historical profession as well as providing useful theoretical tools for doing so. I am still not as interested in women’s history as much as other areas such as religion, but I certainly hold greater appreciation now. More importantly, Scott and Bennett have convinced me that gender and patriarchy play a role worthy note in non-traditional areas of study, and I hope I will keep such considerations in mind in future studies.
