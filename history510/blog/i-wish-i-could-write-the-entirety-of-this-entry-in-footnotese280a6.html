I wish I could write the entirety of this entry in footnotes…

Posted: Sun, 29 Aug 2010

Anthony Grafton, The Footnote: A Curious History

When I began reading Grafton’s volume I found myself constantly wondering what is the point of all this, what can an overly philosophical and seemingly menial inquiry provide to my understanding of the practice of history?  To be sure, I gained a few insights into the history of the footnote, about its ancient antecedents, its relationship to commentaries and the appendix, but I ultimately failed to understand the worth of this book.  The footnote is a technical apparatus used by a variety of writers throughout history, and it may have an interesting history (to some), but does a modern historian really need any deep insight into the philosophy of the footnote?  Is a mere understanding of its function enough to guide the historian in its effective use?

Ah!  But therein lays the rub—its effective use.  Behind the multitude of examples and the discussion of methods used by ancient, medieval, renaissance, and early modern historians, Grafton addresses himself to issues which, while seemingly off topic, are useful to understanding good scholarship.  His analysis of the various forms of footnotes—such as those which provide commentary, educated debate, factual references, or the nature of sources—could spark lively debate over the appropriateness of each form and its usefulness to modern historians.  For our purposes in this course that may indeed be necessary.

Its benefits to debate notwithstanding, I am still not satisfied with the volume.   Throughout most of the book I found myself questioning what purpose the vignettes offered about Ranke, Gibbon, and other historians served.  I found the book less of a history on the footnote and more of an examination of the scholarship of history through the last four hundred years—which absolutely bored me at times, but could also come through with no small amount of humor.  At times it seemed to me as though Grafton actually got caught up in this examination of practice rather than his stated goal of examining the footnote.  It may be, however, that his examination of the practice of history over the course of these centuries is not without merit, and it may prove a lively source of debate about practices—a goal perhaps our instructor. 

Only in the final segment of the book, coyly titled “Some Concluding Footnotes,” could I find anything which would cause me to recommend this book.  Here, the author finally ties together the loose strings from his preceding vignettes.  Here, in the conclusion Grafton finally makes an argument worth reading, asserting that is the footnote and methodologies of history which make it possible to defend against the innate desire among men and nations to keep their skeletons well hidden, so that the Truth may be known and followed back to its sources.  It may be cartoonish, but it was one of the few moments if found in this book worth reading.
