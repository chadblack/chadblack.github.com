10/24/10 Ethnohistory

Posted: Mon, 25 Oct 2010

The Emperor’s Mirror was an interesting read. It was much more like an instruction manual than I had anticipated, and I think it would have been well suited to an undergrad methods course. At the same time, I see its benefit for giving us an overview of what ethnohistory is. I appreciated the long methods section because I think many of the techniques would be relevant to any historian, not just those undertaking ethnohistory itself. As a medievalist, I’ll certainly have to do my share of paleography, and even though there are certainly more advanced/specialized books out there for medieval paleography, this was a nice introductory overview of some concerns to be aware of.

The reality-mediation model, as I understand it, seems such a straightforward idea that it’s surprising that no one had articulated it before. Or, perhaps, it was just such a common tenet that no one felt the need to actually write it out before this book. As I see it, the model states that authors, either consciously or unconsciously, modulate reality/the Truth when they create documents. Perhaps by purposefully leaving out or altering information or perhaps by subconsciously not noticing the importance of an event, an author’s record of events does not exactly equate with what happened. As we discussed last week, this shows up in inaccurate eyewitness testimonies of crime scenes today. As historians, we must be careful when reading primary documents to tease out any biases from the author, and to avoid, as much as possible, mediating the reality, however altered it may already be, found in the primary source.

Geertz’s article emphasized the public nature of culture and the importance of thick description and emergence into a society to understand its culture, as a way of explaining anthropology. The wink/twitch example was really helpful to latch onto throughout the article. Sahlins asserts that anthropology and history, even though they sometimes reach different conclusions and have different methods, can be used to inform each other. I liked his example that for some tribes, the “history of the leader” approach isn’t as outdated as we tend to universally think it is. For some groups, the tribal identity is subsumed into the knowledge and person of the leader; thus, a chronicle of the chieftain is a chronicle of the tribe, at least from the internal perspective of the group under study.
