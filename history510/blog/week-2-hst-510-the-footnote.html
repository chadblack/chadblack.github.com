Week 2 HST 510 "The Footnote"

Posted: Sun, 29 Aug 2010

I must confess that Footnotes were a subject I knew very little about prior to this reading, even though I use them constantly.  In The Footnote, Anthony Grafton recreates the tumultuous history of the development of the use of footnotes and appendices.  The footnote developed as “proof” of scholarly integrity through the proper recording of ones sources of information within a given work. 

I found it interesting that the author whose footnotes I’ve always found most intriguing, namely Edward Gibbon, actually disliked them and felt they detracted in some way from his work.  I also enjoyed the way in which Grafton explains Leopold von Ranke’s role in solidifying the role of proper sourcing of information for our age, not only through his love of research, but through his adeptness at repelling criticism through the integrity of his work.

I was not surprised to see that footnoting, perhaps not in its present state, but in a fashion, existed prior to the Renaissance.  Also not surprising is that religious patronage played a part, however dubiously, in footnote development.  What I was surprised about was that there has been so much controversy about the use of footnotes, what form they should take, and even if they should be used at all.  How odd that they are such an integral part of expressing our research bona fides these days, when they were treated with such contempt by such giants as Voltaire! 

In closing, I’d like to say, tongue in cheek, that it was funny that Anthony Grafton’s The Footnote seemed to be comprised of about half text and half footnotes.  I think this was purposeful.  I was a little disappointed not to see some of Gibbon’s irreverance in them just for fun, but they were still a nice touch.
