Response to Anthony Grafton's The Footnote (*a curious history)

Posted: Sun, 29 Aug 2010

The most interesting section of Grafton’s book is a two-page section where he describes the duty of an historian “to tell the double story of the historical past and the historian’s research” before acknowledging that one of the primary champion’s of the footnote, Leopold von Ranke, “thought that the best historical narrative was a classical one, free of notes.” (pgs 67-68). I find it interesting because it tells the story of the great battle every historian fights: the battle between entertainment and authority. Authority, it seems, is purchased with the cost of at least some entertainment value; while the well-read historian can integrate the footnote into a narrative (or an argument) by second nature, many people get confused by or lost in the notes. Putting them at the end solves much of this; some histories have even done away with the footnote all together, dismissing them in works which they acknowledge are meant for the non-professional audience. The assumption is, of course, that the broad public wants a story while the professional cadre demands sources. While this seems at least minimally insulting to the broad public, it seems equally dismissive of the professional, as if they are some breed of automaton who can only process information if it comes with a superscript number behind it. I wonder, though, if this really distorts the message historians are presenting to the public–and I do not mean that the professional historian is imbibed with both certain etiquette and certain standards, which it should be– but that the historian desires a history that is not entertaining. The goal of every historical writing (I should hope) is that the joy of a subject, felt first by the historian, can be communicated through the mutual process of the narrative. Removing the footnote or the specific end note supplies another level of remove from that research which, while it may be less clumsy, takes away from the joy of learning something: that there is honest work in arriving at the truth.
