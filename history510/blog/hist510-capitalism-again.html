hist510 Capitalism Again...

Posted: Sun, 10 Oct 2010
My first thoughts when I realized that the readings for this week dealt yet again with the issue of capitalism were, to say the least, unenthusiastic. I was cheered slightly upon being told that the approaches adopted by Weber and Wood avoided the materialistic basis of previous readings, but the initial dread still loomed. When I finally brought myself to read the selections, however, I must admit myself pleasantly surprised. It was quite refreshing to read a more culturally-derived analysis of capitalism, and I found these examinations to be much more intellectually stimulating than materialistic explanations. Perhaps it is because my knowledge and understanding of economics leaves something to be desired, but for me Wood and Weber’s arguments provided a more historically grounded and encompassing study of the development of capitalism, although I was not always entirely persuaded of their conclusions. 

Although I did not initially expect much of Weber’s writings, it took only a few pages to grasp both my interest and my respect. Weber was clearly an intelligent man, but more than that I respect his boldness and innovation for advancing such a theory in the first place. The link between religion and capitalism is not one that many would discern, much less be able to provide support for. This is not to say I was entirely convinced of his assertions, for much of the time I felt that he either over-emphasized or over-generalized parts of his argument.  His central contention for the influence of Protestant ethics on the development of a capitalist society is, however, intriguing and deserving of serious consideration. 

As I read Weber’s text, I grew increasingly curious about his opinion of capitalism and the ensuing capitalist society. It soon became clear that the constant for Weber was religion, not capitalism. This does not mean that Weber saw religion as unchanging, merely that he always maintained a positive attitude toward the inclusion of religion in society. According to Weber, when capitalism first emerged it did so in response to new religious mores that advocated hard work and perseverance. Under these new Protestant ethics, particularly those held by Calvinists, people were not only free to pursue the accumulation of wealth but indeed compelled by duty to do so. Only by refraining from idleness and dedicating one’s life to improvement and work could one please God.  Weber referred to this as the “spirit of capitalism” and it was this mood that characterized the early stages of capitalist development. When religious ethics played a part, Weber appears to be okay with the presence of capitalism. As capitalism grew to be more firmly entrenched, however, it depended less on people and became more of an impersonal self-sustaining system. At this point, Weber’s tone turns much more critical. I found his overall approach interesting because it entailed the forced continuation of such a system, not its voluntary maintenance. People participated in capitalism because they had to; it had grown to be far too encompassing for individuals to exist outside of. 

My favorite part of Weber’s argument, however, was his brief examination of how Protestant ethics affected not only the development of capitalism but aspects of a capitalist society. Indeed, I was intrigued simply by his emphasis on the existence of a capitalist society. It is certainly not a new concept, but it is one that  I had not dedicated much thought to previously. When I thought of societies and cultures, I usually defined them in terms of nations, etc. When I thought of capitalism, my mind turned to purely economic matters. I enjoyed having a conscious redirection that integrated the two ideas and made me think of capitalism in a substantially more rounded, and admittedly more accurate, manner. Weber’s ability to draw connections between religious ideas and ensuing cultural developments never failed to leave me impressed, regardless of the level of conviction with which I regarded them.  At times, however, I grew somewhat impatient with Weber’s deep analysis of religious principles. I sometimes felt as if I was reading a theological survey more than a historical inquiry. I may not have preferred it, but Weber’s dedication to proving the beneficial involvement of religion in society made this approach at least understandable.  His later writings in particular stressed the positive advantages gained by incorporating the religion into daily life, notably in capitalist societies where the Church could serve to counteract the individualistic and impersonal tendencies that dominated fully-developed capitalism. Although Weber provided a religious explanation for the growth of capitalism, he does acknowledge the importance of considering materialistic explanations as well.

If I was pleasantly surprised upon reading Max Weber’s writings, I found myself strangely delighted at Ellen Wood’s book. Between her decision to focus on society rather than economics, the book’s clear and concise writing style and the innovative ideas it contained, On the Origins of Capitalism was easily my favorite book so far this semester. It was made even more appealing because it followed the series of, for me, less enjoyable books we have read in the preceding weeks.  

Wood’s main emphasis fell on addressing social influences on the development on capitalism as opposed to Weber’s focus on religion. There were a number of other comparisons and contrasts between Weber and Wood that I found very interesting; I was glad to have the Wood book to guide my reflections upon Weber’s work so that these links were clarified. Wood identified several main goals she hoped her book would accomplish: emphasizing social influences on capitalism, challenging the inevitable treatment capitalism has received, and advancing a forced interpretation of involvement in capitalist societies. By advancing these arguments, Wood placed herself in opposition to much of what Weber wrote, although the two agreed on some points. One thing the Weber and Wood did share was distaste for fully developed capitalism, although in Wood’s case distaste is perhaps an understatement. I thoroughly enjoyed reading such a strong critique of the capitalist system, particularly one that challenged the Euro-centric nature of much of the literature on the development of capitalism. 

Perhaps my favorite part of Wood’s argument was her conviction that capitalism needs to be understood as a historically specific phenomena that emerged in response to a particular set of social relations. I believe that this approach provides a better understanding of capitalism because it does not try to force the development of a single all-encompassing explanation, and it also redresses the injustice done to historical studies that try to impose the capitalist system where it does not fit. According to Wood, feudalism was not a necessary precursor to the emergence of capitalism. She proves this point even within Europe by surveying both English and French historical developments that began from a similar starting point yet ended up in very different spots. Wood argues, contrary to much capitalist literature, that true capitalism began in the English countryside in response to a shift in the social relations between peasants and lords. From England, it spread through the international market to other areas as well. I enjoyed this interpretation of capitalism’s development even more once Wood analyzed other historical aspects from this perspective, such as her analysis examining how changing social relations and the beginnings of capitalism contributed to a sense of national identity. 

Wood’s book was a very informative read, not just because of the innovative ideas she provided but also because of the brief historiographical survey she included as well. It was interesting to see the invisible constraints that seemed to exist on capitalist studies, deterring the introduction of a truly revolutionary approach to the development of capitalism. I also found this book relevant to our earlier discussion concerning the value of big narratives as opposed to local studies. I felt that Wood did an admirable job of studying a broad theme without sacrificing the importance of regional variations, a difficult task to successfully achieve. To me, her book stressed the advantages of both sides; local circumstances played a critical role in her assessment of capitalism, but she posed her study within the larger framework of capitalist studies. Even when challenging predominant interpretations of capitalism, Wood needed to operate within this framework in order to provide a relevant historical study.
