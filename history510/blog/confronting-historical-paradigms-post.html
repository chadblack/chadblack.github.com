Confronting Historical Paradigms post

Posted: Sun, 03 Oct 2010

I think the organization of this book added to its effectiveness. As outlined in the introduction, each essay looked primarily at a specific historical paradigm relating to the study of Latin America or Africa. I agree that placing Africa and Latin America side-by-side added to the study, as certain similarities and differences in past scholarly assumptions were highlighted. The introduction sets up the splintering of traditional modes of thought about Latin American and African studies nicely; rooting it in various experiences of the 1960’s and 70’s, while still noting earlier examples. This book now deals with further changes in scholarship, starting from the 1980’s. I though Stern’s argument against viewing Latin American economics as either the result of feudalism or capitalism was very persuasive; it is clear that neither alone suffices. Cooper’s evaluation of the changes in African scholarship were the most interesting essay to me. It is somewhat embarrassing how many scholars in the past dismissed African history entirely. This necessitated a generation of scholars that affirmed African uniqueness, but there was still more work to be done. As Cooper states, “that Africans had their own points of view and their own history was revealing only to people who believed they did not. A new generation of students took that point for granted and found that Africanists offered no particular set of questions and theoretical issues.”(85) Coopers addendum to his original work was also interesting, to see how scholarship has progressed, and I think his point that “underdevelopment” leads one to think there is a point of normalcy in other nation’s developments that Africans are behind, is very well taken(196). It is always interesting how certain biases seem to come back around, even from historians with good intentions.
Isaacman looks at how the study of peasants voices has progressed; its insight and its potential difficulties. It is certainly true that misinterpretation, either culturally or language-based, is a potent danger for scholars or varying experience dealing with unofficial reports of the people at large, who many times have shared vocabulary and mutual understandings unique to them(225). The essay then looks at studies of how peasant lives changed under capitalism, and peasant accounts are a vital record to these and other studies. The book then shifts back to Latin American was Roseberry’s essay, who looks at peasants, as well. I think this essay was wise to come last(besides the conclusion), because it notes the scholarship already contained in the book and expands off of it. Finally, the concluding essay nicely ties the essays back together with certain themes and areas of future research. This was an impressive bit of scholarship. While it must be admitted that it is probably not intriguing or necessarily accessible to the casual history reader, for our study of the study of history, it was very helpful.
