Reflections on Anthony Grafton's 'The Footnote'

Posted: Sun, 29 Aug 2010

I’ve never given much thought to the humble footnote. It probably would be fair to say I’ve viewed it simply as a device writers utilize “to show that they have put in hours of hard work in archive and library,” as Anthony Grafton puts it (referring specifically to students). Footnotes, Grafton continues, “confer authority on a writer.” ‘Nuff said.

But in his surprisingly engaging book “The Footnote: A Curious History,” Grafton deftly illuminates the ways in which the form’s evolution, like that of historiography as a whole, is in fact interwoven with the overlapping forces of social, political and religious history.

Quite interesting are the stories of Ranke, Gibbon, de Thou, Kircher, Bayle and others whose works and concomitant outlooks helped shape the approaches now widely practiced.

Meanwhile, particularly eye-opening to me was Grafton’s explanation of how footnotes can (and have) been used to perpetrate, rather than prevent, plagiarism. Less surprising was the disclosure that footnotes sometimes are compiled as a means of representing (or even misrepresenting) selected texts, so as to justify pre-established positions.

I would not go so far as to say that I’ll never again see as tedious the writing and reading of footnotes. But thanks to Grafton, I do now have an appreciation of their deeper significance.
