Deviance and the Knowledge-Power Dialectic

Posted: Sun, 31 Oct 2010

Discipline and Punish, Michel Foucault

In Discipline and Punish, Foucault argues for a philosophical shift in the punishment of criminals brought about most notably by the French Revolution and the Enlightenment.  These developments altered the state’s foundation of legitimacy, transferring it from the divine right of kings to the social contract.  Crime itself was changing, too.  The demise of the last vestiges of feudal France brought a new set of property relations as capitalism flourished.  Capitalistic property relations saw a “shift from the criminality of blood to a criminality of fraud” where crime was no longer violent and directed at people, but deceitful and directed at property (77). 

More importantly, “the power to judge” no longer depended “on the innumerable, discontinuous, sometimes contradictory privileges of sovereignty, but on the continuously distributed effects of public power” (81).  Logically extending from these developments, the punishment of crimes took on the model of the social contract, whereby the right to punish was shifted from the vengeance of sovereigns “to the defence [sic] of society” (90).

Less concerned with exacting blood for blood, the punishment of criminals under this new system is concerned with the “collective coercion of bodies” (169).  The main function of the power to discipline is “to ‘train’, rather than to select and to levy” punishments upon transgressors (170).  Under the social contract where crime is prosecuted as a defense of society, the punishment of criminals is not designed to force them out of society but to coerce their compliance with the social contract—to train them to be good citizens. 

Through hierarchical observation (that is observation of the masses by some vested authority), normalized judgment (where the knowledge of individuals was used to rank them according to their potential), and examination (the pedagogical science of treating the individual as a case study), outside authorities placed an arbitrary value on the individual offender so that a treatment may be developed to rehabilitate the offender.     

In Discipline and Punish, Foucault presents not just an argument for the evolution of a modern penal movement, but a larger philosophical suggestion that a power-knowledge dialectic has emerged which is rooted in the institutionalization of science and knowledge.  Perhaps the strangest extension of his system is the recasting of power from a negative, exclusionary or oppressive element in society to that of a generative or producing element.  Under such a view, the modern individual is little more than a composite of the empirical data obtained through being observed.  Such a view fits nicely within Foucault’s Carceral Continuum where all social relations are relegated, in some form or another to the pervasive desire to identify deviance.
