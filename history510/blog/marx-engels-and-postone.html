Marx-Engels and Postone

Posted: Sun, 26 Sep 2010

I began the reading this week with the Marx-Engels Reader. I read a selection and then switched to Postone’s book before finishing with Marx. I am very glad I did, because I feel Postone’s analysis helps express some Marxist theories in a slightly more accessible way. I enjoyed that selection, though it took a while to wade through, because it successfully presented Marxist theories in a way that complemented(and occasionally challenged) more typical interpretations. The selection from Postone dealt specifically with the Captial Vol. I section of the Marx-Engels Reader. However, I was interested to see how many of the ideas had already presented themselves in the section of “German Ideology,” which I had previously read. For example, the circulation of commodities was explained in Postone’s work (page 264) as “a quasi-natural network of social connections; although it lies beyond their control,” based on Marx(207-09). However on page 162 of the Marx-Engels Reader, the idea of an oppressive world trade that “hovers over the earth like the fate of the ancients, and with an invisible hand allots fortune and misfortune to men, sets up empires and overthrows empires, causes nations to rise and to disappear,” was already introduced. This is also true of the rejection of Hegelian interpretation of history(Postone, 285; Marx 148-49, 154). This is not surprising, since these are fundamental components of Marx’s worldview, and some continuity of theme would lend some fluidity to the work(if also some redundancy). However, I think it is important to note, that in all the discussion of empirical evidence to support his theories, there are certain narratives that are supported above others with a persistence that makes me wonder what evidence to the contrary may have been purposefully excluded.
I was particularly interested, in reading both these works, by the divisions of concepts such as labor, value, and time, into smaller subcategories. These notions are often conflated in my own mind, so to read them as separate, though intrinsically linked, entities was very intriguing as a way to broaden my own analysis of history. The one I was especially interested in was the multiple divisions of the concept of time, and further how these separate components of time impact value. I think the argument is particularly strong when it discusses the notion that “value is an expression of time in the present.”(Postone, 296) The argument that as advancements become generalized, they are no longer seen as advancements, but become the new baseline of normal production can certainly be seen in our society to this day, and it promotes the idea of perpetual need for growth and a directional nature of development expressed elsewhere in the work. I am glad to have read more about Marxist philosophy, rather than the abridged versions I have dealt with in the past that discussed only the need for revolution. While certainly prevalent throughout the Marx-Engels reader, these arguments are much more complex and intriguing than simply the high school summary of “capitalism is corrupt, workers unite!” to which I was first introduced.
