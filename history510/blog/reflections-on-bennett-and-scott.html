Reflections on Bennett and Scott

Posted: Sun, 07 Nov 2010

It is a paradox endemic to the American character: We feel that a better tomorrow is our birthright, even as are quick to disregard the lessons of yesterday.

Yes, I know that’s basically a variation on what Santayana said. But it’s a notion that is compellingly laid out in Judith M. Bennett’s “History Matters: Patriarchy and the Challenges of Feminism.”

For all the ways that women’s lot has improved, Bennett says, there remains a glass ceiling, and its name is patriarchy. This system is so pervasive as to be, like glass, practically invisible. And even when gender diversity comes to formerly male-dominated institutions, patriarchy lingers as a largely unquestioned norm.

I am male. I also have a wife and a young daughter. And I am not aware that I have actively engaged in any oppression or subordination of the female population, nor do I have any desire to do so. But does that mean I’ve never benefited from gender inequity? I don’t know. Certainly, I’ve not been oblivious to the constraints that some of my female relatives and friends have faced, but I suppose I’ve been of the mindset that it’s only a matter of time before such dynamics go the way of colonialism. Or slavery. Or Soviet communism. So I guess that brings us back to my original point.

I am old enough to remember the pejorative (a la Gloria Steinem) or at least quirky (a la Alan Alda) connotations that were assigned to feminism in its ERA heyday. Bennett’s work makes clear why such was the take-home message for our mainstream culture.

Meanwhile, Joan W. Scott’s “Gender: A Useful Category of Historical Analysis” continues the cultural history theme by focusing on a very specific language issue.

I have long been aware that “gender” is not, technically speaking, a true synonym for sex, and yet I’ve understood why the terms have come to be treated as interchangeable. And though I consider myself something of a stickler when it comes to linguistic matters, I’ve not really been bothered by that particular practice.

That said, Scott’s article left my head spinning. As she delves deeper, I can sort of understand her points on a case-by-case basis, but taken together, it’s a lot to absorb.

“Gender is a synonym for women.” OK.

“Gender is … a social category imposed on a sexed body.” Gotcha.

“Gender is a constitutive element of social relationships based on perceived differences between the sexes, and gender is a primary way of signifying relationships of power.” Ummmm … yeah.

I’ll be interested to see what light our discussion can shed on these ideas.
