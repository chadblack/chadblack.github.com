de Thou's peer review

Posted: Sun, 29 Aug 2010

Did I read this right?  Did Jacques-Auguste de Thou start the use of peer review?[1]  Today it is almost expected that one’s work is checked by reviewers in the various historical journals as both a demonstration of our professionalism and as a method of protecting the honor of our guild.  I’m sure that de Thou may have seen it fulfilling these, but it also may have been that he was taking the lower position to these erudite individuals.  It was a way of saying, “Hey, I’m new here and this is what I have found.  Would you be so kind as to double-check my findings to be sure the facts are correct?  If something is missing, please fill in the gaps as I want this to be as correct as possible.”  Instead of putting his work out there and saying, “Prove me wrong,” he took what Franklin later called “the humble inquirer,”[2]  By elevating other scholar’s positions in asking for help, de Thou could make use of more brainpower to try to get the history as close to correct as he could.

1.  Anthony Grafton, The Footnote [Cambridge: Harvard Univ. Press, 1997], 136.
2.  Benjamin Franklin, The Autobiography and Other Writings, ed. L. Jesse Lemisch [New York: Signet Classic, 2001], 30.
