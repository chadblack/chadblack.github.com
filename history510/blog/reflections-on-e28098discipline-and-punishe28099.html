Reflections on 'Discipline and Punish'

Posted: Sun, 31 Oct 2010

How ought society to deal with crime and criminals? What is the ultimate objective of “justice”?

Michael Foucault in his 1975 opus considers those thorny and age-old questions. And it is perhaps to his credit that in his treatment I found no real answers, but instead even more questions.

When considering the sick spectacle that was torture executions, many perhaps would be inclined to glory in the more enlightened state of 21st-century humanity. But that, of course, would be foolish. Biologically, we are no different than they. Culturally, we are not at all far removed from their world. So what reason is there to think that we possess some inherent moral superiority that would preclude us from indulging in such coolly calculated atrocities as did they (or others in more recent centuries and decades)?

Or, as Foucault (Page 91) quotes Lacretelle saying, “God, who has imprinted in our hearts an aversion to pain for ourselves and for our fellow men, are they then those same beings, whom thou has created so weak and so sensible, who have invented such barbarous, such refined tortures?”

As for the purpose of punishing criminals, and the deeper psychological issues underlying that question, again I am unsure, and more to the point, I am unconvinced that our society is sure.

It seems that the fashionable mindset remains this: “The prison, though an administrative apparatus, will at the same time be a machine for altering minds” (Page 125). OK. But for better, or for worse? That’s something else entirely.

At any rate, these things trouble me.
