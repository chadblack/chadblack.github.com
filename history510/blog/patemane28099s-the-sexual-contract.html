Pateman's The Sexual Contract

Posted: Mon, 15 Nov 2010

Carol Pateman, in The Sexual Contract, tries to argue that women are largely ignored in the idea of  the social contract, noting that despite its inherent emphasis on free will, it still has served to uphold a patriarchal society. Here, to me, is an example of a book that is far more interesting than it’s own thesis. Her reviews and discussion of the interaction between Patriarchy, paternalism, and the nature of the social contract are really well done and engaging; however,she manages–and perhaps this is simply a function of her writing style, because logically she makes the connection– to muddle these together almost at random, interjecting contract theory into discussions of other phenomena causing it to seem like she has to strain to make the connections between the various thoughts and her thesis. She also is forced to say some rediculus things: arguing, for example, that if someone looks for patriarchy they will find it (the last paragraph of the second chapter). However, her thesis is clear and i think generally proven here. She also handles a lot of thinkers from different eras deftly and is able to glean out what she finds useful and more often than not argue away what isn’t.

The hardest part of reading this book was context;  I was not sure how revolutionary many of her arguments are simply because I don’t know, for example, to what extent the marriage function of the term “patriarchy” had been lost prior to her writing it. I have a hunch my impression of some of the terms she attempts to define are impression formed after her period; many of her writings, then, loose their initial implication and now seem to be arguments against phantoms.

I do hope that  my reviews of this book are not seemed too negative; I did enjoy it and though it was an interesting and well developed take. I am just not sure either where it fits in to feminist theory or just how connected all of these critical approaches are to one another and weather or not contract theory is ocean to which all the rivers flow. The negative, than, is precisely that: a reflection of my ignorance.
