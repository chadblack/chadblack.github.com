A Noble Dream it is

Posted: Sun, 17 Oct 2010
Imagine that, some of the wealthiest people in the area where American students of history first went seeking doctorates in Germany were the very professors they were learning from.  Of course, these students were from the upper class of America as well, but it was the idea that knowledge and learning commanded prestige and wealth that is so striking.  Now, all that is left is the prestige and honor of the title.  But I digress.  These early students brought the ideas of von Ranke back with them and began building American historical ideals.  The greatest achievement these early historians sought for was the elusive ideal of objectivity.  If they could get the study of the past down to a science then bias would be removed entirely.  The problem was using the right science.  In the period before WWI, historians were looking to professionalize their ‘guild’ by setting certain standards and maxims.  Among these was defining truth as being what all who investigated found, or the idea of replicability, and redefining historical work as an “academic” enterprise. (52)  Thus professionalization would allow objectivity to exist. 
After WWI the idea of objectivity disintegrated.  Relativism entered the scene and the fight was on.  For the war, historians had been pulled into creating history for elementary and high school.  This history was directed by the government though, seeking to indoctrinate the children with the exceptionalism of the U.S. and the greatness of American society.  Between the world wars, history was being replaced in the classroom by social studies; part history, part social science, but all relative to becoming good American citizens.  As historians relinquished pre-collegiate classrooms in the quest for autonomy, they found their salaries continually lowered by universities and the students seeking admission to those programs, who would become the next generation of historians, coming from lower social strata than before.  The interwar years saw a growing number of scholars who pushed the idea of conflict and disunity, something the older generation vehemently sought to avoid with its Civil War views. 
The negativity and doubt of these years were replaced with the search for certainty as WWII exploded onto the historical stage.  Relativism was linked to cynicism and nihilism as the world was divided by scholars into the “free” first world, the “totalitarian” second, and the odd men out third world. (281-3) After the war, many historians’ loyalty was questioned in the era of McCarthyism.  To be painted with the subversive brush was to see the end of even the most promising academic career.  Ideologies were the buzzword of the day, and if yours did not match the norm, then here’s your hat and there’s the door.  This mindset brought objectivity back to the fore as it became the hallmark of thought in the Free World. (299)  The war also gave birth to “Western civ” classes to help further this Free World indoctrination, while historians saw “the defense of freedom as the thread which wove American history together.” (333) The postwar years saw a rise in confidence by historians, which came from better criteria for evaluation, a dominant research ethos, and more autonomy â€“ the main desire of professionals. (361) In closing with the view of Richard Hofstadter, “if a new or heterodox idea is worth anything at all it is worth a forceful overstatement.” (338) Basically, go big or go home, which is in line with modern popular sentiments.  If we are not willing to go all-in in this game of history, then we must settle for less than the best we can be.
