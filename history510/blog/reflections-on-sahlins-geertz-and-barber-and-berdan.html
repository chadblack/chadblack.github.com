Reflections on Sahlins, Geertz, and Barber and Berdan

Posted: Sun, 24 Oct 2010

Within ethnography and historical anthropology are found some intriguing ideas about and approaches toward the gathering of historical knowledge. And the writers named above attempt, with varying degrees of success, to explain those ideas.

Of the three works, I found Sahlins’ “Other Times, Other Customs: The Anthropology of History” to be by far the least accessible. I see and understand the thesis provided on Page 518, there are some interesting historical tidbits scattered throughout, and yet I still had a difficult time discerning the manner in which all of it fits together to illustrate the point the writer is trying to make in this short but still rather prolix essay.

To a lesser extent, Geertz’s “Thick Description: Toward an Interpretive Theory of Culture” also suffers from a lack of tight focus. I understand the concept of, and have in fact practiced, thick description. Still, I found it difficult to follow the writer’s train of thought, insofar as all included concepts interrelate. On the other hand, the essay was enlivened by several engaging passages, such as these:

  • “(W)hat we call our data are really our own constructions of other people’s constructions of what they and their compatriots are up to.”
  • “(C)ulture [is located] in the minds and hearts of men” (quote from Ward Goodenough).
  • “Understanding a people’s culture exposed their normalness without reducing their particularity.”
  • “(I)t is not necessary to know everything in order to understand something.”
  • “Cultural analysis is intrinsically incomplete. And, worse than that, the more deeply it goes, the less complete it is.”
  • “(I)f you want to understand what a science is, â€¦ you should look at what the practitioners of it do.”
This latter gem encapsulates a sentiment also found in Barber and Berdan’s book, “The Emperor’s Mirror: Understanding Cultures Through Primary Sources.” Comprising an exhaustive look at the work of ethnohistorians, this work at the same time is wholly engaging and accessible; therefore, it was the reading I enjoyed most.

Although the information regarding concepts and strategies was informative and well-presented, my favorite aspect was Part 2, which dealt with methods. More specifically still, as someone with interests in language and genealogy, I was engrossed by the chapters on paleography and interpretation of names. (As an aside, though, I am a lifelong Southerner, and my family’s roots in this region stretch back some 300 years; still, I have never known anyone named “Bobby Don” [Page 136]).

All in all, I feel that Barber and Berdan succeed especially well in making clear the basics of their discipline.
