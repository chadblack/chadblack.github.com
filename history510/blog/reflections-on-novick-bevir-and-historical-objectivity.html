Reflections on Novick, Bevir and historical objectivity

Posted: Sun, 17 Oct 2010

Objectivity surely is an ideal that most of us would agree is worth striving for, even if we disagree regarding the extent to which it is attainable.

Peter Novick’s “That Noble Dream: The ‘Objectivity Question’ and the American Historical Profession” seeks to trace the evolution of attitudes toward that have accompanied the subject.

“‘Historical objectivity,’” he says, “is not a single idea, but rather, a sprawling collection of assumptions, attitudes, aspirations, and antipathies.” That said, he outlines some of those permutations, along with their causes and their effects.

In my estimation, Novick does an excellent job commingling his specific topic with the larger backdrop of American history. Therefore comprising, as it does, a more-or-less linear narrative, this work was one that I found highly engaging, and sometimes downright compelling.

His account of the rise of “professional” historians, their standards and their idea of truth was intriguing. Certainly, the 19th-century brick-making analogy was a good one.

I especially enjoyed Novick’s examination of how the historical profession’s development mirrored the socio-political history of U.S. from the Civil War era to the aftermath of World War I.

In the case of the former, I found it interesting to see another deviation from the idea that history is written by the victors: As pertains to slavery, the “War Between the States” and Reconstruction, “the southerners cared so much more about the points at issue that in the interests of professional comity it seemed wise to let them have their way.”

As for the cataclysmic effects of the “Great War,” American historians certainly were not immune, losing “the optimism and faith in progress which … had grounded their faith in objectivity,” Novick says.

It’s interesting to note that the subsequent relativism, which some said helped make World War II possible, certainly did not vanish after the latter global conflict.

And then there is this quote from Frederick Jackson Turner: “Each age writes the history of the past anew with reference to the conditions uppermost in its own time.” The fact that he wrote those words in 1891 would seem to put to rest any notion of historical revisionism as a new phenomenon.

Still, when thinking of changing attitudes toward certain once-revered historical figures and events, I cannot help but be struck by Novick’s position that in times past, “It was easy enough – indeed, requisite – that one take a loving attitude toward landmarks in the development of liberty and constitutionalism, or toward moments in the expansion of national power.”

Meanwhile, in his article “Objectivity in History,” Bevir attempts to set forth a “how-to.”

Certain of his principles, including the nature of truth and the importance of criticism, square nicely with ideas found in Novick’s work.

But Bevir takes matters a step further with his emphasis on “comparing rival webs of interpretations in terms of agreed facts,” in a “more or less continuous” fashion.

In doing so, he presents what seems to be a credible argument for a worthwhile approach to the pursuit of objectivity.
