9/26/10 Marx

Posted: Sun, 26 Sep 2010

Like some other people, I certainly found Marx easier to read than Hegel. Having never read any of Marx’s own writings before, and only being familiar with his ideas through the filter of media and education, I was excited to actually read what he wrote. I appreciated his, at least intended, approach to discuss his views in terms of “materialism,” that is, real people, real events, and real phenomena; as opposed to starting with the Ideal of consciousness and trying to relate that back to humanity. However, as I read through the sections, I couldn’t help but think that at times, Marx himself veered off into Ideals. Yes, everything was couched in terms of linen, coats, workers, bees, and use-values, but it’s still all very theoretical and full of supposition.

I found his small sections about labor conditions the most interesting, given the appalling lack of safe conditions in factories at the time; it was noteworthy, though, that Marx didn’t seem to really advance any concrete ideas toward fixing the problems, beyond reiterating how awful/unfair the capitalistic society that had created the conditions was. Also noteworthy was the way in which Marx saw the capitalistic overlords as the descendants of medieval feudal lords, thus making the wage-laborer, who sells his body, in a sense, the descendant of serfs.

Throughout, some of his terminology was difficult to latch onto, and I found myself having to flip back to previous sections to remember definitions. At times, I wondered whether all his theories would just fall apart (beyond what we know of Communism today) if the terms were defined slightly differently.

Finally, I was struck by how much the Communist theory pervaded everything in society, in Marx’s view. I especially noticed this in the “Origin of Family…” section, in which historically the wife became the proletariat. Despite what we know about current theories regarding the potential matrilineal nature of some early civilizations, I didn’t find anything in Marx, per se, that gave evidence toward this. There was simply an assertion that it was so, before linking the idea of the subjugation of women with the oppression of the worker. It made me wonder what Marx/Engels thought about women’s suffrage, or if the idea was postulated simply to further their economic ideas.
