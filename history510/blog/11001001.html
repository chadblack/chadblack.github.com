Posted: Sun, 21 Nov 2010
Who knew so many traditional archives began creating online digital versions almost from the inception of Al Gore’s internet. (Yes, I’m laughing) I remember when I first got a computer with a modem and internet connection.  My phone wouldn’t work while I was “online” and it took five minutes to get a single page, but it was from somewhere else in the world!  The job to get email and all that was on my windows desktop – the operating system had a massive 400 megabyte hard drive and weighed about 10 pounds not counting the monitor – was called Gopher, which I think was because it was developed at the home of the Golden Gophers of Minnesota.  Now, a scant fifteen years (has it been that long?) later, we surf the internet from our phones and wonder why it takes those interminable seconds for the webpage to load.  I am happy to see that there were archivists with the foresight to commence digitizing – I think it was called scanning back then – for future generations.  If you think about it, that was quite a smart move on their part to not only make it available but to preserve the manuscripts.  Instead of countless oily, salty, acidic fingers destroying the pages, unless gloves were used, then there is still the wear and tear on the old and brittle pages.  Untold numbers can now turn the pages with a simple mouse click.  Few need to actually examine the pages for their research, so this has greatly increased the ability of scholars to be more thorough in their work, which means more is expected of modern graduate students.  I can state from experience how much I prefer going over digitized and scanned records on my laptop as opposed to hours in front of the microfilm machine looking a government records – and I can’t wait for them to digitize those rolls of microfilm to make searching the records easier.

On virtual museums – yes, they are nice.  However, one can look at the pieces from the world trade center in an online museum, but when you are standing in a physical museum, staring at one of the girders that have been twisted like a plastic straw; it has the power to move you on such a deeper level.  Also, I’ve seen pictures of the Alamo, but when you are there, inside in the still quiet looking at the bullet pits in the walls, it brings you face-to-face with the sacrifices that were made.

I’ve created websites since about 1998 both for myself and for church youth groups, so I’ve been through much of what this book recommends.  I’ve also scanned into .pdf format several journals and books that I use in writing papers.  I did this for ease of access back in 2001 for a research paper.  Yes, I would prefer digitized, searchable format but all I had to work with then was my personal flatbed scanner and Adobe acrobat, which would group the scanned images into pages.  We have a better version of this now in Hodges library.

I believe though that no matter how advanced digitalization gets there is still something magical about words on dusty pages that inspires historians the world over to brave all troubles and obstacles to turn those pages.
