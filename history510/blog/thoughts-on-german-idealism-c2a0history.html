Thoughts on German Idealism & History

Posted: Mon, 20 Sep 2010

When I read Ranke, Kant and Hegel I was dumb struck on several different fronts.  In truth it was Kant and Hegel who made me struggle as Ranke I simply agreed with, for his promotion of Narrative style writing with emphasis on empirical data, i.e. primary documents won the Historiographical Debate in the end.

I take issue with Kant’s underlining theme that history has a finite end to its evolution.  He referred to  it as the “natural course” which in my view is an argument for Destiny.  History in his eyes always has a “logical end” which is in direct conflict of my understanding of history as a “Foreign Country.” A conclusion that I reached with several Historiographical including my current course that when viewing history it should be a distant thing taken into account within its historical context.

The humanist in me enjoys Kant’s belief that states which spend all of their commonwealth on war greatly hold back the “full development of human nature,” which is the ultimate goal, to unlock the betters of human nature with a society as opposed to allowing the worst of human nature which according to him lie in the individual.

Hegel was more detailed in defining history than Kant’s vague Destiny argument.  From my understanding of Hegel, there are 3 kinds of history, Original, Reflexive, and Philosophical.  Original history, in his view, is the history of Thucydides and Herodotus which take on a Political tone. Reflexive histories get divided into three sub-categories; Universal history(which concerns itself with the history of “a people or a country, or of the world” as a whole), Pragmatical history (“reanimating the past to bear upon the present conditions of things”), and Critical history(the history of history, meant to criticize historical works so as to improve them). The final form of history was the most obscure, and I sincerely did not understand it, Philosophical history which Hegel shorted as “thoughtful consideration of it[history].”
