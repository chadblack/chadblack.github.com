Week 6-- Readings on Materialism

Posted: Fri, 24 Sep 2010

     Our reading assignment this week centered on the Materialist philosophies of Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels.  I found this to be very accessible and entertaining reading, especially in comparison to Kant and Hegel.  Several passages were assigned from The Marx-Engels Reader, which is an excellent resource for understanding the ideas of these two, well, how to best describe them, philosophers, subversives, or revolutionaries? 
      In “Origins of the Family, Private Property, and the State,” I was reminded of the old maxim I’ve heard a million times: communism looks great on paper, but doesn’t work in real life.  Engels makes the same suggestion about the evolution in society from status to “free” contract.  In the family, which Engels portrays as the ‘cellular form of civilized society,’ (pg 739), the monogamous contract between man and wife is a pleasant fiction that is upheld by legal agreement that appears, at least on paper, to be of mutual benefit, although it is not. 
     Marx and Engels’ “The German Ideology” elaborates on his “materialist conception of history,” which is berates both the Old and Young-Hegelian movements for their “philosophic charlantry,” (pg 147).  For his part, Marx defends Engels and himself in materialist fashion by stating “the premises from which we begin are not arbitrary ones, but real premises from which abstraction can only be made in the imagination,” (pg 149).  “In direct contrast to German philosophy which descends from heaven to earth, here we ascend from earth to heaven,” (pg 154).  This serves as the launching off point for Marx’s materialist theory about the stages of ownership and historical development.
     In “Wage Labour and Capital,” Marx begins his preoccupation with economics that are necessary to prove his points about the development of labor and labor power in the context of a very turbulent point in history, exemplified in the revolutions of 1848.  Marx explains that Wage Labor is the social relationship between commodity producers guided by the character of the means of production.  Capital is the character of the bourgeois society’s means of production.   Marx’s conclusion is that this relationship enslaves the proletariat to capital, or subjects him to a John Henry vs. the machine scenario.  The solution: abolishing wage labour destroys capital! 
     Along this line, our reading of Postone’s Time, Labor and Social domination took a tediously pedantic look at this same economic theory of labor and capital.  Postone attempts to disect and explain “the Marxian dialectic of the forces and relations of production,” (pg263) in order to shed new light on Marx’s theories capital’s destruciton.  I found this work extremely difficult to read through without my eyes crossing.
     In “Crisis Theory,” Marx argues against criticisms that aim at explaining away economic crises in abstract terms instead of material issues such as supply and demand and the dialectic of exchange-value and use-value.  He also describes world trade crises as proof positive of the inherent contradictions in the bourgeois economy, and its natural attempt to right itself.  Marx explains that the general possibility of crisis is contained in the metamorphosis of commodity to money to commodity, or C-M-C, where the connection between purchase and sale erodes due to the fallability of the use of money as a means of payment.
