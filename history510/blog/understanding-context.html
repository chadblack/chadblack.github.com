Understanding Context

Posted: Sun, 24 Oct 2010

The Emperor’s Mirror, Barber & Berdan | “Thick Description,” Geertz | “Other Times, Other Customs,” Sahlins

The Emperor’s Mirror presented as a good introduction to the field of ethnohistory.  From an operational definition of ethnohistory as an “interdisciplinary field that studies past human behavior and is characterized by a primary reliance on documents, the use of  from other sources when available, a methodology that incorporates historiography and cultural relativism, and a focus on cultural interaction,” the authors set about to explain the intricacies of field’s methodologies and basic assumptions.  Interestingly, the authors seemingly reject the established methodologies for their own construct of “reality-mediation” in which sources are viewed as texts that mediate and simultaneously distort the external realities they record.  Within this framework, therefore, it is the historian’s task to describe the external reality as presented by sources and to explain the effect of the sources’ innate perspectival inadequacies.  On the whole, the book was interesting, though I often found the authors’ literary style lacking.  It is an excellent introductory textbook for exploring ethnohistory. 

“Thick Description” by Geertz expressed much the same sentiment.  According to him, ethnography is a field of interpretation—it assumes that “culture is composed of psychological structures by means of which individuals or groups of individuals guide their behavior” (11).  Because “culture is a context,” an unengaged objective point of view is removed from reality as the details needed to “comprehend a particular event, ritual, custom, idea, or whatever, is insinuated as background information before the thing itself is directly examined” by the source or observer (13, 9).  At its essence, then, ethnography is simply an attempt to understand culture.

Sahlins’ “Other Times, Other Cultures” sets out to prove a rather simple point that “different cultural orders have their own modes of historical action, consciousness, and determination—their own historical practice” (518).  In establishing an investigatory framework, the author argues that “history is culturally constructed from the bottom up … [and is based on] the prevailing inclinations of the people in general” (517).  History, therefore, is a given culture’s hybridized consensus of its participants’ individual actions. The author sets about supporting his position through several very complicated discussions, however, and I fear that I may have misjudged the impact of his argument.
