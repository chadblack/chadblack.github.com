Off with Their Head

Posted: Sun, 31 Oct 2010
I first opened Foucault’s “Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison” a while back.  After the first few pages I knew I would not have wanted to be a criminal, and I put it down.  After finishing it this week, it was interesting, and a little strange.  The writing was rough.  I’m guessing it was translated from French, which explains a bit.  I really hate it when a writer says something like, ‘there are four items of import. First,” and then there is never a second, third, or fourth, you just have to guess what the others are.  Once I got past that, it got a little better.  Moving from a tortured death to public executions, the idea was to make people not want to commit the crime, which was covered very graphically in the first chapter.  This was on the idea that the crime was essentially against the king, and he was exacting vengeance.
Once the move was made to reform the way punishment was handled, things began to change.  They still kept the different punishments for different social strata â€“ gentlemen could not be imprisoned by commoners, but the idea of reforming the criminal came about.  The old adage of letting the punishment fit the crime (104) and making crime not glamorous were nice.  You can see a lot of modern prison ideas in the old reforms as well.  Like the idea of a punitive city, (113) sounds like the old penal farms, and placards and hats to distinguish the inmates gave way to prison jumpsuits.  They also caught on that locking people up for all offenses would simply not work, so let’s retrain them went the idea.  Along with time off for good behavior, you can see the Protestant work ethic being administered here in the idea that if they are taught to like work they will not return to criminal ways.
The panopticon was an ingenious idea. I kind of thought of it like the surveillance cameras at the store.  You’re not sure if the camera inside that black ball is looking at you or not, and this is to keep down shoplifting.  Now, image a place where they beat you with a stick if the eye in the sky sees you get out of line.  After all this fun, we move onto the retraining of the criminal by educating them, putting them to work at different endeavors, even the idea of chain-gangs to control order.  Yet, the author seems to return all the while to the idea that all the prisoners are really learning is how to be better criminals.
In the end we see that the prison and punishment of criminals and delinquents are born within the society that produces them.  If there was a way to remove the criminal desire from the person it would be a wonderful world.  However, because there is a little outlaw in all of us, the prison will forever be within our society.
