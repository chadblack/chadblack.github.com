Post #1

August 29, 2010

Grafton, Anthony. The Footnote. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1997.

Anthony Grafton takes an incisive look at the history of the footnote and its use in historical writing. He examines the multiple uses of the footnote; to aid the reader in their scholarship, to lend legitimacy to one’s work, to give credit to previous historians, and to discredit, discount, and humiliate one’s academic rivals, past and present. He devotes about as much time to the last use of the footnote as he does to the historical development of the footnote, the first use of which he dates to be around 1700, if not a few years before. Grafton explains the various uses of the footnote well, and how they have been part of historical debate for centuries. He extends his focus to the historians who are using them, and he does well examining the scholars and periods of academia with a fair eye. He acknowledges both the difficulties the scholars faced and their often bloated sense of accomplishment. These brief biographies of the historians are insightful and fairly even-handed, offering both praise and addressing failures and short-comings. The strongest point of the book, I felt, was the chapter placing the footnote, finally, at its inception in the Eighteenth Century. He demonstrates the difficulties historians at that time faced to reassert the importance of history and the other humanities at the time of so many other scientific Enlightenment breakthroughs. To confirm the validation of the subject while radically changing its practice is certainly a difficult task. Grafton cites some historians’ articulate defense of the subject, and supports the progression of the discipline, advocating the double-narrative that historians often use today.

The book moves along at a decent pace and is well-written. It must be admitted that the author seems to greatly enjoy his own writing, and employs various turns-of-phrase and literary analogies, which are at times interesting side-notes, but often seemed to me to be gratuitous assertions of his own learning. To pay tribute to his multiple literary references, I think it fitting to compare him to the character of E. K. Hornbeck in Inherit the Wind, a play by Jerome Lawrence and Robert E. Lee. Hornbeck is a Baltimore newspaper man, who writes with a clever sophistication and “sneers politely at everything, including himself.” That being said, for what could have been a painfully dry subject, Grafton infuses a lot of life into the narrative, with his character sketches and related stories. Using the footnote as a lens, he is able not only to reveal the development of this historical apparatus, but also to offer a look into the world of historians, and the politics surrounding the study of history in each of the centuries he addresses.
