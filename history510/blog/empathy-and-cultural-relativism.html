Empathy and Cultural Relativism

Posted: Wed, 27 Oct 2010

In many respects, empathy is a polarizing concept similar to objectivity—it is either a goal to be sought or a mental construct which can cloud historians’ judgment of the evidence.  To be sure, the latter is quite plausible, and most historians would agree that identifying too closely with peoples under investigation is a danger.  Improperly used, empathy can tend towards simple imagination and perhaps even counterfactual flights of fancy.  It can also tend toward over-identification with historical actors.  Though it may be admirable and entirely warranted in certain instances, over-empathizing can cause sympathy for historical actors, which is an obvious threat to the historian’s judgment.

On the other hand, historical empathy offers a way to recognize the differentness of the past, and to understand the variety of perspectives held by historical actors.  In this sense, empathy offers a window to understanding the question of why historical groups or figures acted the way they did.  In situations with minimal or contradictory evidence, empathy could be a useful tool because it brings historical events away from theoretical constructs or dispassionate words on a source document and places it back in human scale.  In certain instances, then, empathy is a useful tool in abductive reasoning (which is much better way of describing what is otherwise historical “guesswork”). 

Empathy thus has its pros and cons, but which side is the historian to take in the discussion?  Perhaps the safest answer is to say that empathy is a useful tool to be used in moderation.  The greatest benefit of empathy is its ability to bring the story of history back to the human level so that readers may better understand the past.  Empathy also permits the historian to frame research in useful ways when sources are problematic.  Over-indulgence, however, is to be avoided as it may diminish the effectiveness of professional research.   

From this framework, understanding culture is an important part of understanding the past.  Defining culture then is a key to understanding the past.  Culture seems to be the intellectual framework within which a given society operates.  Understanding culture, then, creates a lens through which we may view the events of a given time relative to their setting and actors.  As historians, it allows us to make connections between events, people, places, movements, and ideas which may not otherwise be evident.  Culture, however, is not necessarily the most important factor in understanding the history of a given time or place; culture’s importance depends on what the evidence shows. 

Tangentially related then, is the difference between history and anthropology: anthropology, at its most basic is a search for definitions, to define the human condition; at its root, history is search to answer questions of why events played out as they did.  To be sure, there is a sense of cultural relativism which comes with connecting past events to the society which brought them about.  As historians, however, we are bound by a sense of evidentiary relativism which compels us to form our judgments according to what the sources tell us.  Cultural relativism can be used to complete the picture, so to speak, but as professionals we are tied to our sources.
