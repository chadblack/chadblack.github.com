Reflections on Weber, Wood and capitalism's genesis

Posted: Sun, 10 Oct 2010

“Capitalism is the astounding belief that the most wickedest of men will do the most wickedest of things for the greatest good of everyone.” – John Maynard Keynes
“The inherent vice of capitalism is the unequal sharing of blessings; the inherent virtue of socialism is the equal sharing of miseries.” – Winston Churchill
“History suggests that capitalism is a necessary condition for political freedom.” – Milton Friedman

Surely, few would debate that capitalism is by now a significant global phenomenon. However, just as that system’s merits are debatable, so, too, are its origins.

Max Weber’s pioneering 1904-05 work, “The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism,” was his attempt to frame that economic system’s start in socio-religious terms.

In the process, as he delves into the Reformation, he invokes the specter of predestination. And here he delivers one of his most intriguing ideas. Per that particular doctrine: Folks’ eternal destination was already decided, and there was nothing they could do about it. Per Weber’s corollary: For many, the suspense was too much to bear, naturally, so they sought a sign; they worked to show themselves that they were approved unto God, as secular success must mean heavenly sanction.

How bizarre. How paradoxical. And yet, how viscerally satisfactory.

So it is with many of Weber’s explanations. His is ultimately a well-reasoned, if rather unscientific, treatment (as noted, criticized and/or excused in the Norton Critical Edition’s “Contexts” section).

Ellen Meiksins Wood, in “The Origin of Capitalism” (1999), likewise presents a wholly plausible account. However, her approach – framed in socio-cultural terms – seems more balanced than Weber’s, or, put another way, less encumbered by a predestined conclusion.

For example, her ideas regarding capitalism’s specific provenance are more convincing than Weber’s rather vague views on that count.

Meanwhile, her key criticism of attitudes whereby capitalism’s emergence was “the realization of ever-present tendencies” is hammered home a bit too frequently, perhaps, but still is well-taken.

All in all, I find both works to be quite compelling in their own ways – Weber’s as a triumph of the imagination, Wood’s as an exercise in thorough, unremitting rationality.
