hist510- Foucault

Posted: Sun, 31 Oct 2010

 

When I first discovered that the book for this week was written by Michael Foucault, I must admit I was less than excited. I have not had much direct exposure to Foucault prior to reading this book, but what brief writings I had read and from hearing discussion of his idea, I was fairly sure that I would not enjoy his study. Although Discipline and Punishment is still far from my favorite book assigned throughout this class, it was nonetheless much more enjoyable than I had anticipated. My biggest complaint stems from a personal distaste for strongly theoretical works, which Foucault’s certainly is. The ideas he presents are interesting and thought-provoking, but I cannot bring myself to enjoy theoretical analysis as much as narrative forms of history. On the positive side, however, I found the foundational subject for this book, prisons and the shift in penal punishment, to be incredibly interesting. This is not a topic with which I am very familiar and I greatly enjoyed Foucault’s treatment of the material. Another factor that stimulated my involvement with the book was t its surprisingly brutal nature. I appreciated Foucault’s decision to incorporate such stories simply because, for me, the vivid descriptions helped counteract the somewhat callous and disassociated tone of the book. 

It took a great deal of time and rereading to get through Discipline and Punishment, and even at the end I was unsure I completely understood all of Foucault’s arguments. He puts forth a great deal of constructed relationships to help explain the shift in punishment that occurred from the mid eighteenth to mid nineteenth century, such as power-knowledge or body-object. Often these models prove useful for understanding Foucault’s ideas, but it can be difficult to grasp the many nuances Foucault perceives to exist in these relationships. To my surprise, I found myself rather convinced by Foucault’s argument that punishment shifted from a corporal form of vengeance to a disciplinary one focused on control. I believe there is much merit in his analysis of cultural shifts and their ensuing impact on how power was perceived during the advent of the modern age. Torture fell into disuse as the primary concern became the creation of “docile bodies,” which involve a subjugation of the mind- Foucault would argue the soul- rather than a subjugation of the body. According to Foucault, this necessitated the disassociation of power from the body as disciplinary institutions sought to increase the efficiency of the body while imposing a strict subjugation to ensure the power relationship. This new goal could only be achieved through constant surveillance and an ability to control. In addition, it must be achieved through non-violent means because the creation of a docile body is an internal phenomenon. Such criteria could not be met prior to the expansion of institutions and advent of modern technologies that occurred throughout into the nineteenth century.

Although I did enjoy this book, I would have like to see an in-depth expansion of Foucault’s analysis beyond France. He addressed other countries as well, but I wondered to what extent his conclusions would apply to other countries, such as Spain, had they received the same level of attention as did France. Perhaps one of the most enjoyable aspects of this book was its though-provoking nature; I certainly reflected more upon this book and its implications than I do in most. I am not an unconditional supporter of Foucault’s power theory, but I do find this interpretation to be very interesting because of its broad applicability. Personally, and I believe this holds true for most of Foucault’s readers, I could not read this book without reflecting on contemporary penal institutions and the nature of the power-knowledge relationship in today’s society. After reading and reflecting, I finally came down on the favorable side of the book despite my initial misgivings. Regardless of one’s personal feelings toward Foucault, I believe that at least an awareness of his theories is important for any professional historian.
