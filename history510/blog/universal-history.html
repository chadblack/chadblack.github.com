Universal History

Posted: Sun, 19 Sep 2010

This is a brief synopsis of three multifaceted and prodigiously expressed beliefs on Universal History by three historians: Immanuel Kant, Leopold von Ranke, and Georg Hegel. All three ascribe to the notion that human beings can advance and are in fact in a perpetual state of improvement. They see history as following a natural course, the study of which must balance the particulars with an understanding of the general trend leading on toward perfection. They each seem to combine scientific ideas with a deep spirituality, whether by asserting that Reason can be used to understand universal truths and divine plans, that evolution does point toward an ultimate goal, and/or that humanity is perfectible with careful study and an inspired capacity for change. The task of discovering these truths is a very difficult one, but one that must be attempted, and studied with great care.

Despite their similarities, the progression of mankind is understood in slightly different ways by each of them, and their views of Universal History are shaded accordingly. Kant writes that human advancement comes from “social unsociability.” It is a competitive drive that lifts humans above other animals, as men both want and detest the society of their fellows. Von Ranke, the continual proponent of archival research, argues that the facts take preeminence in all historical works, with interpretation second, since generations and new documents can sometimes disprove interpretation, but strict reconstruction of how things were will withstand time. I picked the heading of my blog simply in jest, as a play on words of an all-too-frequently used phrase, but von Ranke would maintain that this is, in fact, what we do and our highest obligation. And it is from this careful attention to things as they really were that we have the best chance at correctly identifying “the event itself in its human intelligibility, its unity, its diversityâ€¦we deal with humanity as it is, explicable or inexplicable; the life of the individual, of generations, or nations; and, at times, with the hand of God above them.”
Hegel evaluates that role of nature in human evolution. This idea clearly has a root in the earlier writings of Kant, who professed that human beings, like Nature, follow universal laws and evolve. However, Hegel argues that while Nature exists in cycles, the Spirit can be improved toward perfection, and in this humans derive their ability to progress and create(54).

I would argue that these men, despite some of their own claims to the contrary, are much more similar than they are different. The focal point of their conjectures(that humankind is progressing toward perfection in accordance with a divine plan and that the stages of this progression can and should be identified) is accepted by all three, but this is the point of reasoning that I would think many scholars would choose to question today. I found these studies to be very interesting, as both the obligation and limitations of academic history seemed to be laid out in terms of spirituality, which to me, seemed somewhat contradictory to these same scholar’s commitment to scientific precision and detachment. Still, I enjoyed these men’s contribution to a very serious question: can mankind actually improve?
