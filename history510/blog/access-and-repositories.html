Access and Repositories...

Posted: Sun, 21 Nov 2010
Archive Stories, edited by Antoinette Burton promised to give us an inside look at not only the creation of archives, but their maintenance, protection, and access.  Just the name, archives, conjures up for me images of rows of books on shelves with those rolling ladders to access them; the hidden finds that await the turn of a musty page inside a dusty book.  Thrilling just to think about it isn’t it?  However, these were not those stories. Von Ranke is not hunched over a giant book in these.  Rather, it is the real archives that we as historians may face regardless of our specialty.  I found the information contained in this book enlightening.  It introduced ideas about how to deal with archivists we may encounter at the various repositories.  Who would have thought that simple items like melons (57) that we think little of would be the key to getting more information and access from the gatekeepers of the archives?  I can see how others would want to protect certain information from becoming public as it would force questions they did not want to ask and answers which they did not want to seek.  Many people like their version of history as it is; boiled down to a working definition of what their people believe.  Ghosh exposed the ways archivist protect their warehouse of knowledge.  It also hinted that the archivists knew the information she was after was in their charge and were very unwilling to give it up.  I am glad that, instead of destroying information they did not like, as did the Spanish with the Mayan codices, they acknowledge their historical value and tried to protect them with cajoling and condescension until they had to give up the information.  This was also what the archivists did in the Praslin case; refusing to turn over historic documents knowing they were to be destroyed. 
I love to see that oral histories are beginning to see acceptance, as in the court case in British Columbia.  With legal precedent set, I wonder how this will affect the view many now hold on such archives.  And what about the living archive in the Terasita la Campesina story?  I would hope that the author recorded some of the information she seemed to freely give as it would provide different perspectives on gender and sexuality that ones like Butler, Scott, and Bennett may find useful in their arguments.
Turning now to the U.S. of A.; it is amazing how many hoops you have to jump through to get at information.  I understand the problems with personal information and ID theft, the need for redaction of information provided, and the need to keep that information secure.  However, I wonder if the director decided to write his own history of the passport after he heard the author’s desire to do the same.  And having the power to prevent access to the information until his was complete, the foot-dragging began.  It makes you wonder if, in having to spell out what, when, where, and why you want/need information from the US government only leaves you open to having your ideas co-opted by the very officials in charge of helping you.  I know, conspiracy theorist love this stuff but it’s still interesting.

I want to close with this thought about the importance of archivists. Most have probably seen the Lord of the Rings Trilogy.  Remember when Gandalf told Frodo to keep the ring safe and secure, then left to consult some information?  Did anyone catch that while he was reading all those dusty books there was an archivists bringing them to him.  How about that?  A middle earth archive with a reading room and an archivist â€“ nice touch of realism since some would have simply had him grabbing books off the shelf and thumbing through them.
