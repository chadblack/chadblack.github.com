Thoughts on "How to Write the History of the New World"

Posted: Sun, 12 Sep 2010

At the end of his introduction and thorough outline of his book, Jorge Canizares-Esguerra  managed to get my attention blatantly declaring war on what he calls North Atlantic-centric historiography.  He calls the understanding  of the what the West is as “amusingly Pompous.”    He openly challenges what is expected of him as a specialist of Latin American history by saying “I have learned that the public expects from historians of the region cautionary tales of revolutionary violence and, if socially conscious, stories of cunning peasants resisting treacherous oligarchs.  I am a storyteller of a different kind, who believes that there ought to be other tales for the public to consume.”  With this kind of fire in his writing I hoped for the rest of the book to channel rage constructed in such a way as to amuse me and keep me begging for more.  I was mistaken.  This book was a sincerely difficult read, but that is not to say I did not learn from it!

I like his concept of the philosophical traveler, a scholar who is “aware of the inadequacies of human perception and hence doubted the bare testimony of the senses,” and their “philosophical histories of the land and peoples of the America in which evidence from linguistics, natural history, ethnology, and geology took precedence.”

I also found very interesting the struggle that Spanish historians had in the 18th century attempting to patriotically defend their nation from historiographical attacks coming from Northern Europe.  If the Spanish historians stuck to what was “cutting edge” or I should really say “fashionable” readings of sources and writing of history then Spain would look like the “Bad guy” in history or the Ignorant in their 18th century present.

Overall, I enjoyed his attack on misconceptions and the anti-Spanish bias that seemed to plague a good deal of historiographical literature, while at the same time balancing the reading of Spanish sources with a critical eye.
