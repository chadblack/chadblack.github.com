Performance, Gender, and Contract Theory

Posted: Sun, 14 Nov 2010

Butler, Gender Trouble: Feminism and the Subversion of Identity | Pateman, The Sexual Contract

Butler’s argument was somewhat convoluted (possibly made more so by the fact that I did not have the text in my hands) and I am sure that there are many important nuances that I did not pick up on as she explained her thesis.  Such caveats notwithstanding, the main objective of her work is arguing that the manner in which feminists identify (or are forced to identify by a patriarchal system), and connect that identity to politics, is constraining because it assumes “women” are a single unified body based solely on sex.  In Butler’s opinion, accepting such a definition of gender is a logical fallacy for feminism as a political movement.  Through a deconstructionist theory, she is able to mock the idea of a unified concept of “women” behind feminism.  For Butler, the concepts of sex and gender are indeterminate.  Furthermore, concepts such as male-female and masculine-feminine are political constructs and should have no bearing on how feminists, or anyone else, identify. 

Gender, in Butler’s mind is little more than performance, and people in general, and feminists in particular should be aware of how they perform  their socially constructed roles in order to effect change.  Advancing an argument for a more fluid definition of gender rooted in self-identification and sexuality makes it easier to subvert the male-dominated culture.  Marginalized segments of society can also use fluid definitions of gender rooted in sexuality to work against the established culture.  Essentially, because women, lesbians, and gays are less engaged in the traditional male-dominated culture, they have more freedom to subvert or denaturalize the established categories of gender.

Butler’s argument is based on a definition of gender that is a political construct.  Pateman offers an exploration of just how that came to be through a reexamination of social contract theory.  Behind the rhetoric of liberty and equality espoused by proponents of the social contract, Pateman argues there is a larger unifying force of fraternity that, while eliminated inequality among men, has increased inequality between the sexes.   ”Sex” she argues “is central to the original contract,” and “civil freedom includes right of sexual access to women and, more broadly, the enjoyment of mastery as a sex—not a gender” (225).  Through extrapolations of the social contract via the marriage contract and the prostitution contract women are “tainted with the odour [sic] of slavery” (230) and subjugation by men. 

Pateman concedes that movement for equality between the sexes has made some great advances in the last century, that women now enjoy judicial equality and the a certain equality of opportunity with men.  However, sexual harassment has been “discovered in the workplace and the patriarchal division of labour [sic] has not been greatly upset … and that women’s economic circumstances still place them at a disadvantage in terminating the marriage contract” and the act of sex has been encapsulated by a growing sex industry “which provides men with new forms of access to women’s bodies” (228).   In essence, she argues that advances have been made and they have been easily worked around because the social contract was always predicated on the subjugation of women by men.
