Kant's world view...

Posted: Wed, 15 Sep 2010

Here is my take on Kant’s “Universal History from a Cosmopolitan Point of View.” (1784). Immanuel Kant was a deep thinker, but still a man of his time.  This reading was from the period just before the French Revolution, and after the American War of Independence.  So, yes it would seem as if the world was in chaos, with different groups opposing each other looking to assert their collective will.  His introduction seems to be an attempt at soothing anxious Europeans in saying that the destructiveness will work out in the end for the betterment of man.  His first thesis seems to sum up the idea of the Enlightenment â€“ in which humans are progressing to a natural end through their own ability to reason; with the understanding that this ‘natural’ end is far better than the current state of human affairs.  His second thesis would appear to answer why there are peasants and nobility, ignorant, slow, normal, smart, and gifted individuals by saying that the human race itself advances, but not all individuals move at the same pace. (Good statement of the obvious) Thesis three appears to set the stage for man taking credit for the bettering of life through his own abilities, as if to say it was done without divine intervention.  Thesis four comes off to me as a justification for taking Amerindian lands.  Thesis five, six, and even seven are the framework to create this ‘just constitution’ Kant wants.  Thesis eight and nine are the justification for the need of universal constitution between countries.
In all this Kant appears to head toward a humanist idea of the world.  A world in which we humans make it on our own, and without any help from above.  However, he circles back toward ‘Providence’ at least to link the deist idea to the Enlightenment where God, or Providence, watches with approval what the reasoning men have created.
What else can be learned from this excerpt from Kant’s work?
