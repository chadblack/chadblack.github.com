Reflections on Marx-Engels and Postone

Posted: Sun, 26 Sep 2010

Socialism, communism, Marxism. These ideologies, as grounded in the works of Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, unquestionably changed the world. They were espoused by forces of political revolution in Russia and China, which in turn spread/imposed those ideals within their spheres of influence.

An overly simplistic summation, certainly, but it gets to the heart of a notion that for decades was embraced by the “the Iron Curtain” and feared by the capitalistic West.

Certainly, Marx’s words leave no doubt as to the mutual exclusivity of those two worlds. As he wrote in “Wage Labour and Capital,” “(E)very social reform remains a utopia until the proletarian revolution and the feudalistic counter-revolution measure swords in a world war.” Scary stuff.

That “real movement which abolishes the present state of things” (as described in “The German Ideology”) was to represent “an act of the dominant peoples ‘all at once,’” leading ultimately to a classless society.

Sounds good – in theory.

But for all their expounding of economic principles, for all their historical ruminations, for all their cynicism, Marx and Engels display remarkable naivete as regards human nature.

So it is that the Soviet Union and Red China cannot be said to represent anything even close to a true “dictatorship of the proletariat.” So it is that the former, economically and morally bankrupt, collapsed in the early 1990s. So it is that the latter, with its billion-plus consumers, has become quite willing to do business with, and to a growing extent emulate, the capitalist world (although one wonders if this phenomenon could to some extent play into Lenin’s prediction that “The Capitalists will sell us the rope with which we will hang them”).

At any rate, Marx wrote that “We shall try to make our presentation as simple and popular as possible” (“Wage Labour and Capital”). The extent to which he and Engels succeeded in this regard is debatable. But Postone’s “Time, Labor, and Social Domination: A Reinterpretation of Marx’s Critical Theory” does little to further the cause.

By and large, and perhaps of necessity, Postone merely restates Marx’s ideas in different words. He occasionally does offer interesting elaboration or clarification. But original insights seem few and far between. Among them, however, is his assertion that “Marx’s conception of the social relations constituted in the sphere of production cannot be understood solely in terms of class relations of exploitation.”
