Foucault and Power

Posted: Fri, 29 Oct 2010

When I started reading this book, two things struck me immediately—first, this was much more interesting than I expected, and secondly, I had no idea how it connected to historiography.

I quickly got at least one of the points. The opening section of Discipline and Punish shows a fundamental shift in Western thought not only on punishment but on the concept of the body and personal rights. The section, Torture, discusses the marked change from atrocity to humaneness in punishment rituals, looking at how it went from reflecting the horror of the crime physically back onto the criminal to having to demonstrate the humaneness of the ruler. This is really a description of the power dynamic between ruler and people; a breach of the law is a personal affront to the sovereign, requiring a display of the sovereign’s power in order to rectify the challenge to his authority. The punishment is a ritual that confirms the ruler’s power, and the societal model based on the ruler’s power—the torture, the punishment, the confessions, etc., all are part of a rectifying of a breach of power.

The idea that comes across most heavily, at least to me, is that culture and society are based on power relationships, and within this text punishment and law are the power relationship between the sovereignty of the ruler and/or the state in relation to the people. Activities like torture, imprisonment, etc., are all “rituals of power,” the trappings of these power relationships that allow it to function.

The changes throughout the rest of the book, the change in punishment in general, is representative of both a change in government and a change in the perception of the body and its relationship to individual rights.

Foucault wasn’t as intimidating as I thought. This is an interesting book, because I would not have thought that you could use such a specific historical analysis to explain a greater philosophy of history, but it worked out really well.
