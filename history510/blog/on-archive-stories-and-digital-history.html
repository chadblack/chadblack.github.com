On Archive Stories and Digital History

Posted: Sun, 21 Nov 2010

Our readings for this week were Archive Stories: Facts, Fictions, and the Writing of History, and Digital History, A Guide to Gathering, Preserving and Presenting the Past on the Web.  The common thread I found in the collection of essays that make up Archive Stories, and the web publication of Digital History  are the common issues of legitimacy that crop up when historians use archives to perform their research.  Whether these archives are found via the internet, which holds millions of archives, created by individuals and professional organizations, or whether the archives are found in some dusty government repository in India or South Africa, problems exist as to how, by whom and why these archives were formed, what purpose they served then, and what purpose they serve now. 

As John Randolph points out in his chapter in Archive Stories, “The Bakunin Family Archive,” (209-232), the essential problem is one of control.  There is very little positive control over most of the content published on the internet.  There is also a lack of permanency to it as Renee Sentilles points out in the “Archives of Cyberspace.”  These are particular issues discussed in Digital History as well. According to the authors, one is left having to rely on the general concensus among historians on what is considered legitimate sourcing on the internet, like Early English Books Online, and what is considered ‘tertiary,’ like Wikipedia.  However, as Archive Stories points out, there are problems of legitimacy with the traditional archive as well. 

In contrast to the internet, many archives have issues with too much control over their content.  Barriers to archival research exist between historians and those who have political and cultural control over archives, such as the Bakunin Family Archive, or the Uzbekistan archives.  these archives serve as “contact points” (pg28) between archivists and historians that often spark debate over what versions of history may be viewed negatively by individuals with vested interests in maintaining, even moulding, the ideas the archives have come to represent.  According to our authors, many archives (especially national archives) represent ethereal monuments, and to tamper with them is to invite opposition from those who do not wish to see these monuments disassembled and rebuilt into unfamiliar and/or unwanted forms.
