Gender Troubles...

Posted: Sun, 14 Nov 2010
As I began the preface, I realized this would probably be a confusing book for me.  Philosophical works are barely on the main deck, let alone in my wheelhouse.  With that said, here goes.  Upon reading the 1990 preface, I came to only one conclusion; she may have been using English, but it wasn’t any English I understood. “As a strategy to denaturalize and resignify bodily categories, I describe and propose a set of parodic practices based in a performative theory of gender acts that disrupt the categories of the body, sex, gender, and sexuality and occasion their subversive resignification and proliferation beyond the binary frame.” I have no idea what this means.  Maybe if I read it a few more times it will start to make sense.
Her 1999 preface made more sense; at least I understood it better.  Maybe that is why she wrote it.  She said the book was rooted in “French Theory” with some feminist theory thrown in for good measure, so I was prepared for another round of the man-haters club.  However, she was on a separate course.  She wants her lifestyle choices to be accepted by all. (xix) This sounds a little hedonistic to me, but it would seem she had a rough go of it early and that made her a little mad at the world and this was her venting session.  I can understand that.
As she begins, we learn that “woman” and “gender” are political constructs that limit what a person actually is.  She says the definition of ‘women’ has been harder to redefine that ‘patriarchy’. (7)  She asserts that “gender is culturally constructed: hence, gender is neither the causal result of sex nor as seemingly fixed as sex.” (9-10) She speaks about Monique Wittig and the desire to use “person” to remove gender and the baggage that comes with it.  This would seem a good idea.  Think about it, does the phrase, “That is one happy kid,” seem more open to interpretation than, “That is one happy boy?”  By using a tag, boy/girl, the connotation of the statement is altered. 
As Butler is talking about Douglas’ ideas about how bodies are inscribed by our culture to designate them male and female, it got me to thinking about how they are inscribed. I was expecting thoughts on tattoos, piercings, head shavings, and the like.  I found none of that.  However, I would have thought she would have pulled tattoos into the mix.  Wasn’t this a common male custom, especially sailors?  Now, I’ve seen women with tattoos on their chest at Belk in Knoxville – it’s a little weird.  It would seem this would fall under gender questioning wouldn’t it?  To address the elephant… it’s weird because I’m a little old fashioned.  Discreet tattoos on females may be okay.  Unless you’re a sailor or a member of a tribe, that really cool tattoo is going to look funny when you’re 75.  A few piercings may be cool, but if refrigerator magnets stick to your face, or anywhere else, that may be a bit too much.
In the words of Chief Ten Bears, “That is all I have to say.”
