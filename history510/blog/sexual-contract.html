Sexual Contract

Posted: Sun, 14 Nov 2010

The Sexual Contract by Carole Pateman examines the historical subjugation of women as a necessary part of social contract history. She argues that patriarchy was not overthrown by the shift to representative government, but rather that it has taken on a new modern form. Pateman argues that despite some problems inherent in the term “patriarchy,” it still best describes the multifaceted social control that males have over females. She evaluates many contract theorists and argues that their perceptions of women are crucial for fully understanding their contract theories. Women are conspicuously absent in many historical accounts of social contract theory, but Pateman makes clear that they are not ignored by the traditional theorists, nor are they treated neutrally. With the exception of Hobbes, all traditional theorists believe women to be innately inferior to men, and as such are not fit for citizenship. Women cannot be “individuals” in the same way as men. Pateman cleverly points out the contradiction in this argument, however, as women are expected to engage in marriage contracts and contracts can only be legitimized between two consenting individuals. She also makes a few interesting comparisons to slave contracts(also inherently contradictory) and marriage, in that both result in civil death and the loss of one’s autonomy over one’s own body.

Where the book seems to falter, in my mind, is the periodic victimization of the women discussed within the book. While Pateman argues at the beginning that women should not be viewed as passive victims of a corrupt system, her language obscures this fact at later points in the book. In her articulation of male sex-right over his wife, a fully legitimate example of male dominance, she seems to deny that females also have a desire to engage with their partner sexually. The marriage is not complete until it is consummated, she interprets as male sex-right, though it could just as easily be interpreted as a mutual sexual act, releasing both spouses from social restraints against premarital sex. She also omits the religious significance of the act as two bodies becoming one, a belief she seems to deride as a facade for male lust in other parts of the book, in favor of a Freudian interpretation. She does seem to forestall some of this criticism by insisting that while many couples are not oppressive, the institution itself cannot be separated from its inherent subjugation of women. Still, she seems to imply that the sexual appetite of the wife will never be satisfied to the extent of the husband, a statement that would need more evidentiary support.

Elsewhere, she makes the case for despotic authority more persuasively. Women are forced into marriage by societal expectations and economic restrictions and then surrender their labor power, personhood, and sexual capacities- this part of the argument is well supported. Though sometimes couched in dismal language, she remains convincing that this grim portrayal is warranted by the experience of many married women, and more importantly, by the capability of all married men to become tyrants(whether or not they would, personally).
