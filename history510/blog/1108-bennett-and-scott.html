11/08 Bennett and Scott

Posted: Mon, 08 Nov 2010

I really enjoyed Bennett’s book, though I have a few small points of disagreement about it. She argues that feminist activism and history should be more closely aligned, and she presents reasons for why they have diverged since second wave feminism took hold in the 1970s: the need for women’s history to be accepted, presentism, and the move away from history by feminist scholars in literature and other fields. The discussion of presentism, and Bennett’s negative view of it, form a strong secondary thesis to the book, one that I, as a medievalist, appreciated.

As a TA this semester who’s seeing a western civilization textbook for the first time (my undergrad survey professor hated them and assigned us several primary sources instead), I certainly see Bennett’s point about women being added on as an afterthought in the master narrative. At the same time, I think she, at times, goes a little too far: women certainly should be included in narratives of the medieval period, but Christine de Pisan, for example, while interesting, doesn’t merit the same weight as Gregory of Tours. At the same time, women such as Eleanor d’Aquitaine and Emma of Normandy certainly deserve more attention than they receive. They are the “women worthies,” but as Bennett says, it’s not hard to include “…and nuns” when discussing monasteries.

As said, I appreciated Bennett’s defense of the medieval period throughout in her assertion that feminist scholars need to look beyond 1800 in order to examine continuities and changes. Events in the 1800s, whether concerning women or men, sometimes have roots in earlier time periods, and those should be considered. I disagree, however, in her attempts to make the past relevant to students today. I agree that the “past is a foreign country,” and while we can find parallels to today’s society, the idea that Value X is important today just as it was in 1300 is uncritical. Past events can inform what we do today, but finding those links isn’t the goal of history.

I was already familiar with the ideas in Scott’s article, that “gender” should have more theoretical meaning, and that the construct of gender is different from biologically-determined “sex.” However, it was nice to read the argument in her words.
