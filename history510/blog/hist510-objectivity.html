hist510 Objectivity?

Posted: Sun, 17 Oct 2010
When I first began reading That Noble Dream, I was initially impressed with Novick’s approach and the clarity of his analysis. Although I remained favorably inclined to these aspects throughout the remainder of the book, my enthusiasm for the Novick’s work diminished somewhat the more I read. To be fair, my relative disinterest stemmed solely from preconceived expectations that remained unfulfilled and not from any shortcomings within the book itself. My biggest shock was the degree to which Novick focused on institutional and technical elements of the historical profession, such as the shifting status of professional historians and changing job prospects, to name a few. Upon reflection, however, I acknowledged that incorporating such information was simply weaving in yet another important strand in the history of objectivity in America. 

Perhaps my biggest complaint with Novick’s book is the somewhat negative attitude he adopts toward American historiography. In the interest of objectivity, I realize that my offense probably stems from an innate sense of patriotism that resists the suggestion that American historians lack the finesse, skill and creativity of European historians. While I did not feel that these statements were direct criticisms of American historiography, I nonetheless wondered how different a book on objectivity in America would have been if written by an American, particularly the link between European historical developments and their ensuing influence in America or if such as link would even have been perceived. Once again, however, my personal complaint does not detract from the overall authority of the book.

I believe that overall That Noble Dream is a well-researched study that provides an informative and enjoyable overview of the role of objectivity in the American historical profession. I liked that Novick used the concept of objectivity as a focal point and examined how American historians have regarded and/or sought the realization of this goal over the last one hundred years. I was surprised at the degree to which Novick related internal academic developments to external forces, although I absolutely agree that approaching objectivity in this manner is critical to truly understanding America’s “quest for objectivity.” Historians recognize that World War I and other major developments impacted nearly very aspect of American life, but rarely have I considered the intellectual and institutional ramifications of such events within an academic context, as does Novick. 

I thoroughly enjoyed the complicated narrative of objectivity laid out by Novick; I did not get the sense that he attempted to constrain the history of objectivity into a neatly developmental path. Rather, he embraces the challenges this ideal faced as well as stimulants contributing to its furtherment in American history, both of which combined to create an intriguing picture of how this most elusive concept has contributed to the historical profession. As intended, Novick’s study highlighted the undeniable relationship of objectivity to the American historical profession. Objectivity is not just an unreachable ideal that historians maintain in the interest of professionalism. It is and has been an important factor influencing American historiography, just as developments within American historiography have affected professional interpretations of objectivity. The mutual influence of this ideal with other aspects of the historical profession was one of my favorite parts of this book. I must also admit that I enjoyed the manner in which Novick treated historical study in America as a developing profession; he advanced a theory that assumed a more evolutionary and time-consuming process of professionalization than is frequently put forth. More importantly, however, he examines how the search for objectivity played a critical role in this process of professionalization. Overall, I found Novick’s book to be informative and thought-provoking. It is certainly a long read, but one well worth the effort.
