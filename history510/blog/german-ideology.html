German Ideology

Posted: Sun, 26 Sep 2010
Okay, I’m a little lost in all this.  Are Marx and Engels pro-Hegel or anti-Hegel, or do they just not care because it all concerns religious matters not political ones.  Marx moves from the philosophical, Hegelian, concerns to real human beings in historical settings.  It all comes to that division of labor angle for Marx, starting with ownership.  Tribal, communal and State, Feudal, and Monarchal all rose and fell through conquest.  His idea of ascending from earth to heaven (154) says a lot about his humanist tendencies.  He wanted real life, which is where he felt positive science began. (155)  History in four moments is interesting; you live, you have needs, you procreate, then you interact.  Now we come to division of labor and private property, along with oppression and want.  It comes to a call for communism, and a fight against the enslaving world market.  Once national and local barriers are removed, communism will allow the proletariat to have all things in common, and then will history truly have meaning.  Until that time, history is separated from ordinary life. (165)  Marx explores location as important in the production of historical consciousness.  The French and English follow political illusion, but the Germans operate on religious illusion; the Hegelian philosophy.  This is where the starting point of history is, the religious man puts the production of fancies in place of the means of subsistence and of life itself.  Marx seems more anti-religious than anything else.  Not just with the religious “fancies,” but also in saying that the “realm of God” existed only in the imagination. (166)

The basis of ideology: town vs. country/ burgher vs. lord / laborer vs. serf
The town provided order, defense, and a political association.  This was the beginning of the separation of capital and landed gentry.  In the towns, capital was from individuals; the merchants emerged as the separation between production and commerce.  The burgher’s ability to organize their town for defense and to link it with other towns for information and trade raised their class against the nobility.  After feudalism had succumbed, the guilds were the new threat to this new class of elites.  The merchants led to the manufacturers who could produce in quantity for large markets.  This in turn led to big industry and world markets, which Marx claims as the beginning of world history. (185) From this we see capitalists appear along with the working class, proletariats.  Marx’s main idea though is communism, where the individual subjects the material power and abolishes the division of labor.  So then, the proletariat must overthrow the state to assert itself. (197,200)  With all this going on, you can see why the Germanic area was also the physical battleground of Europe as well as the philosophical.
