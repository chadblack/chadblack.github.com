hist510-- digital and archives

Posted: Sun, 21 Nov 2010
I must admit that I was slightly nervous upon hearing of the subject of our readings this week; I did not necessarily expect bad things from these two titles, I was simply leery of the technological focus. As most people have figured out, technology is not one of my strong points and I worried my unfamiliarity with computers and whatnot might hamper my understanding of the author’s arguments. I was quite relieved, therefore, to discover that I had no problem following the readings. In fact, I had a much easier time with these books than several others we have read throughout the course and found myself even enjoying them at times, something I expected from Archival Stories but certainly not from A Digital History. Personally, I can say that these two books have been the most practically useful texts assigned. Numerous professors and colleagues have attempted to persuade me of the advantages to be gained by a more extensive use of technology, but none have done so with as much success as A Digital History. I also appreciated how Archive Stories illustrated the constructed nature of historical archives, dispelling the illusion of immutable truth-holding that surrounds popular conceptions archival collections.

Somewhat unexpectedly, A Digital History was my favorite of the two books assigned. I thought Cohen and Rosenaweig put admirable effort into persuading academics to partake more actively in the transition of historical document from paper to digital storage devices. The authors abstained from promoting an absolute opinion on either the usefulness of technology or its potential dangers, instead opting for a middle track that focused on how to maximize the advantages posed by technological developments while avoiding, or at least minimizing, some of the more detrimental effects. Some of the benefits the book advanced I had not previously considered, such as the manipulability of textual evidence â€“ an idea that could entail something as simple as the ability to simultaneously compare, contrast and arrange evidence from a single location. The dangers of historical expansion onto the web are problems I am more familiar with, a result, I am sure, of the ultra-conservative beliefs espoused by my advisor as an undergraduate. 

To be clear, I acknowledge and appreciate how much easier online access to thousands of historical documents has made historical research. I certainly have benefitted from this development and I am sure my dependence will only grow with time. But I also agree with one of the primary dangers Cohen and Rosenzweig point out: the internet’s capacity to blur the boundary between professional and amateur work. I find the idea of amateur work functioning as historical truth inherently distasteful; although some claim historical credibility, the vast majority either regurgitate traditional interpretations or present ungrounded arguments. Because this material can be found on websites that somehow incorporate the word “history” or “historical,” words that apparently entail legitimacy in the eyes of the general public, “true” history remains in the background. On the other hand, I must admit that I am happy the general public is gaining at least some exposure to history. Since the expansion of history onto the web, public interest in the past had increased substantially and I simply cannot perceive this as a bad thing, despite the occasionally questionable nature of their information.

I am not sure what I expected from archive stories, but what I read was certainly not it. I suppose that I imagined a book focused the stories created in archives, a view the authors directly sought to challenge by addressing instead the way in which archives themselves are created. This collection of archival stories did an excellent job at this goal, collectively illustrating the numerous ways in which personal experience and the history of archives affects the narratives that emerge. Although the idea that archives are constructed seems logical once one thinks about it deliberately, it is not a consideration that would have remained at the forefront of my thoughts when doing archival research.  After reading this text however, similar to my mindset following our readings on objectivity, I will be much more conscious of archival flexibility. After all the complications we listed when discussion the quest for objectivity, Archival Stories seemed to add yet one more layer between “the truth” and historical interpretation. I also agreed with the argument for the influence of current pressures upon interpretations of the past, another factor that must be considered when constructing historical narratives. The idea that individuals exist under political and social pressure is nothing new, but I had not really considered the impact of such circumstances of institutions and documentary preservation, two elements crucial in the construction and maintenance of historical archives. This book, too, is a useful guide to policing my future endeavors to accurately and successfully pursue historical research.
