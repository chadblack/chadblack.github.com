On American trends in Objectivity and Relativism

Posted: Sun, 17 Oct 2010

Our readings this week were thought provoking accounts on the various manifestations of Objectivity and Relativism theories in American historiography.  We read That Noble Dream: The “Objectivity Question” and the American Historical Profession, by Peter Novik, and an article from History and Theory titled “Objectivity in History,” by Mark Bevir. 

I read the Bevir article first, and several questions crept to mind.  In employing objectivity to historical research, how would you work out problems of commensurability when the worldview of, for example, a medieval knight cannot be worked out from a twentieth century historian’s point of view?  In this sense, the rationalization on pg 233 that “facts” can be related to each other to get at truth, is not enough tou counter the possibility of specious correlations by the author, which is a question of relativism.  I felt that Bevir did not address this problem sufficiently.

I need not have felt alone in this assessment, however, That Noble Dream was filled with many objections to the aspirations of objectivity by historians throughout America’s history of the profession, which began in the 1880s.  Novick’s thesis is that, in general, ojectivists won out over the majority of the historiographical timeline due to extenuating circumstances in American history such as World War II and the Cold War, which gave rise to conformist attitudes among historians in order to defeat external threats to the nation.  However, Novick credits relativists like Becker and Bean with performing “an important service in freeing historians from the belief that ‘the facts spoke for themselves,’ (pg 410), and suggests that a happy medium has developed over time despite the often heated debate between objectivism and relativism.  Perhaps subconsciously, American historians adoped a historiographical philosophy Bruce Kuklick described as that of the “ideal observer.”  According to Kuklick, this person “has the characteristics attributable to the ideal historian:” “There will be one correct evaluation in every situation calling for evaluation… It will be necessary, at least theoretically, for everyone finally to agree on the truth-value of any evaluation…”  These evaluations would then be declared ‘objective,’ (pg 409).  In the end, this is very close to Bevir’s idea of objectivity, but it also allows for an amalgum of personal judgement, which the relativists taught is impossible to avoid, and objectivism, which is an admirable goal, but is equally impossible to achieve.
