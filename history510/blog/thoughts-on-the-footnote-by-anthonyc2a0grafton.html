Thoughts on The Footnote by Anthony Grafton

Posted: Sun, 29 Aug 2010

This book was comically written and truly a joy to read.  That was sincerely unexpected as the history of the Footnote has connotations of boredom implied in the title.  Now to comment on its contents, I was pleasantly surprised to learn that it was in the field of the Footnote that modern historical disputes are “punched out.”  Grafton enlightened me of the key insult technique of “CF” in American historical work, which he then used in the same page to take a stab at a contemporary of his in this field of historiographical work.  This intellectual battleground gave a third level to a history book, the first being the narrative, the second the footnote, and within the footnote the fight between historians.

He also does an excellent job of explaining that the purpose of the footnote today is extremely varied.  There are varying schools of thought, all methodical in respect to different pieces of information.  And so the footnote allows the historian to know “the intellectual styles of different national scientific communities, the pedagogical methods of the different graduate programs, and the literary preferences of different journal editors.”(pg 13)

The original purpose of the footnotes as they developed in historiography were to provide the reader with “proof.”  Through the course of Historiography however, the “proof” gradually found its way more and more in the main narrative of the text and the footnote took the role of supplying the sources that the historian used, so as to demonstrate where the information was gathered and show the reader that an “acceptable” amount of work was done to come to the conclusions that the author developed.

Overall, I enjoyed reading this book and learning of the evolution of the footnote, and more specifically from roles that Gibbon, Ranke and De Thou played.
