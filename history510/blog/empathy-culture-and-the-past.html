Empathy, Culture, and the past

Posted: Sun, 31 Oct 2010
Just my personal opinion, but yes you need to be able to understand how a people feel about something to be able to write a proper history of them that is more than just facts and figures.  An example would probably be in order here.  Let’s take the Powhattans and the Jamestown colonists.  You can give facts and figures like, this many died, this and that happened, and so on and so forth.  However, to actually advance an idea of why this and that happened, to look at the social history of the situation, one has to get inside the mind of the participants.  In the case of the English, you can research the teachings of that time, the education that many had, the experiences leading up to the colonization, and a host of other items to enter the mindset of the people.  I think it was Thor Heyerdahl that called this emulsion in the past living history.  I’m not saying that you have to go so far as to live the experience by spending a week at the Jamestown settlement, but to speculate what the motive for actions were requires a more anthropological approach.  The idea of the past as a foreign country to me does not mean that it is something that will always be unknown.  It means that it is something that is different from my own views and perspectives and when writing about it one must always remember that yours is one view among many.
Culture is significant when it comes to history, but I would not say it is more important than personal experience or class location as it pertains to the historical character you are researching.  Looking at a society as a whole, culture is still one aspect.  There are several layers of society.  Take America for instance; we are considered to have a culture of consumerism and all are capitalists.  Yet, there are different groups within America that would differ from that assumption; like the Amish, Mennonites, socialists and communists groups, and many others.  Yes, culture is import to understand some things but it can’t answer all things.
