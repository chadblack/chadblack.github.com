On How to Write the History of the New World

Posted: Sun, 12 Sep 2010

How to Write the History of the New World, by Jorge Canizares-Esguerra is a history of the European historiographic tradition of depicting the New World.  In particular, Canizares-Esguerra deals with sixteenth through nineteenth century eurocentricism and how it affected historical works about the Aztecs, Incas, Mexica and others.  I found this to be a very interesting book because it does an excellent job of illustrating the dangers involved with dealing with subjects that are far removed from the historian by both time and geography.  The author reflects on the major conjectural faux pas of notable historians of the aforementioned centuries and their sometimes comical depictions of the peoples of the Americas. 

Evidently, at least where the Americas were concerned, the academic hubris abounded through different historiographical eras and schools of thought from Renaissance humanism and the Enlightenment to romanticism.  This is evident in many historians’ refusal to believe eyewitness accounts of Spaniards because they were thought, as a people, to be deceitful by nature.  Also, historians’ depictions of the New World always seemed to have an alternate agenda serving some political purpose or to prove an alternate point, such as Locke’s Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1690).  Possibly the most intriguing thing about this book is that it makes me wonder if we ever got historiography right?  I mean, how do you write objectively when it’s impossible to be objective?  We say that good historians simply have to work around that dilemma, but the men in this book also thought they were employing the best means of getting at the truth, often missing the mark completely.  I don’t know about us, but as for them, I think Canizares-Esguerra sums it up nicely on page 103 when he stated that “…two impediments had to be overcome to create the science of history, namely, la boria delle nazioni  and la boria de’ dotti: the conceit of nations and the conceit of the learned.”
