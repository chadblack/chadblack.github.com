Weber-- The Protestant Ethic

Posted: Sat, 09 Oct 2010
I used the 2003 Dover edition of this book, which I was told would be okay since we are to focus on Weber’s writing.  I’ll try to use chapter references instead since page numbers will not line up.  Weber undertakes the task of identifying what capitalism is by looking at it through various division of Protestantism, occasionally comparing them to Catholicism.  His overall stress on the argument seems to be that capitalism could only happen in the Occident – Western Europe and America.  In the introduction he calls it “the most fateful force in our modern life,” which is a big statement.  He further clarifies that unlimited greed is not true capitalism, and that a form of capitalism, or pre-capitalism, has existed everywhere.
He starts with the notion that Protestants make better capitalists than Catholics because the latter’s disdain for accumulation of wealth and ecclesiastical piety prevent them from making enough money to rise above the station in life wherein they were born. (Ch. 1)  He goes on to break down denominations like Calvinists, Lutherans, Pietism, Methodists, and the various Baptist sects to explain how each brought capitalism into an accepted place with the Christian faith.
As I was reading this, I got to the place in Chapter II where it said, “The sound of your hammer at five in the morning…” and I thought, “Isn’t that from Poor Richards Almanac?”  Sure enough, when I turn the page, and from then on, Weber is referencing Franklin as the quintessential worker bee of capitalism.  He also uses Franklin as a baseline when comparing the various religious groups as he worked to separate himself from any denominational affiliation.  Of course it helped that he was a deist.
One last note I wanted to bring up.  At the end of the next-to-last paragraph of Chapter III, Weber states, “At the same time we must free ourselves from the idea that it is possible to deduce the Reformation, as a historically necessary result, from certain economic changes.”  Did a lot of people in that time think that the Reformation was caused by economic forces?  That is most interesting.  He also said he believes the Reformation did not create capitalism, which is good since he first said that capitalism has been around forever.  Just my two cents.
