Marky Marx and the Funky Bunch

Posted: Sun, 26 Sep 2010
I’ve always heard that Marx was a good historian (sometimes this would be replaced with philosopher) who was a terrible fortune teller. I think, in a large way, those impressions were right.

There is, however, a lot to good to say about Marx. There are many who dismiss him simply because Communism failed in eastern Europe. That is, however, short sighted.  While many of the compliments thrown his way are summed up best when Foucault called him a “founder of discursivity”, it seems prescient to reiterate just how influential his work was: even those who disagree with him are forced, in essence, to speak his language.  However, I believe where the true dissonance appears between Marx and his detractors appears when we fail to recognize the social conditions of his day. It seems Marx had an odd connection with the Romantics for a man who disagreed so much with non-concrete or non-empirical evidence.  I wonder, too, if the Romantics on a fundamental level were more successful than Marx in actively engaged change. The “Communists” Marx speaks of– who require revolution– in one sense came before Marx in the form of Blake and Byron. They are separated now not only through history but ideologically (though both are generally and anachronistically placed on the left) in how they attempted change; for the Romantics usually fought localized wars and battles while Communists had a more global aim.

Again, these are anachronistic comparisons. Certainly, the horrors of everyday life (especially for the poor) we made all the more ghastly by the machine’s which Marx writes of, both in terms of poverty and in real violence and accidents.  Certainly, the world in which the totalitarian governments who ostensibly claimed his philosophy operated   offered an even more dangerous method of implementation.

Marx, I think, would have shuddered in fear and hatred at the government which came to be associated with his philosophy thought nothing of the people. And, perhaps, thats where the implementation of his philosophy failed: as his philosophy  was born with a bent toward a consciousness based purely on physical conditions, he left too little room for the human element to stand as a last line of defense against atrocity.
