Confronting Historical Paradigms

Posted: Sun, 03 Oct 2010

Confronting Historical Paradigms, Cooper et al.

The overall thrust of Confronting Historical Paradigms, is that the established analytical frameworks of history are inherently ethnocentric in that they are generated by a capitalist culture (either directly or by way of a response to it) that ultimately force native populations into foreign universal paradigms without regard to cultural applicability.  A prime example is the Marxist envisioning of peasants in Africa and Latin American as members of the European proletariat.  Generalization is classified as a useful tool, but this comes with an important caveat: in evaluating history the world system must not be seen as the most important factor, it must be carefully balanced with accounts of local cultures, peoples, and governments (which may defy linear metanarratives).  Thus, the authors deconstruct the monolithic world system, arguing that it is actually defined by a multitude of local factors and experiences.

The ethnocentric paradigms were difficult to give up, and scholars searched for ways to force their evaluations into the structure of existing paradigms.  By the 1980s “a series of ‘post-isms’ and events in the Third and socialist worlds all contributed to blowing most paradigmatic lids off permanently.”  In arguing that foreign or ethnocentric structures are no longer valid, the book suggests that academic openness is the key to preventing similar forced evaluations in the future.  The goal is to keep the avenues of debate open to human agency, regionalism or localism, and to include as many different perspectives as possible. 

While not perfect, when taken as a whole, the book provides a good framework for understanding the development of the different conceptual structures western scholars have used to examine non-western and colonial cultures.  Such a discussion cannot help but reveal the misconceptions created by such a system or the historical blinders it places on academics evaluating these periods and places.  Thus, while I dislike the book’s organization because I feel it presents a lack of continuity, it was a useful read.
