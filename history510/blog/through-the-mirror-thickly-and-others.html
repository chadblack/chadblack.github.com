Through the Mirror, Thickly, and Others

Posted: Sun, 24 Oct 2010
Mirror, Mirror, what can I see?
“The Emperor’s Mirror” is a deep book.  Lots of info about categorizing, analyzing, alphabetizing; if it could be zing-ed, it was covered.  Don’t get me wrong.  I found every bit of it quite interesting and informative.  I wish this book had been assigned in my undergrad days as part of the honors research program as we went over a lot of the same material but through different books and the instructor explaining these processes to us.  This one book put it all in one place.  I especially loved chapter 12.  Research design, or lack thereof, could make or break you as you push toward your goals.  This gave me much to ponder and it follows a lot of what we are pursuing in this class currently.  Finding a valid topic, getting a list of reading together, making a plan of how it fits, methods to use, things to watch out for, it is all very helpful to me.  Chapter 13 as well, Working in Archives and Elsewhere was quite helpful.  It is always important to remember that you can’t just “Google” everything.  Research has to involve going through archives, and talking with the archivist as some holdings are not listed in catalogs, but the curator knows they are there.  The last thing anyone would want to do is research something that has already been researched.  This is definitely a book I will keep to reference often as I proceed in historical research.

Thick Description
The excerpt Thick Description opens with a quick speech on trying to define culture.  Then, we get inundated with possible meanings and variations of what a wink/twitch could mean based on concepts of what our culture construes as a wink/twitch.  However, we did learn that this sorting out what it could mean is what ethnography is. (7) We also find that it is note-taking about what the ethnographer sees; this by his recounting of the sheep incident in Morocco, where we learn that identifying established codes is also important. (9) We get a decent overview of what culture is, and a little of what it is not – power.  We learn that in the end, the ethnographer studying a culture is only writing what he sees, analyzing those views, and not predicting anything from it only adding to the general knowledge about a certain people and their culture.

Other Times….
Marshall Sahlins started well wanting to support the idea of a “life of communities” (517) as opposed to an elite history.  It got murky when he began the heroic history section, but I tried to keep up.  With a history tied to kings it seemed a bit elitist, but I believe the point was the people saw their lives as tied to their king – the “cultural order” he called it. (520) That would explain why it is so hard to get a picture of regular people in ancient societies; their understanding of self was as a subordinate to those in charge.  Once he started with the New Zealand war of the Maori, I was almost totally lost.  The best I could come up with was that he was applying anthropological information to see the history of a people in a new light – one where the people constantly relive the heroic leader history of their society, just with new players.
