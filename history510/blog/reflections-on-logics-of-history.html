Reflections on Logics of History

Posted: Sun, 28 Nov 2010

History. Anthropology. Sociology. Can’t we all just get along?

William H. Sewell – in his 2005 work, subtitled Social Theory and Social Transformation – argues that social scientists and historians can and should not only respect one another’s work; they all have something to gain from an ongoing dialogue.

Historians get “social temporalities”; anthropologists get “the power and complexity of culture”; sociologists get “commitment to explanatory rigor” (p. ix). And while history has borrowed ideas from the social sciences, the reverse has not occurred with much regularity. Historians, Sewell says, “have lacked the confidence to become active participants in theoretical debates” (p. 6).

The author, based on the breadth of his self-described experience, certainly is qualified to diagnose and comment upon these dynamics. And for what it’s worth, based upon my exceedingly limited experience, his observations ring true.

For example, this semester we have spent considerable time discussing major theories of causation. Yet I have loosely inferred that theory (whether social or otherwise) is something several of my classmates have not previously dealt with extensively (whether as a matter of choice or curriculum). I certainly fit into that category. And to give a more specific example, as Sewell examines the notion of culture in Chapter 5, he invokes Saussure’s concept of semiotics – an idea I have studied, with limited results. It’s fascinating stuff, but difficult for me to wrap my mind around.

Sewell evidently has no such deficiencies. Because of his rich and varied background, he seems to know whereof he speaks. And so it is that the case he makes is not only compelling, but credible. As he presents various events and works as backdrops for possible theories of history, his is seen to be a truly well-reasoned stance.

(As a side note, I was especially intrigued by Sewell’s treatment of “history’s little accidents,” vis-a-vis Sahlins’ account of Captain Cook, in Chapter 7. There’s a great example of right place, right time becoming wrong place, wrong time. Similarly fascinating is Chapter 9’s look at the role contingency – as opposed to necessity – played in helping Marseille’s dockworker corporation attain and retain a position unusual among France’s 19th-century working class.)

Throughout this work, Sewell drops in countless salient points that serve to further fortify his standing. All in all, he lays out an impeccable case for “forms of structural thinking that are compatible with historical conceptions of temporality” (p. 15).
