The Origin of Capitalism

Posted: Sat, 09 Oct 2010
Ellen Meiksins Wood explores the origins of capitalism and comes to a unique conclusion in this book.  She first outlines the various debates and Marxist alternatives to the creation of capitalism.  However, the main trouble she has with these treatises are that they assume the germ of capitalism existed before recorded history, and the emergence of capitalism was unavoidable once the removal of those pesky inhibitions of people to acquiring wealth were removed.  She posits that “capitalism was born when market imperatives seized hold of food production.” (81)  She goes further to say that capitalism did not begin in the city but in the countryside, and the rising number of propertyless rural labor gave the landowners a surplus labor pool to use. (95,102,103) 
The idea of ‘agrarian capitalism’ foreshadowing industrial capitalism is interesting in that it is based on large land holders squeezing unproductive tenant farmers to the wall while utilizing wage laborers, which differs from the  Marxist format of landlord-tenant-laborer.  The tenant farmers would be market-dependent if their land could not produce enough to sustain them.  This led her to the idea that market dependence was the cause of mass proletarianization. (131)  Since the landlords and farmers produced more food, a larger population was sustainable.  This would allow industrialization. This larger population would naturally live in cities, which made their means of self-reproduction market-dependent just like their means of production in that they must now buy their food.  Their consumption of necessities would drive both agricultural and industrial production goals.  This is what the author claims as the basis for the capitalist mode of production. (141) 

The need to sustain a growing population required that land was ‘improved’ and not left idol, which meant increasing its output or production.  The idea of property rights became entangled with the need to improve it or lose the right to own it.  As colonization began for England in the Americas, this idea of improving the land’s production was brought along as well.  Whereas the Spanish argued the right of conquest to control their claims and the French formed working relations with Native populations, the English applied this natural law to their endeavors.  In their view, since the Indians had not fenced in any land, or tried to improve it in any way like building houses, or raising cattle, then the land was open for the English to take as they would do what God had ordained and improve the area and make it valuable. (159)  To me, this seems an underhanded way to steal from people.  Didn’t the nobility in England have land that was not used for anything except hunting?  Was it their absolute dislike that these half naked heathens had some of the best land in the world that drove them to devise such a scheme as this?  Did they actually think that history would see them as noble for providing this ‘gift’ to the backward people of the Americas?  At least the Spanish were honest in that they in essence said, “We want this and this, and we’re just going to take it.  Try and stop us.”
