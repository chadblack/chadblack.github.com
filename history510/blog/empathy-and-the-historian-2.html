Empathy and the Historian

Posted: Mon, 01 Nov 2010

I was a little sad that this discussion got cut off early last week, and I’m even more sorry that it has taken me this long comment on it. Empathy, the ability to really get into the mindset or feelings of others, is a very useful ability in life; when you tell your friends “I feel for you,” you’re at least claiming empathy, and a lack in empathy makes any social interaction much more difficult, let alone a study abroad experience or reading works in a foreign language. When you’re a historian, empathy allows you to take a relatively unbiased look at a period, to at least attempt to get inside the mindset of the historical actors and view them from their own context, rather than your own—even when you fail (as you inevitably will), at least you will have made the attempt and gotten closer than you would otherwise. In the case of the Crusades, my area of study, the use of empathy allows me to look at the sack of Jerusalem through the eyes of the fighters—tired, hungry, thirsty, baking under the sun, having undertaken the longest land journey imaginable for someone in Western Europe, harassed by Muslim armies the entire way, devoid of effective leadership, angry, filled with religious zeal… That doesn’t mean that I have to approve of their actions, or not condemn the slaughter in Jerusalem, but I can understand how it came to that.

Now, this doesn’t mean that I necessarily agree with moral relativism. I am, perhaps, preprogrammed to believe in the righteousness of my culture (ok, ok, nix the perhaps), but I think some things are wrong, and I refuse to disregard those beliefs—oppression of women, slavery, etc. etc. However, I don’t think my personal disapproval necessarily makes me a bad historian, or the fact that I think some things are universal wrongs. I can accept that, while I think they were wrong, they lived in a different time, and my views are immaterial. Will it come through in my writing? Probably. But that’s unavoidable, and at least I embrace the fact that I am biased.

Historians need empathy; but that doesn’t mean they have to approach history without any personal biases or a belief in moral/ethical relativism.
