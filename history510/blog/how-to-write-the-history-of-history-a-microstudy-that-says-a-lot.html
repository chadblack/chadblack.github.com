How to Write the History of History-- A Microstudy that Says A Lot

Posted: Sun, 12 Sep 2010

I will freely admit that I picked this book at my first presentation on a lark—I’m not a huge fan of Marxist critique, and I don’t yet feel comfortable with material culture or feminist theory, so I thought, “Hey, a book on the historiography of the New World! That sounds different.” It’s always nice when your gambles pay off.

Jorge Canizares-Esguera’s text is a fantastic analysis of the historiographic debates that emerged during the eighteenth century in Spain and Spanish America, essentially on the reliability of Amerindian sources. This is an incredible simplification of the debate, which also involved northern European views on Spanish backwardness, the effects of patriotism, and the like, but I have the opportunity to discuss them at length tomorrow night when I lead the discussion, so I just want to touch on a couple of points I found most interesting/useful. The cyclical nature of the historiographic debate interested me greatly, and I find it applicable in my own research. Renaissance humanist historians took native accounts to be of the highest value; Enlightenment philosophical historians denigrated them; the reaction sought to give them mixed status; and in the end we now view them as worthwhile again. Of course, this isn’t to say there aren’t flaws with the sources, or that we don’t have to be careful in how we use them, but in the end the use of these sources is once again admired and encouraged. The way history is thought about, the “philosophy of history,” changes, and what we think of as the correct method right now may not be viewed that way in, say, a century. Anything we write as historians may someday be slammed for using antiquated ideas, or be taught in a class simply to show how ignorant scholars used to be. It’s a humbling thought.

I also found that the arguments used by both sides, the patriotic aspects of the text, were incredibly informative. I’ve thought about nationalist histories, and I’ve also considered how patriotism affects the way history is taught in US schools, but I had not thought about the grander implications when it came to the study of history in general. The idea of the Spanish American Enlightenment, the differences between the Spanish and northern European Enlightenments, the idea that Spain was backwards, antiquated and medieval… even the general idea of a Protestant vision of history versus a Catholic one, all of these are areas I had not ever pondered. The book does a great job of showing the grander scale of the problem, while focusing on the very specific internal debates.

Final thought on the book: this book, How to Write the History of the New World, looks at such a specific time, place and problem—how eighteenth century Spanish historians dealt with the history of the New World. At the same time, it uses this narrow lens to explore how we, as modern historians, think about Atlantic history, how the greater debates of Enlightenment vs. Renaissance history were shaped, how our views on native sources, reliability of primary source, etc., were developed, and how Spanish and Latin American history has been pushed out of the realm of the “Western” world and into the fringes.

If only I could do something so incredibly complex through a microstudy.
