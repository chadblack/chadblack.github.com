Reflections on Kant's 'Idea for a Universal History From a Cosmopolitan Point of View,' Hegel's 'The Philosophy of History' and Ranke's 'The Secret of World History'

Posted: Sun, 19 Sep 2010

These writings, which span roughly a century, have much to say regarding the German Idealism shared by their respective authors.

As was typical of that movement, these writers fixated on the idea of a universal manifestation of their particular discipline. Hence their concept of “universal history,” defined by Ranke as “encompass(ing) the events of all nations and periods, with this limitation: that they be possible subjects for scholarly debate.”

Philosophy and – as the movement’s name suggests – idealism are to be found in all these works. The ways in which they diverge are a matter of degree.

The most heavily philosophical – and thus least accessible – of the three works is Hegel’s. Still, mixed in amid all the obtuseness, we find nuggets such as this: “Nations are what their deeds are.” Intriguing, too, is his expounding on the concept of freedom.

The most passionate – ergo, most idealistic – seems to be Ranke. Within his works is found the fascinating leitmotif of unattainable perfection that he nevertheless is compelled to seek. And yet fairly palpable is the joy he seems to have derived from his journey.

Finally, Kant’s work is the most succinct on both counts. Yet he still offers much in the way of intriguing insights as regards world history. However, he looks not only at the past; in a passage more concretely prescient than anything put forth by Nostradamus, Kant offers the “fantastical” idea of a “league of nations” in which “even the smallest state could expect security and justice … from a united power acting according to decisions reached under the laws of their united will.”

Ultimately, the writings noted above do much to distill the essence of those German thinkers’ major contributions to the study of history.
