Capital, Vol. 1

Posted: Sat, 25 Sep 2010
“Segui il tuo corso, e lascia dir le genti”

This spoke to me.  It is Italian, I believe.  Someone correct me if I’m wrong, but Marx was going on his own when he wrote Capital, right?  As such, this line would make sense; “Follow your own course, no matter what people say.”  And follow it he did.  This was some heavy reading for me.  Mainly because I have no financial understanding at all, so the arguments about labor, time, commodities, and money left my head spinning.  From what I can gather, he’s saying that a capitalist only lives to make more money and monopolize everything in a worker’s life right down to when he sleeps and how he raises his children.  Now that is the ultimate micro-manager if ever I’ve seen one.  I understand the part about human labor creating value, but having no value itself â€“ it only has potential to create value, and once labor has created that thing with value, labor has no value because it has been spent.  Chapter XIII is where I recognized the pointy-haired manager, whose only job is make sure everyone else does theirs.  Chapter XIV’s assembly line may have been where Ford got his notion , or Marx may have seen one operating, and I would have to agree that children would love school when it got them out of heavy labor.  Chapter XV, section 9 was curious and hopeful.  I still have yet to see the working-class take power anywhere that it was not controlled by a military leader of some kind.  It was good to see that there were those who would fight to protect children from being exploited by everyone, including their parents.  Marx does paint a grim picture of the world in control of capitalists; the few in control of the many, a surplus population ready to do the bidding of the owners of production.  There are times when Marx references scriptures, but then he wheels immediately to shift things in the opposite direction as if to say, “One is a utopia, the other reality.”  At the end, it is easy to cheer for the workers to revolt and expropriate the possessions of the few and give them to the many, especially those who have suffered under the capitalist labor system.
