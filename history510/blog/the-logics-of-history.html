The Logics of History

Posted: Mon, 29 Nov 2010

Sewell, Logics of History

William Sewell ultimately argues for an increased between history and the social sciences.  He picks certain foundational tools of historians, sociologists, anthropologists and other social scientists to provide a more articulated and nuanced approach to explaining the interrelation of the many disciplines.  Historians’ greatest advantage, Sewell asserts, is their understanding of the “temporalities of social life.”  In essence, historians understand contingency.  The social sciences’ greatest strength is “their penchant for structural thinking.”  Sociology’s foundations in theory help social scientists to understand the past and the present according to set of unchanging systems or structures of social organization and power. 

Sewell argues that history and the social sciences must bend and subject themselves to modification in order to create a unified social theory for all the disciplines.  He calls for a mixture of historians’ understanding of temporalities, sociologists’ commitment to structural explanations, and anthropologists’ recognition of the dialectics of power and culture within societies.   The categories of culture and structure, he asserts, are useful, but they must be modified to accept the idea of temporality.  Everything is contingent, and cultural and structural interpretations must be responsive to that understanding of reality. 

At the surface, it is clearly evident that history can benefit from the single causation of sociologists to help winnow our multifaceted strands of causality.  But here again, I think we come back to the issue of objectivity.  If a historian’s research is driven by a belief in the existence of a fundamental structure of power or society is he not likely to find exactly that because he reads the sources to fit his conclusions?  But it can also easily be argued that because everything is contingent, that the structures are constantly evolving and historical investigations will not find the structures we seek, but their historical counterparts which, while similar, are intrinsically different.  

My own cautionary reticence notwithstanding, Sewell’s volume had many engaging discussions—some of which I am sure I failed to comprehend as they were intended.   In particular I enjoyed the discussion of temporality and causality and the shift within the historical profession from the “new social history” to a history driven by culture were quite interesting and well written.
