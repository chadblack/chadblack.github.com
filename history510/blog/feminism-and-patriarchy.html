Feminism and Patriarchy

Posted: Sat, 06 Nov 2010
Judith Bennett makes no bones about where she stands; as a lesbian or as a feminist.  Her book, History Matters: Patriarchy and the Challenge of Feminism, seems like a venting of frustration at first.  She starts strong, stating that feminism is not the ‘she-woman man-haters club’ that many think of it as.  Feminism can be very useful to men as well, mainly in the area of the history of masculinity.  Her main point is that by looking at the past from a feminist perspective, a whole new approach opens up.  New angles on old questions, new insights into why and how something could have happened.  She takes issue with the so-called feminist magazines that used to run sections devoted to the exploration of strong female characters of the past.  Now they rarely devote much space to history articles, and then only those that focus on more modern history.  She finds three troubling problems: loss of a golden age for female history, the present mindedness of most feminist scholars today, and the current focus on women of the West.  Patriarchy has three meanings to her: ecclesiastical power of men, legal power of husband/father over the family, and the main one for her; the one that draws on the “feminist critiques of male power.” (55)  When she said that “by analyzing the nature and causes of women’s oppression in the past, we can directly contribute to feminist strategies for the present,” (81) it sounds a lot like using the past to define the present.  Wasn’t that a no-no for historians? 
Her comparison of wage differences is compelling, but I never like wage comparisons between past and present for a couple reasons.  First, what is the information pool of the past? Second, is this comparable to today’s pool of workers?  I know that in every wage job I’ve had in my life the females in that job have made the same as I did.  This is what throws me off.  I’m sure to make the arguments of discrimination you have to look at non-hourly wage workers since people in the past were not under law to pay equally and everything was negotiated.  But wouldn’t the very fact of there being a law of equal pay signify a change in society?  This was never addressed in her work, at least not that I found.
Her idea of “lesbian-like” (109) does add a new dimension to the study of women in the past.  It would make for interesting discussions about the motivations of some, and the behaviors and aims of others.  However, she concedes that the term will not answer any real questions, but it does open many possibilities.  In the end, she did achieve her goal.  I was shocked by her forthrightness and it made me think in new ways about things I had never really given much thought.  Patriarchy is not dead, not by a long shot.  That is her real message; feminist historians need to wake up and smell the coffee, because there is much work left to be done.
