9/12 Canizares-Esguerra

Posted: Sun, 12 Sep 2010

Canizares-Esguerra, Jorge. How to Write the History of the New World.

In his heavily researched book, Canizares-Esguerra (CE) explores the ways in which Spanish and New World historiography of the New World changed in response to politics, the development of history, and the growing idea of nationalism. Throughout the course of the book, he discusses the debate concerning alphabetical vs. non-alphabetical texts and their mertis, the effects of patriotism on Spanish historiography in response to criticism from the rest of Europe about Spain and Spanish historians, effective elitism as historians chose to downplay common accounts of the conquest and the New World in favor of Amerindian aristocrats, and the distrust of foreign and “armchair philosopher” accounts of the New World.

Although my head was certainly spinning from the names of so many different 17th- and 18th-century historians, and I sometimes got irritated at CE for going on rather long tangents to explain one point, the book overall gave me some new perspectives on how we write history: the privileging of certain types of sources, the effects of politics on history, and the ways in which we decide the validity of accounts. Certainly, the practice of researching and writing history has changed since the time period covered in CE’s, but these considerations are still valid.

In particular, I found the preference for aristocratic Amerindian sources interesting. As a medievalist, the sources I have encountered, and am likely to see in the future, are the products of the elite; they’re all we have, no matter how much we might like to know about every non-aristocrat in Europe. Thus, although I understand the rationale of the time (members of the elite writing histories are perhaps rather likely to prefer accounts from other members of the ruling classes), I find it interesting, and disheartening from an informational standpoint, that the views, histories, and myths of so many common Amerindians were ignored because they were viewed as being untrustworthy—especially in a time/place when so many other sources, such as the non-alphabetical ones, were also viewed as inaccurate.

My biggest complaint with the book is that CE seems to have forgotten about Peru and the Inca civilization partway through. I’m not very familiar with the history of the Spanish colonies, or certainly of the historiography associated with them, so it’s possible that the histories of the Inca entered a standstill/lack-of-debate period. If so, however, I rather wish that CE had directly addressed that, rather than choosing to spend the remainder of the book exclusively in Central America.
