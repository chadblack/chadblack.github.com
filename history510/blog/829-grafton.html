8/29 Grafton

Posted: Sun, 29 Aug 2010

Grafton, Anthony. The Footnote. Cambridge: Harvard University Press, 1997.

While reading Grafton’s book, I was intrigued by the long-lasting debate in the scholarly community of the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries about whether to even use footnotes at all. Did a footnote disrupt the flow of a learned, stylistically pleasing narrative or add to the scholar’s credibility and allow readers to check the work and subsequently perform their own scholarship? A seemingly endless drama appears to have raged, with adherents from the Pantheon of Great People firmly entrenched in their positions on either side.

And it never seemed to have ended. Even Ranke struggled with the question of whether or not to include citations, and from the way Grafton presents the information, printing your sources and criticism was perhaps more likely to get you heavily critiqued than not doing so, at least for a time. Of course, we have the modern footnote today, with some rather more carefully delineated usages for it, but the question remains: do you clutter the bottom of every page with notes, some of which must by spatial necessity flow onto the next (or the next) page, or do you shift your second narrative, as Grafton views it, to the back of the book and simply denote its presence with unobtrusive numbers? Certainly, I had noticed the difference between footnotes and endnotes before, but Grafton’s book prompted me to really consider their uses for varying audiences, the members of the historical profession or a lay audience, which would be perhaps more interested in the narrative and the conclusions rather than being able to trace the author’s time spent in archives.

Though I enjoyed Grafton’s book and his comments about footnotes and the way in which he uses the idea of source documentation as a sort of lens through which to view the construction of the modern historical field, I must admit that I was perplexed by the book’s organization. The word “vignettes” kept running through my mind while reading it, and I think I would have gotten more out of it had Grafton chosen to organize his study in a more chronological fashion, rather than choosing to leap around in time and pursue a sort of treasure hunt, on the path of the elusive footnote. I’m certainly a fan of in media res openings, so I don’t fault him for beginning with a short bit about Ranke, but overall, I felt as though I was not only reading an in media res opening, but also an imr middle and an imr conclusion.
