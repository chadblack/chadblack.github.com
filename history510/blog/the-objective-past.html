The Objective Past

Posted: Sun, 17 Oct 2010

Objectivity is an elusive, elusive thing.

The book we read this week explained more than just what objectivity is and why it has been such a major part of American historiographic tradition, but took us through the development of historiography in the United States. I found this a very informative and helpful work, and I’m noticing that as the semester moves on I am having an easier time defining myself as a historian and what my philosophical views on history are.

I have to say that in my beliefs of the probably, I agree with the relativists that true, objective fact is not going to be attained, and that assuming a historian can do so is both naïve and dangerous. That being said, this book, and looking through the debates, makes me want to champion the objectivist cause, because I believe the pursuit of objectivity, in its original, real sense, is the most important thing a historian can do. This book gives example after example of how historians have constantly been driven off the straight and narrow path, deviating from the objective lessons of history both due to their own personal/societal faults (like the big section on racism) or because of political expediency (both WW1 and WW2 showed a lot of that). It certainly is a false hope to believe that people will ever be able to either get to the entire truth of an issue or to put away all of their own biases, but I think it’s a horrific idea to abandon the quest simply because of its unreachability.

Perhaps I listened to the Don Quixote soundtrack too much when I was a kid.

In any case, I think “that noble dream” is one that is vital for the profession. I know that there is a strong motivation in a period of economic uncertainty to make the humanities somehow more “relevant,” as if learning about the world is not relevant enough, or to market them as practical skills (they’re not, really), but to give up the idea of pure truth, the quest to discover the Platonic ideal of the past… well, that’s the death of history as a field worth studying.

We will, of course, never really know the whole truth about the past. Our use of documentary evidence immediately prevents that goal, because we have to rely on biased sources. But the quest, the journey towards the truth—that is what we do, and even if we fail, and perhaps because we fail, that is how we become epic.
