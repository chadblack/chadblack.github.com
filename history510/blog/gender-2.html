Gender

Posted: Sun, 07 Nov 2010

Joan Scott notes the creation of the term “gender” to apply to the socially constructed expectations that surround the biological division of male and female. Instead of using the term “sex,” which conjures up both sexual activity and a certain biological determinism, “gender” denotes a distinction from a study of physical difference to a study of male-female dynamics. Women’s or gender history is an expanding field, though as Judith Bennett points out, it is not without its detractors. There is a larger disciplinary trend within History to expand the topics of historical study. This includes increases awareness of class, race, religion, and now gender, as well(Scott, 1054; Bennett, 142). Like these other fields, there is tension within gender history over inclusiveness, as some studies stop short of full integration into the analysis of the period and instead just add a few interesting case studies of particular individuals. In addition to call historians to do more than create biographies for a few “women worthies,” Scott also looks at the strengths and weaknesses of current theories to explain patriarchy, or male dominance.

While her points are well-taken that women taking the primary responsibility for childcare is not biologically determined (in fact, there are some cultures where the man had primary childcare responsibility), I do think she underplays the period of nursing after the child is born, which certainly marks the woman as necessary to the child’s care. Her attention to the pattern that seems to emerge of female suppression when new regime takes over, despite a lack of economic incentive to disadvantage that group, is strong evidence for the issue of patriarchy. It seems one of the popular and effective strategies for cementing a new rule is to first “put women in their place,” which is, to me, powerful evidence to the political implications of patriarchy and a lens through which the traditionally male-dominated field of political history, could be opened to included gender(Scott, 1072).

Finally, one part of Bennett’s book that was particularly intriguing to me was her analysis of the Virgin Mary. As both the ideal woman and the intercessor, female virtue is embodied in this central religious figure; that much has always been clear. However, the more nuanced interpretations of Mary as both mother and bride, virgin and mother, new mother and bereaved parent, create an impossible standard that women cannot achieve as well as connecting female worth and authority entirely to their connection to men(Bennet, 146). Both of these have real implications for women’s place within society, then and now.
